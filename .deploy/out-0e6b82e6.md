# Building a Team You Would Follow

There is a particular silence that falls in an interview room when you have just asked a young man, sitting across a desk in a borrowed shirt, to tell you why he wants the job. He does not, in most cases, have an answer prepared that is worth very much. He has the answer his cousin told him to give on the taxi ride in, and he has the answer he read on a printed sheet at the labour office, and somewhere underneath both of those he has the real answer, which is that he needs the work, and that he is hoping you will not see how badly. The silence I am talking about is the one that comes after the rehearsed answers have been used up and before the real one arrives, if it is going to arrive at all. You learn, over the years, to wait through that silence without filling it. The silence is the interview. Everything before it is just paperwork with a chair under it.

I want to begin there, in that silence, because almost everything I have come to believe about building a team starts in those few seconds. Not in the policy manual. Not in the recruitment brochure with its careful photographs of people in clean overalls pointing at clipboards. In the small, awkward pause when a person you have never met before is deciding whether to tell you the truth.

This chapter is about what I learned to listen for in that pause, and what I learned to do, in the months and years afterwards, with the people I chose to trust. It is about hiring, and training, and developing, and — because the honest version of the story would not be honest without it — about the cost of getting it wrong. The cost to the business, which is the part everyone counts. And the other cost, the one nobody puts on a spreadsheet, which is the part I have come, in the end, to count more carefully.

## Where I was hiring from, and who I was hiring

I should be plain, before I go any further, about the kind of people I was hiring. Almost all of them, for most of my working life, came from the same world I had come from. They were apprentices and young tradesmen and, later, young supervisors who had grown up in houses where the wage was tight and the future was not, in any meaningful sense, planned. They came in through the gate in clothes their mothers had ironed too carefully, and they sat down opposite me with the same mixture of pride and fear I had carried into my own first interview, a long time before, when the foreman across the desk had glanced at my hands and not at my face.

I am not pretending this gave me some mystical advantage. It did not. Plenty of men from my background turn out to be poor judges of other men from their background, because they spend the interview either over-identifying or over-compensating, and both of those are mistakes. What it did give me was a working vocabulary. I knew, more or less, what a tight house sounded like in a young man's voice. I knew the difference between somebody who had been told to be respectful and somebody who actually was. I knew when a story about a previous job had been polished into a shape that no real job has, and I knew when the rough edges of a story were the rough edges of the truth.

That working vocabulary was the first tool. It was not, by itself, enough. But I would not start this chapter without naming it, because it shaped everything else.

The second thing I want to name is who I was *not* hiring, or at least not very often. I was not, in those years, choosing between graduates with neat CVs and references from people who used the same vowels I did. I was choosing, mostly, between young men whose CVs fit on one page and whose references, if they had any, were a phone number for an uncle on a building site. The decision in front of me was almost never *who has the best record*. It was *which of these unfinished people is going to finish well, and which one am I going to be sorry about in two years' time*.

That is a different question, and it asks for a different kind of attention.

## What I learned to listen for

I will not pretend I had a formula. Anybody who tells you they have a formula for picking people is either selling a course or has not picked enough of them yet. What I had, after a while, was a short list of things I had learned to listen for. They are not clever. They will not surprise anybody who has done a lot of hiring. But they were the things that, in my experience, did the most work.

The first was **the way a person talked about the people they had worked with before**. Not the bosses. The bosses get talked about in predictable shapes. I listened for how a young man talked about the other apprentices, the older tradesmen, the cleaner, the woman who ran the canteen. If he spoke about them as if they were furniture, that told me something. If he remembered their names without being prompted, that told me something else. People who notice the people around them in the small jobs go on noticing them in the big ones. People who do not, do not learn it from a workshop, no matter how many of them you send them on.

The second was **how he described a mistake**. I always asked, in some form, about a job that had gone wrong. The answers fell into three broad categories. There were the ones where nothing had ever gone wrong, which were obviously untrue and told me the man had not yet learned that honesty was safer than performance. There were the ones where everything that had ever gone wrong had been somebody else's fault, which told me he would, in due course, be telling that same story about me. And there were the ones in which the man, sometimes haltingly, sometimes with a small flush of embarrassment, told the story of an error that was clearly his own, and then, without being prompted, told me what he had done about it. That third group was the one I almost always hired from. The story did not even need to be flattering. It just needed to be his.

The third thing was harder to name. It was a kind of **stillness**. Not shyness. Not nervousness. A capacity to sit in a slightly uncomfortable conversation without scrambling to make it easier for me. The men I came to trust most, in the long run, were the ones who could sit through a difficult silence and let it be difficult. They turned out to be the same men who, ten years later, could sit through a difficult meeting with a client, or a difficult conversation with a junior who had let them down, without panicking and saying something they would regret.

I would not have called any of this an interview technique at the time. I would have said, if anybody had asked, that I was just *getting a feel*. But I think, now, that the feel was made of those three things, and a few others I never managed to name properly, and that the men I chose well I chose largely on those grounds, and the ones I chose badly I chose because I had, in one way or another, stopped listening for them.

## The duty I felt I had inherited

I have spoken, in earlier chapters, about the men who took a chance on me. The foreman who promoted me before I thought I was ready. The charge-hand who let me ruin a piece of plate and did not write it up. The older tradesman who, without ever making a speech about it, made sure I was on the jobs that would stretch me and off the jobs that would only have bruised me. I owed those men something. Not money. Not even, in any simple sense, gratitude, because most of them would have brushed gratitude off if I had tried to offer it. I owed them a continuation.

That is the only word I have for it. A continuation. The chance I had been given was not the kind of thing you pay back to the person who gave it to you. It was the kind of thing you pass on to somebody else, in roughly the same shape you received it, and you do that not because anybody is watching but because the chain only stays a chain if each link, in its turn, holds on to the next one.

I came to think of hiring, in those years, less as a transaction between me and the candidate and more as a small act of trust paid forward on behalf of men who were, by then, mostly retired or dead. When I sat across from a nervous twenty-year-old in a borrowed shirt, I was not only deciding whether he would suit the shop. I was deciding whether the chain was going to carry on through him or stop at me.

I would not have said any of this out loud at the time. The trade I came up in did not, on the whole, talk in those terms. But I think it was sitting under most of the decisions I made, and I think the days I made my worst hiring decisions were the days I had, for one reason or another, forgotten that the chain was even in the room.

## Training, in a place that did not believe in training

When I started taking on responsibility for bringing people through, the industry I was in did not have what anybody now would recognise as a training department. There were courses you could send people on, and there was the apprentice board, and there were the trade tests, and there were a few hours of safety induction that almost everybody slept through. Beyond that, training was something that happened, or did not happen, in the slow, daily friction between a young man and an older one, on the floor, in front of the work.

I want to defend that older way for a moment, even though I have, in my own businesses later on, built something more structured. The older way had a virtue that the structured way often loses, which is that the **training was inseparable from the work**. A young man did not go away for two days to learn how to lay out a job. He laid out a job, badly, next to a man who had laid out a thousand of them, and he listened to the older man swear under his breath and rub a chalk line out with the side of his boot and start again. He learned the layout, but he also learned the standard, and he also learned the manner in which a man with the standard tolerates a man without it. None of that fits on a slide.

But the older way also had a defect, which I want to be equally honest about. It assumed that the older man on the floor knew how to teach. Most of them did not. Some of them taught beautifully without ever knowing they were doing it. Others, equally skilled at the work, were appalling teachers — impatient, sarcastic, prone to humiliating a young man in front of his peers for an error any apprentice would make. And because the system did not name teaching as a separate skill, the apprentices who happened to be paired with the bad teachers either left the trade altogether or hardened into versions of themselves they should not have had to become.

When I started running sections, and later departments, I tried to do something about that without overcorrecting. I did not invent a training department. I did not buy a software package with a name in three languages. What I did, mostly, was think very carefully about who I put a new man next to. I treated **pairing** as the actual training intervention, and I treated it with more seriousness than almost anything else I did with my week. A young man on his first three months would be put next to a tradesman I had chosen, deliberately, for his patience and his clarity. The job he was on would be chosen because it taught the right things in the right order. The supervisor over them would know — not from a memo, from a quiet word — that the new man was on a development plan even if nobody used those words to him.

I did not always get it right. I sometimes put a young man next to a tradesman I trusted to do the work and discovered, a month in, that the tradesman could not, for the life of him, explain why he did it the way he did. I sometimes put a man on a job that was too easy because I was being kind, and watched him learn the wrong lesson, which was that the work was easy. I sometimes did the opposite. But the principle stayed, and the principle, I still think, was the right one. **Most training that works happens in the company of another human being on a real piece of work.** Everything else is support material.

## The men I gave the chance to

I will not put names in this book. I have not put any names in any of these chapters, because the men I am thinking of did not ask to be characters in somebody else's account, and I do not want, by accident, to praise one and slight another. But I want to describe a few shapes, because the shapes carry the lesson better than abstractions do.

There was the young man who came in from a small town and could barely sit still in the interview, his knee bouncing under the desk the whole time, and who I almost did not hire because I mistook the bouncing knee for unreliability. I was wrong. He turned out to be one of the most diligent men I ever employed. The bouncing knee was just a bouncing knee. He stayed with us for a long time, became a leading hand, then a supervisor, then ran a section of his own. Years later, when his own first child was small, I stood in his garage drinking a cold beer while he showed me a workbench he had built for himself out of off-cuts. He said, without any ceremony, *I would not have any of this if you had not given me the start*. I did not, on the night, know what to say to that. I still do not. But I remember it, and I have used it, since, as a private corrective for the days when I have been tempted to take credit for things that were not, really, mine to take credit for.

There was the young man I hired largely because his uncle, who worked for a friend of mine, vouched for him in the kind of unprompted way that, in our industry, was worth more than any reference letter. He turned out to be capable in the work but, in some way I could not at first see, half-absent. He arrived on time. He did the job. He passed the trade test. But he never, in all the years I worked with him, fully chose the trade. He was waiting, the whole time, to do something else, and the something else never quite arrived. I did not see, until late, that I had been training a man for a life he did not want. He left, eventually, on good terms, and went and ran a small spaza shop a long way away, and I am told he was happier there than he had ever been on a shop floor. I do not regret hiring him. I regret not seeing, earlier, what he was actually trying to tell me with his careful, polite, unenthusiastic competence.

There was the woman who came in for a clerical role and ended up, by a series of accidents I did not engineer, running our entire planning office. I want to mention her separately, because the world I came up in did not, for a long time, take women in industrial businesses very seriously, and I was, in the early years, no better at that than most of my peers. She corrected my education in the most useful possible way, which was by being plainly and visibly better at her part of the work than the men around her, until pretending otherwise became absurd. The lesson there was not, in the end, about gender, although gender was the surface on which the lesson arrived. It was about how easy it is to fail to see talent because you have, without noticing, narrowed your idea of where talent might come from. I have tried, since, to keep that idea wider than it instinctively wants to be. I have not always succeeded. But I have tried.

And there was the young man whose story I would like, in some other version of this book, to be able to tell as a triumph. He came in young. He was bright. He was likeable. He had, very visibly, the kind of quick hands and quick mind that you do not get to choose for very often in a single hiring season. I gave him every chance. I put him next to the best men I had. I overlooked the first warning signs because I wanted, very badly, for him to be what I thought he could be. He was not. He left under a cloud, after costing the business a sum of money I will not name and costing me, separately, a friendship with the person who had originally introduced him to me. I will come back to that story in a moment, because the lesson it taught me is the one I most want to put in this chapter.

## The cost of choosing the wrong people

I want to be careful, here, not to slide into the kind of cheerful business-book tone that turns a real cost into a tidy anecdote. The wrong hire is a serious matter. I have seen, over a long working life, what it does to a small business, and I have seen what it does to the rest of a team, and I have seen what it does to the person himself, and I do not want any of those to be flattened into a bullet point.

Let me try to describe the three costs separately, because they behave differently.

The first cost is **financial**. This is the one that is easiest to talk about. A wrong hire costs you the wages you paid him while you were working out he was wrong. It costs you the wages of the people who had to clean up after him. It costs you, occasionally, the price of a job that went out badly because he was on it. In the worst cases — and I have lived through one or two of these — it costs you the trust of a client, which is a number that does not appear on any invoice but which is, by some distance, the most expensive thing on the list. I have seen estimates, in management literature, that put the cost of a bad hire at some multiple of his annual salary [unverified]. I have no quarrel with those estimates, but I think they understate the thing they are trying to measure, because they only measure what they can count.

The second cost is **organisational**. The team around a wrong hire pays in a currency the spreadsheet does not see. They pay in extra checking. They pay in covered work. They pay in the small, daily erosion of standards that happens when a man on the floor sees that another man on the floor is not being held to the same line he is. They pay, most expensively of all, in their trust in your judgement. Every time you keep a man who should not be kept, the people who are watching you — and they are always watching you, more than you realise — adjust their estimate of you very slightly downward. They do not say anything. They just adjust. And the next time you ask them for an extra mile, the mile is, by a small but real fraction, shorter than it would have been before.

I came to take this second cost more seriously, over the years, than the first one. The first one you can absorb. You can write it off, you can re-do the job, you can pay the supplier. The second one compounds, quietly, in directions you cannot easily reverse. A team that has stopped quite believing in your judgement is a team you are leading on borrowed credit. The credit does not always announce when it has run out.

The third cost, and the one I find hardest to write about, is **the cost to the person himself**. A man kept too long in a job he is not suited to does not, on the whole, thrive. He becomes, by stages, smaller than he was when he arrived. He learns habits he would not have learned in a job that fitted him. He picks up resentments. He starts to define himself by what he is putting up with rather than by what he is doing. And when, eventually, he leaves or is asked to leave, he leaves carrying a version of himself that the wrong job, and my wrong choice, helped to make. I have, more than once, looked at a man I let go too late and known, in some quiet part of myself, that the kindest thing I could have done for him was the thing I had been too kind to do, which was to part with him a long time before. That is a kindness I have had to learn, and I do not think I have ever learned it without flinching.

## The conversation I should have had earlier

I want to spend a moment on the conversation, because everything I have just said is theory until somebody actually has to sit down with another human being and tell him that the road forks here and his road does not, any more, run alongside yours.

I used to put those conversations off. I used to dress them up in the language of *another chance* and *one more month* and *let us see how the next project goes*, and I used to tell myself, on the drive home, that I was being patient. I was not being patient. I was being squeamish. There is a difference. Patience is when you are giving a man room because you genuinely believe the room will help him grow. Squeamishness is when you are giving him room because you do not want to be the person sitting across the desk when the room runs out. The two look almost identical from the outside. They are not the same thing at all, and the people on the floor can tell, every time, which one you are doing.

The conversation I should have had earlier, in almost every case where I had it too late, looked roughly the same. It was a short conversation. It was an honest one. It said, in plain terms, that the fit was not right. It did not pretend that the fault was all on one side. It did not, also, pretend that there was no fault. It offered, where it could, a way forward that allowed the man to leave with his dignity, and it offered, where that was not possible, an ending that was at least clean. None of those conversations were pleasant. Several of them I still think about in the small hours. But the ones I had on time, I never came to regret. The ones I had late, I have regretted in proportion to how late they were.

If I could go back and edit one habit of my younger management self, it would be that one. I would have those conversations earlier. Not coldly. Not without giving the man what he was actually owed. But earlier. The cost of an early honest conversation is almost always less than the cost of a late one, both for the business and for the man, and the only person it is more expensive for, in the short term, is me, and I have learned, slowly, that my short-term comfort is not a serious item on the ledger.

## Building, not assembling

I have used the word *team* throughout this chapter without quite defining it, and I want, before I close, to draw a line under what I think the word actually means in the working world I came up in. Because a team, in the sense I mean, is not the same thing as a group of people who happen to be on the same payroll.

A team, in the sense I mean, is a group of people who have, over time, learned how to cover for each other without keeping score. They know each other's strengths well enough to use them and each other's weaknesses well enough to protect them. They share a standard that none of them, individually, would maintain on his worst day, because the standard is held by the group and the group is held by the standard. They argue, sometimes hard, in the morning, and they sit at the same lunch table at noon. They notice when one of them is having a bad week. They notice, also, when one of them is starting to drift. And they tell the truth to each other in small, regular doses, so that the truth never has to arrive in a single, expensive instalment.

You do not assemble that. You build it. The difference matters. **Assembly** is what you do when the parts are finished. **Building** is what you do when the parts are still becoming what they are going to be. The people on a real team are still becoming, all of them, all of the time, and the job of the man building the team is not to find finished people and click them together. It is to find people in whom the right things are still possible, to put them next to each other in the right order, and to keep tending the small fires of trust between them until they begin to tend each other.

I have, by the end of my working life, been part of two or three teams that became that. I would not pretend any of them were perfect. They were not. But on their best days, they did the kind of work that no individual on them, alone, could have done, and they took care of each other in ways that did not require anybody to write a policy. Those teams are, in the end, the thing I am most proud of having had a hand in. More than any contract I won. More than any title I was given. More than any number on any year-end report.

## A short list, for the man who is starting to hire

If I had to compress this chapter, for the version of me who was about to sit across from his first nervous twenty-year-old in a borrowed shirt, it would come down to these:

- The interview begins when the rehearsed answers run out. Wait for the silence. Do not fill it.
- Listen to how he speaks about the people he has worked with, especially the ones with no power over him. That tells you almost everything.
- Ask about a mistake. The man who can tell you, plainly, what he did wrong and what he did about it is almost always worth your time.
- Remember that you are not only choosing for the shop. You are continuing a chain that older men handed to you, often without saying so. Try to choose as if they were still watching.
- Treat **pairing** as the real training intervention. The slide deck is not the training. The man next to him is the training.
- Keep your idea of where talent might come from wider than it instinctively wants to be. The best hire of your life will, statistically, surprise you.
- When the fit is wrong, have the conversation early. Patience is a virtue. Squeamishness is a tax. Know which one you are paying.
- Build the team. Do not try to assemble it. The people you are working with are still becoming. So, for that matter, are you.
- And when somebody, years from now, stands in his own garage and tells you he would not have any of this without you, do not, on the night, take the credit. Take, instead, the quiet instruction that the chain has held, for one more link, on your watch.

The chain held, in my working life, more often than it broke. Not because I was a clever judge of men. I was not, particularly. It held because I tried, on most days, to listen for the right things, and because I had the good fortune to be surrounded by older hands who corrected me when I stopped listening, and because, in the end, the people I hired turned out, more often than not, to be better than I had any right to expect. That is the truth about building a team. The team, when it is real, makes you look like a better builder than you were. The least you can do, in return, is notice, and say so, and keep handing on what was handed to you.
