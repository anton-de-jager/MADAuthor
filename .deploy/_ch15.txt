# Sport, Discipline and the Hidden Curriculum of the Boardroom

There is a part of my story that some readers might be tempted to skip — the chapter where the engineer puts down the spanner and picks up the racquet. Please do not skip it. Of all the threads that run through my life, none has taught me more about leadership, more about discipline, and more about how a person from a mine house on the East Rand can stand his ground in any room in the world than the years I spent training, competing and losing on the sports field. Sport was not a hobby alongside my career. Sport was the laboratory in which I learned the habits that would later let me transform companies.

I am seventy-three as I write this. I still go to the gym on a regular basis, I still watch every Springbok Test that is on television, and I still feel the same quiet pleasure when I see a perfectly struck cover drive, a flat second serve that catches the line, or a putt that drops from twenty feet. None of what I am about to tell you in this chapter would have been possible without good health, and good health, in turn, has been the by-product of a half-century of training and a steady diet. The body and the mind are not two separate accounts. They are one balance sheet, and you cannot run a deficit on the physical side and expect your career to keep paying dividends.

## The school years — rugby, athletics and a silver medal at gymnastics

Like most South African boys of my generation, I started with rugby. I played at school and I enjoyed it for what it was — a game that taught you to tackle a boy bigger than yourself, to get up off the ground when the next ruck was already forming, and to put the team's result above your own bruises. I was never going to play Currie Cup rugby, and I knew it early, but the lessons of the scrum and the tackle are with me still. There is a particular kind of South African discipline that comes from a winter afternoon on a hard mid-Reef field, with your jersey stuck to your back with sweat and dust, and a coach telling you that the next minute is the only one that matters. I think most of the men of my generation who went on to run businesses learned something about persistence from rugby long before they ever read a management book.

Athletics came naturally after that. I was not the fastest boy at the school, but I was competitive, and competitive at school athletics teaches you a useful lesson — that the boy who looks faster in the warm-up is not always the boy who breaks the tape. I learned to keep something back, to read the race, and to push when others were tightening. It is the same lesson I would use, three decades later, when I had to sequence a R200-million plant turnaround at Tiger Brands. The first lap is not the lap that matters. The last one is.

Gymnastics was the surprise. At a training camp during high school I came away with a silver medal — a small piece of metal that no one outside our family has ever seen, but a piece of metal that taught me what coordinated, repeated, full-body discipline feels like. From gymnastics I drifted naturally into trampolining, and there I had real ability. A trampolinist learns three things very quickly. The first is that you cannot fake a routine — every flaw is visible in the air. The second is that confidence and fear sit on either side of the same coin, and you decide every morning which side faces up. The third is that the people who make it look effortless are the people who have done it ten thousand times in private. Twenty-five years later, when a German foreman in Dusseldorf told me my plant-repair schedule was impossible, the trampolinist in me was the one who answered: I have been doing the equivalent of this routine for two decades.

## The pivot to tennis — and the four Vaal Triangle titles

The sport that truly took me, though, was tennis. It came into my life sideways, the way the best things do. My sister had married a man who loved the game, and the two of them invited me into it almost casually. I did not know at the time that I had just walked through the doorway of the sport that would shape the rest of my life.

Tennis is a deceptively complete teacher. It looks like a simple, individual game — two players, a strip of fenced concrete or grass, a ball, a net — but it is one of the most demanding management exercises ever invented. You are the strategist, the tactician, the player, the coach and the psychologist, all at once, with no time-outs and no team-mates to pass to. Every point is a complete cycle of plan, execute, observe and adjust. Every match is a project. Every season is a business plan. I sometimes joke with younger managers that if you can win a third-set tie-break from 4-6 down on your opponent's serve, you can chair almost any executive meeting that the world is ever going to throw at you.

I won, by my honest count, about twenty titles over the course of my career. Some were club titles, some were regional, some were invitational tournaments arranged through the network of clubs across the Reef, the Vaal and the Eastern Cape. The four I remember best are the four Vaal Triangle championships I won in succession — a provincial-level tournament with a deep field and a long memory. The first one is the one you remember as the breakthrough; the fourth one is the one you remember as a habit. Anyone who has won the same competition four times will tell you the same thing — by the fourth one you are no longer playing the other players, you are playing your own standards. The opponent across the net is a circumstance. The opponent in your own head is the contest.

## A tennis court at home, and sixty pupils a week

When my career began to take me higher into AECI and then into Adcock Ingram, we built a tennis court at our home. That court was, in some ways, the most important capital expenditure of my life. It was where my children learned the game. It was where I coached, on most weeks, around sixty pupils. And it was where I held myself accountable when the working week had ended and I still had a forehand to sharpen.

At one stage I had under my coaching the champions in every junior age band the club ran — the under-12, under-14, under-16 and under-18 boys' champions, all coming through my court. Coaching is the part of sport that prepares you most directly for management. When a fifteen-year-old hits a forehand into the net for the fourth time in a row, you do not shout at him. You do not threaten to drop him from the squad. You walk over, you change one variable — the grip, the foot position, the toss height, the contact point — and you ask him to try again. You congratulate him on the part that was right, and you correct, gently, the part that was wrong. It is exactly what good leaders do with engineers and operators on a factory floor, and a coach who has done it for ten years has the muscle memory long before the leadership book has been written.

There is another part of coaching that I think very few managers fully grasp until they have done it themselves. You cannot, as a coach, win the match for your player. Your job is to give them a structure, a confidence, a set of options and a state of mind, and then to sit down and let them play. The temptation in business is to take the racquet out of the subordinate's hand and play the point yourself. It is always the wrong instinct. The court — like the factory — belongs to the people who are actually on it.

## Coaching the AECI Germans for two months in Dusseldorf

The second-most unlikely tennis assignment of my life — the most unlikely being the Alberton story I will come to in a moment — happened during my AECI assignment in Dusseldorf. I had been sent across to specify and supervise a reactor repair, and somewhere between the technical meetings and the night shifts I had agreed to mention, casually, that I played tennis. The German colleagues seized on it. Within a week I was on the AECI European staff's club courts every available evening, and within a fortnight I had been asked to coach the full company contingent for two months.

It was an extraordinary experience for a young South African engineer. The Germans took to coaching the way they take to most things — with seriousness, punctuality and an absolute respect for the instructor while the lesson was running. By the time I left, the standard of play across the group had risen visibly, and the relationships I had built around the net carried directly into the technical meetings and the nightshift schedules. The reactor repair came in thirty days early. I have never been able to disentangle the influence of the tennis court from the success of that contract. I do not think the two can be separated.

## Beating the Free State number one and Pieter Aldrich

If you play first-league tennis for long enough in South Africa, you will eventually meet players whose names you later read on international result sheets. I was lucky enough to do so twice in a way that has stayed with me. The first was in a provincial match against the Free State, when I drew their number one player in the singles. He was a player of real ability and I was, on paper, the underdog. The match went the distance, but I won it, and I won it because I had played him in my head a dozen times before I walked on court. I had planned the first three points of his service game and the first three points of mine. He was a better striker of the ball than I was. He was not, on that afternoon, a better planner.

The second match is the one I most often tell young players about, because the player on the other side of the net was Pieter Aldrich. He was at that stage the South African under-16 number one, and would later become one of the top ten men's doubles players in the world. I will not pretend that beating him at that stage of his career was beating the player he would become. But beating him on the day we played taught me that you do not concede a match because the name on the draw sheet has international ambitions. You concede a match by deciding, before you walk on, that the other person is going to win. That decision is yours alone, and if you refuse to make it, anything is possible.

## Late twenties, golf, and a hole-in-one at Vereeniging

In my late twenties I added golf to the rotation. I had no intention, when I started, of becoming a serious golfer. I was a tennis player who needed a Sunday morning game with friends. But the discipline that had taken me through tennis transferred. Within about two years I had played myself down to a three-handicap, which in club golf is a respectable place to live.

My best tournament round was a 71 gross at the Macau Vlei Golf Course in Vereeniging, off an eight-handicap. I won the competition that day. I remember the round in some detail. I remember the par fives I had decided, before the first tee, that I would play conservatively. I remember the par threes I had decided I would attack. I remember three putts that dropped on the front nine and gave me the confidence to play the back nine without thinking too hard. A 71 gross at Macau Vlei is not a Major championship, but it was my Major, and the discipline that produced it was the same discipline that, a few months later, would let me sit in a Tiger Brands boardroom and recommend a R10-million automation capex with full conviction.

I had a hole-in-one at the Vereeniging Country Club on a par three I had played dozens of times. Anyone who has ever had a hole-in-one will tell you the same thing: you do not remember the ball going in. You remember the silence as it tracked towards the flag, and the cheer half a second after it dropped. It is the closest thing in golf to the moment, in business, when a strategy you have been working on for six months finally lands and you see the operators on the factory floor celebrate without being asked.

The other course I will mention because it has a place in my memory is Gleneagles in Scotland. I was fortunate enough, during a business trip, to play eighteen holes on the King's Course at Gleneagles — the same complex where the 2014 Ryder Cup was contested. To stand on the first tee at Gleneagles as a boy from Sub Nigel mine housing is a kind of full-stop. I am not a sentimental man, but I allow myself a moment of quiet wonder about that day every now and again. The boy who walked behind his father to church on the East Rand had no idea that the same legs would one day walk a Ryder Cup course in Perthshire.

## The 1987 Alberton double — won from forty-fourth on the squash ladder

Now we come to the story that I tell young people more than any other, because it is the cleanest example I know of the principle that runs through every chapter of this book: if you put your mind to something — even the apparently impossible — it can be achieved.

By my late twenties I was, in addition to my tennis, playing squash. The two games are cousins, and a good tennis player who is willing to put in the conditioning work can develop into a good squash player surprisingly quickly. I enjoyed the speed of the game and the associated fitness. At that stage I was the Alberton tennis champion — I had been for a few years — and I was beginning to climb the squash ladder at the same club.

One night in the early part of 1987, after a game of squash, the senior players were standing in the bar discussing who was likely to be the squash champion later that year. I do not know exactly what came over me. Perhaps it was the residual confidence from another tennis title, perhaps it was the adrenaline of a game that had gone the distance, perhaps it was the look of mild dismissal on the face of one of the top-ranked squash players. Whatever it was, I threw my hat in the mix. I said, in front of every senior player at the club, that I would win both the Alberton Tennis and the Alberton Squash titles that year.

The room laughed. They were entitled to. At that stage I was number forty-four on the squash ladder at the club. Forty-four. There were forty-three players ahead of me in the queue for that crown, including several who had been club champions and several who would have been favoured by any reasonable bookmaker. The prediction was, on paper, absurd.

The prediction was also a contract I had signed with myself in front of witnesses, which is the most binding kind of contract there is. I went home that night and I made a plan.

I started doing what we used to call roadwork — jogging and sprinting almost every morning in the months between that conversation and the championships in September. I built my fitness with the squash season specifically in mind. Squash demands an aerobic and an anaerobic engine simultaneously, and I knew that if I could outlast my opponents, I could compensate for the technical gap that would still exist between me and the top of the ladder.

I trained until I could run five kilometres in nineteen minutes and thirty seconds. I am still proud of that time. I was in my late twenties, holding down a senior position at Adcock Ingram, raising young children with Antonette, and running a five-kilometre time that any club athlete would respect. The fitness was the foundation. The squash technique I sharpened in parallel, climbing the club ladder one match at a time as the year wore on. From forty-four, I worked my way into the top ten.

The Tennis championship at Alberton came first. I won it comfortably. I was so fit from the squash preparation that I was almost playing a different sport. My opponents were playing tennis. I was playing endurance with a racquet in my hand. The trophy went on the shelf, and the squash championship was a few weeks away.

The squash championship was, as everyone in the club had expected, harder. The technical gap between number forty-four and the top of the ladder does not entirely close in a single year of training. But by then I was inside the top ten, my fitness was as good as anyone's in the draw, and I had spent every spare evening on the squash court. I won my early-round matches. I won my quarter-final and my semi-final. And then I won the final, and I was crowned the new Alberton squash champion.

The same year. The same club. Both titles. From forty-fourth on the squash ladder.

I do not tell that story to brag. I am too old for bragging, and Antonette would not allow it in any case. I tell it because it is the cleanest evidence I have ever experienced in my own life of a principle that has run through every job I have ever held: you can achieve almost anything you put your mind to, provided you are willing to do the unglamorous, repeated, daily work that no one else is watching.

## A Saturday in 1987

Some weeks during that 1987 build-up, my Saturday looked like this. Squash from six to seven in the morning, before most of Alberton was awake. Eighteen holes of golf from eight until one, walking the course in a four-ball. First-league tennis from two in the afternoon, often running into early evening. Then home, a meal cooked by Antonette, and bed by ten o'clock so that I could be at the AECI or Adcock Ingram office on Monday morning at full capacity.

That Saturday schedule is not a recommendation for everyone. It worked because I had a wife who understood what I was building, children who were old enough by then to come with me to several of the events, a strong constitution, and an instinct for sequencing. But it makes the point that the chapters of a life are not lived in series. They are lived in parallel. The discipline that produced a 71 at Macau Vlei was the same discipline that produced the 50 per cent output lift on the AECI cyanide plant. The fitness that produced the squash title was the same fitness that allowed me, ten years later, to fly to Munich, to Boston, to Solofra and to Seoul in a single quarter without losing my footing on any of them.

## The hidden curriculum

When people ask me what business school I went to, I list, honestly, the qualifications I hold — the Mechanical Engineering Diploma, the diploma in Company Direction, the final-year subjects of the International MBA, the intensive MBA short course at the University of Cape Town. But the deeper education was the one nobody examined me on. It was the curriculum hidden inside three decades of sport.

The first lesson the curriculum teaches you is to **plan beyond the visible scoreboard.** A tennis player who only watches the score is a player who reacts. A tennis player who watches patterns — which serve has produced the most cheap points, which side of the court the opponent's backhand is leaking, which break point came after which rally length — is a player who controls. In business, the manager who only watches the monthly P&L is reacting. The manager who watches the leading indicators — the OEE numbers, the staff turnover, the waste percentages, the customer complaint trend — is controlling.

The second lesson is to **train for the opponent you have not yet met.** When I trained for the Alberton squash championship, I trained for an opponent better than any of the players I had seen on the ladder that year. The opponent who finally faced me in the final was, in fact, the easier opponent of the two I had prepared for. When I walked into Kolosus Holdings in Port Elizabeth, I had already, in my head, prepared for a competitive environment more brutal than the one I actually found. The Italian leather buyers who arrived in our offices were tough negotiators. They were not the toughest negotiators I had rehearsed against in my head.

The third lesson is to **lose well.** Anyone who has played sport seriously has lost more matches than they have won. The way you carry a loss is the single most important thing about you, because the next match always begins inside the loss of the previous one. When a R200-million Tiger Brands gum-and-jelly installation went wrong before I arrived, the staff were carrying the loss badly. Half my work was simply to teach them how to set the loss down — how to look at it, understand it, learn from it, and walk on to the next mogul line repair without dragging the failure behind them.

The fourth lesson is to **respect your opponent without fearing them.** When I drew the Free State number one, I respected him. I did not fear him. There is a profound difference. Respect makes you prepare. Fear makes you concede. When I walked into the AECI specification meeting in Dusseldorf with German contractors who had built more reactors than I had ever seen, I respected them. I did not fear them. Respect made me ask better questions. Fear would have made me accept their first answer.

The fifth lesson is to **show up early.** A tennis player who is on court a quarter of an hour before the warm-up has already won something the other player has not. A golfer who has played the front nine in his head before he walks to the first tee has already shaped the round. A businessman who has read the board pack on Friday afternoon, for the meeting on Monday morning, is operating in a different time zone from his colleagues. Sport teaches you that the work that wins the contest is the work nobody can see at the time of the contest.

## Sport and the people I love

I would be writing dishonestly if I left this chapter without acknowledging the cost. The Saturdays I have described — squash, golf, tennis — were Saturdays during which my wife was at home with our children, often without me, doing the quiet, undramatic work of building a Christ-centred family. Sport gave me discipline. Antonette gave the children stability. The two cannot be separated, and I owe her, in this chapter as in every other, the debt that no amount of trophies could repay.

I have also been blessed to see sport pass into the next generation. My daughters and sons won tennis tournaments of their own. My youngest son, Anton, was chosen to play squash for South Africa and became a prefect at his high school. To see the children of an apprentice Boilermaker line up to represent their country in the same sports that had carried their father is the kind of moment that sits, undeclared, on a man's mental list of unique life moments. It sits there alongside the day Mr Mandela accepted a cheque for his seventy-fifth birthday celebrations in Port Elizabeth, the day I attended the 1995 Rugby World Cup final at Ellis Park with my management team as managing director, and the day I stood in the centre of the field at Murrayfield in Scotland. The list is not a brag list. It is a list of moments that would have astonished the sixteen-year-old apprentice who was riding the cage down at Sub Nigel on his first morning underground.

## Still going at seventy-three

I am still in the gym, still watching every Test that matters, still recommending to every young manager I mentor that they pick a sport, any sport, and stay with it long enough to learn what it is trying to teach them. I do not believe you can run a complex factory if you cannot run yourself. I do not believe you can transform a business if you cannot transform your own habits. And I do not believe you can lead a team of fifteen hundred people, the way I did at Tiger Brands, if you have never been the captain of even a club tennis side and felt the weight of fourteen other players watching your second serve at five-all.

If you take one thing from this chapter, take this. The boardroom does not have a hidden curriculum. The hidden curriculum is the same one your school sports master tried to teach you when you were thirteen — show up, train when no one is watching, plan your point before you play it, respect your opponent, lose well, and never let the ladder you are currently on tell you what ladder you are allowed to climb to. Forty-fourth on the squash ladder one January. Champion the same September. That is not luck. That is the curriculum.

And that curriculum, more than any qualification on my wall, is what carried me from a miner's son on the East Rand to the managing director's office, and on, eventually, to the Business Transformation contracts that I am still being asked to lead today at seventy-three.

It is the same curriculum that is available, free of charge, to every reader of this book. All you have to do is sign the contract with yourself, in front of witnesses if possible, and then go and do the unglamorous, repeated, daily work that no one else can see.

The ladder will take care of itself.
