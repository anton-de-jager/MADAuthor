# Overseas Exposure: A Global Mindset Built One Trip at a Time

I have been asked, more than once in the last decade, what single ingredient I would put into the working education of a young South African engineer who wanted, in due course, to run a manufacturing operation of consequence. I have given different answers in different rooms. The one I have come back to most often, when the question is asked seriously enough to deserve a serious answer, is the one that surprises the listener least and changes the listener's mind most.

Send the young man overseas. Send the young woman overseas. Not to study, although study has its place. Not to holiday, although a man who has never seen another country at leisure has missed a kind of education he cannot easily repair. Send them to work. Send them to a working yard, a working line, a working laboratory, in a country that has had longer than ours to develop the standards we will, in our own time, have to meet. Let them stand on somebody else's factory floor for long enough to see what is normal there. Let them come home with the comparison in their hand.

I have, in the course of my career, been sent overseas on the working side of that prescription more times than I have any business counting. The first folder I carried out of Jan Smuts in the winter of 1980, with Antonette and three small children and a German specification I had only just learned to read, was the beginning of a habit of travel that has, in one form or another, never quite ended. The continent on the cabin map has shrunk steadily over four decades. The benchmark in my head has moved the other way.

What I want to do in this chapter is pull the international thread of the book into a single piece of cloth. I have written, in earlier chapters, about Dusseldorf and Munich, about Boston, about East Kilbride and the Jeyes line, about Paris and Solofra and the leather fairs. I will not rehearse those stories at length. What I want to set down here is the underlying lesson that those trips, and a great many others I have not yet named, taught me in common.

## The reset of a benchmark

The first thing an overseas assignment does to a competent engineer is reset the benchmark in his head. He arrives, in almost every case, with a private conviction that the operation he has just left in South Africa is being run as well as that kind of operation can be run. He stands, for the first morning, on a yard in Dusseldorf or a line in East Kilbride or a tannery near Solofra, and he watches the housekeeping. He watches the way a foreman runs a Monday morning. He watches the way the first hour of a shift is spent. He notices, somewhere in the middle of the second day, that what he had been calling a tidy plant in Sasolburg would be regarded, in this yard in this country, as a plant in need of a quiet word.

The benchmark resets without ceremony. It does not need to be argued. The eyes do the work. A man who has stood for a fortnight on a German shop floor cannot, when he comes home, pretend that he has not seen what he has seen. He cannot, ever again, accept his own operation as the standard against which his own operation is to be measured.

That is the deep gift of overseas exposure. It is not, in the end, a gift of technique, although technique travels in the same suitcase. It is a gift of standard. It is the quiet permanent upward pressure on what a leader is prepared to accept as good enough, applied for the rest of his working life, by a memory of how somebody else, somewhere else, has decided to do the same job.

## Germany: Dusseldorf, Munich and Krefeld

Germany taught me the discipline of the specification, and I have written elsewhere about the reactor work in Dusseldorf and the shuttle to the second yard in Munich. What I did not, in that earlier chapter, give its due was the third German leg of the same broad assignment, in Krefeld, where a specialist contractor had been engaged for the high-pressure ancillaries.

Krefeld was a smaller town than Dusseldorf, in the Rhineland, with the same patient industrial culture and a yard whose foreman wore a tie under his overalls. I spent fewer weeks there than in the other two cities. I spent enough to confirm a pattern I had begun to suspect on the train rides between them. The pattern was that the German engineering culture, taken as a whole, was less a single national style than a federation of regional traditions, each of which carried its own version of the same underlying rigour. The Dusseldorf yard prized the written specification. The Munich yard prized the toolbox. The Krefeld yard prized the test certificate. Each yard would have told you, politely, that the others were not quite as serious as it was. Each yard, looked at by a foreigner with a notebook, was unmistakably more rigorous than the South African yard I had flown out of.

I came home from Germany with the metric units in my fingers, with a private rule about Friday notes that I have never since abandoned, and with the first version of the question I have asked on every overseas assignment since. What does this yard know that my yard at home does not? The answer is rarely a piece of equipment. It is, almost always, a small habit of attention that has been there long enough to look ordinary to the people who live inside it.

## Boston: a faster American voice

Boston was the second overseas assignment of any scale, and I have written about it in the Adcock chapter. What I will add here is the comparison the Boston trip forced on me with the German months.

The American supplier did business in a faster, more verbal register than the German contractor had done. The specification meetings were shorter, louder and more inclined to settle, in the room and on a whiteboard, what the Germans had insisted on settling on the page. The technical content of the work was just as exacting. The accompanying culture was the opposite kind of disciplined.

I adjusted my method without abandoning it. I kept the analytical core. I let the surface manners follow the room. I learned, in those Boston weeks, that the imported habit of one country's engineering is a tool you carry in a suitcase, not a uniform you wear in every climate. The German specification discipline served me as well in a Boston conference room as in a Dusseldorf yard, provided I did not insist on the room behaving like the yard.

## Scotland: East Kilbride and Gleneagles

Scotland, for me, is two memories sitting beside each other.

The first is East Kilbride, where the Jeyes blue-line range was running in the parent factory of the British company, and where I spent more visits than I have any business remembering in the months leading up to the South African transfer. I walked the line with the engineer who had been running it. I asked the foremen, in the unhurried Scottish way one asks Scottish foremen, what the line knew that the drawings did not. The answers, written down across several visits, became the unwritten half of the transfer document. The line came down the Atlantic in shipping containers. The product, on the South African side, came off in the same specification, in the same packaging, with the same shelf life. The Scottish foremen had given me, without quite saying so, the operating intelligence that no drawing ever carries.

The second is Gleneagles. I have mentioned it in another chapter, and I will not labour the point. I will only say that to play eighteen holes on the King's Course at Gleneagles, as a boy from Sub Nigel mine housing, is a kind of full-stop that earns its own small pause in a chapter like this one. The same legs that walked behind my father to church on the East Rand walked a Ryder Cup course in Perthshire. The benchmark, when overseas exposure resets it, does not only reset the engineering. It resets, very quietly, the sense of what a working life can reach.

## Italy: Solofra, Capri, Rome and the Continental Oils commissioning

Italy entered my career first through the leather fairs and the Italian buyers of Dorper sheep-skin from Kolosus. The visits to Solofra, where so much of the Italian finishing industry sits, taught me to walk my own wet-finishing department with an Italian buyer's hand in my mind, and to write down what that hand would notice three weeks later when the consignment landed.

What I did not write into the Kolosus chapter, because it sat properly outside it, was a commissioning assignment I took on in the same Italian region a few years later, on behalf of a different employer, at a plant that traded under the Continental Oils name. The plant had been built against a specification that had not quite been finished, commissioned by a contractor who had moved on to the next job before the line was running cleanly, and handed over to an operating team that had been told the difficulties would settle on their own.

They were not settling. The plant was running at a loss against the financial model the parent had approved, and the loss was widening month on month. I was sent for six weeks. I stayed for nearer to twelve. I did what I have done on every operation of that kind since SA Oil Mills. I walked the line in the first fortnight without writing a single email. I sat with the maintenance fitters at lunch. I read the commissioning logs that had been quietly filed in cupboards. I asked the operators, in my limited Italian and their better English, what they would do if they were given the keys.

The plant came right inside one calendar quarter. The loss closed. The quarter that followed it returned a modest profit, and the quarter after that returned a respectable one. There was no new equipment of any significance. There were a series of small corrections to a starting position that had been internally inconsistent from the day the contractor had left.

I took, at the end of the work, a long weekend with Antonette on Capri, and a short couple of days in Rome on the way to the airport, and I count both of those interludes among the quietest gifts the working life has given us. The benchmark, in Italy, was not only the patient craftsmanship of the Italian operators. It was also the reminder, on a Capri terrace at the end of a hard piece of work, that a leader who never lifts his head to see the country he is working in has not, in any full sense, been there.

## France and Holland: the soft trades and the long port

France I have mentioned in the leather chapter. Twice a year, for the long years at Kolosus, I flew to Paris for the leather fairs. What I did not, in that chapter, give its due was the parallel French education in a softer trade. I spent more than one trip with French food-ingredient suppliers in the years that followed, and the discipline of a French ingredient house, where a single supplier might serve half the chocolatiers in Europe, is a quieter discipline than the one I had learned in Germany. It is built on relationship rather than specification. It is built on the long memory of who has done what for whom, over how many seasons, with what consistency. It is the first kind of overseas exposure I learned in which the technical content was not the leading instrument.

Holland came at me through the same broad confectionery and edible-oils sweep. I visited oil refineries in the Rotterdam port complex more than once, on the trail of equipment suppliers and seed-handling specialists, and the Dutch operation taught me the discipline of the long port. A Dutch refiner reasons backwards from the ship and forwards from the customer in the same sentence. The supply chain is the strategy. The plant is a node in a system that does not stop at the plant gate. I came home with a sketch of how a Southern African oil mill could be reasoned about in the same way, and I drew on that sketch more than once in the SA Oil Mills years.

## Korea: a pharmaceutical plant assessment

The Korean trip stands a little apart from the others, because it was, in the strict sense, an assessment rather than a commissioning. The brief was simple and the politics were not. A Southern African business with pharmaceutical ambitions had been invited to consider taking equity, or a technology licence, in a Korean plant that was producing a particular range of formulations at a scale that no South African plant had then matched. I was sent, with one colleague and a small folder of GMP questions, to walk the plant and to come back with an honest answer.

Seoul, in those days, was an education in industrial seriousness. The plant we walked was clean to a standard I had only previously seen in a small handful of European operations, and the operating discipline was tighter than anything I had seen in Asia. The Korean engineers received us with the formal courtesy that the country reserves for visiting professionals, and they answered our GMP questions with a directness that I would have welcomed, in my time, from any number of European yards.

The honest answer, in the end, was that the plant was capable, that the technology was real, and that the cultural distance between Seoul and the Southern African plant on the other end of the transfer was wider than the parties had assumed. I wrote that into the assessment. The deal was restructured. The relationship survived, in a smaller form, and the Korean engineers and I exchanged the kind of cards one keeps in a drawer for years afterwards in case the trail ever returns.

What Korea taught me was that a regulated industry, in a country one does not know, is read first through the floor and last through the boardroom. I have applied that order ever since.

## China, Japan, Malaysia, Singapore, Taiwan and Thailand

The Asian trail, taken together, made up a substantial portion of the second half of my export life. I will not pretend to have known each of those countries equally well.

China, in the years I was visiting it, was changing faster than any traveller could keep up with. I went into ingredient houses and packaging suppliers in three different industrial belts on three different trips, and each trip the operation I had visited the year before had grown a wing, replaced a line, or moved to a larger site. I learned, in China, to suspend the assumption that the plant I had walked last time was the plant I would walk this time. I learned to ask, on every trip, what had changed since the last trip, and to listen carefully to the parts of the answer the supplier was proud of and the parts he was politely declining to mention.

Japan I have invoked elsewhere through the principle of Poka-Yoke, which has run through the second half of my career like a thread. I will say only here that to walk a Japanese line in person, after years of carrying Poka-Yoke in the abstract, is to understand for the first time that the principle is not a slogan. It is a posture. The Japanese operators stand to their lines differently from the way operators stand anywhere else I have been. The mistake-proofing is not bolted on. It is built into the way the person and the machine meet.

Malaysia and Singapore I visited together more often than I visited either of them alone. Singapore taught me what a small country can do with a port and a long view. Malaysia taught me what an industrial culture can do when it is being asked, by its own ambition, to lift itself a notch every few years. The two of them, taken as a pair, were the cleanest demonstration I had seen of what regional policy can quietly accomplish.

Taiwan and Thailand were briefer engagements, but both of them taught me the same uncomfortable lesson. The Southern African industrialist who arrives in a Taiwanese or Thai operation with an air of superiority is, within an hour, the most embarrassed person in the room. The benchmark moves again. It moves further than the visitor expected. He goes home a little quieter than he flew out.

## Cyprus, Greece and the Mediterranean accident

Cyprus came at me as a beachhead, as I have written in the Adcock export chapter. A Cypriot importer had taken an interest in our cough syrups and our boiled sweets at a European trade fair. I flew out for what I had expected to be a two-day visit, and I stayed nearly a week, walking pharmacies and small grocery chains, watching how Mediterranean shoppers picked up an over-the-counter product, weighed it in the hand, read the back, and made their choice. From Cyprus the trail led naturally to Greece, where the same distributor had family connections, and where a small but steady volume of cough preparations, antacids and confectionery began to move every quarter.

The Cyprus trip is the one I cite, in younger ears, when I am asked how a country gets onto a company's map for the first time. The answer is rarely a strategy. It is, almost always, a person, at a stand, at a fair, who has been quietly persuaded that the product on the shelf in front of him is the product his own market has been waiting for. The strategy follows the person.

## Mauritius and Southern Africa

I will close the geographical roll-call with the destinations I have left for last because, in the strict sense, they are not overseas at all. Mauritius and the Southern African neighbours — Botswana, Namibia, Zimbabwe, Zambia, Mozambique and into East Africa — sit in a different category in my mind. They are the countries to which a South African industrialist learns, before he learns anything further afield, how to send his product reliably across a border.

Mauritius taught me the discipline of the small island market, where a single retailer's preference can move a national category, and where the relationships are personal in a way that does not survive the size of a larger economy. The Southern African neighbours taught me, in the years of building the Adcock export book, that the basic logistics of the region are within reach for any operation that is prepared to learn them properly. Each market has its own quirks. None of them are unsolvable.

The Adcock export book lifted, in one particular year that the planning team in Johannesburg flagged at the time, by a little over two hundred per cent year on year. I will not pretend that I built that lift alone. It was the cumulative effect of the work of a small export team I had been encouraging for several years, of a distributor network that had been patiently assembled across the region, and of a product range that had finally caught up with what the market had been quietly asking for. What I will say is that the two-hundred-per-cent year would not have happened if the people behind it had not, between them, walked enough overseas floors and overseas pharmacies and overseas trade fairs to know what the benchmark in the next market actually looked like.

## What the trips taught me to do at home

I want to end this chapter not with another country, but with the working habit the trips, taken together, left behind in me.

The first habit is the comparison. I walk every South African operation now with the unconscious comparison in my hand. I notice the housekeeping the way I noticed it in Dusseldorf. I notice the Monday morning the way I noticed it in East Kilbride. I notice the wet finishing the way I noticed it in Solofra. I notice the toolbox discipline the way I noticed it in Munich, and the mistake-proofing posture the way I noticed it in Japan. The local operation is no longer being measured against itself. It is being measured against the best version of itself I have seen, anywhere in the world, doing the same job.

The second habit is the suitcase. I do not return from an overseas trip without a single concrete habit, written down in the small notebook, that I intend to import into the operation I am running at home. It is not always a large habit. It is sometimes a single sheet of paper, or a single change in the order in which an end-of-shift checklist is read, or a single rearrangement of a foreman's Monday morning. The discipline is not the size of the habit. It is the refusal to come home from an overseas trip empty-handed.

The third habit is the patience. Overseas exposure, when it is real, slows a leader down rather than speeding him up. The man who has seen another country properly does not come home full of slogans. He comes home a little quieter, a little more careful with his standards, and a great deal more reluctant to accept the easy domestic answer.

I have, in the course of forty years, flown out of Jan Smuts and out of O.R. Tambo more times than I have any business counting. I have come home, every time, with the continent on the cabin map slightly smaller than it was when I left, and with the benchmark in my head slightly higher than it was when I last walked into a South African factory. The two movements, over a working life, add up to a global mindset. The mindset is not built by a single trip. It is built one trip at a time.

If I were sending a young engineer overseas tomorrow, I would tell her three things, and only three. Walk the floor. Write it down. Bring the comparison home. The rest, given enough trips and enough years, will assemble itself on the inside of her head, where the benchmark lives.
