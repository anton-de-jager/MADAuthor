# Tiger Brands Turnarounds: Mogul Lines, Chocolate and Peanut Butter

There is a particular phone call that arrives in the life of every transformation specialist sooner or later, and when it arrives you know, before the second sentence, that the company on the other end has finally run out of its own answers. The call that brought me to Tiger Brands came on a Tuesday morning. I remember it because I had spent the previous evening writing up the final notes from the Candy Tops and Mr Sweet integration, and I was sitting at my desk in Johannesburg in that quiet hour between an old contract and a new one, when the operating mind is at its most useful and the diary is at its emptiest.

The voice on the line belonged to a senior executive at Tiger Brands. He came to the point with the kind of directness I have always preferred. There was a plant in the confectionery group, he said, that had received a capital installation of roughly two hundred million rand for a new gums-and-jellies line. The line had been commissioned. The line did not work. The volumes promised to the board were not being produced. The chocolate and candy operations alongside that line were tired, under-measured and below standard. The supply chain across the broader confectionery business was not what the group expected of it. Would I come and see them.

I went and saw them. By the end of that meeting we had the outline of an agreement. I was to take responsibility for the failed mogul line first, restore it to the volumes the business plan had assumed, and then, once it was earning its keep, broaden my mandate across the rest of confectionery. The contract was set up in stages, as the best contracts always are. I would prove the first task. The second task would be mine to lose.

## A Two-Hundred-Million-Rand Machine That Would Not Run

For readers who have never stood in front of a gums-and-jellies installation, a brief description is in order, because the scale of it explains the seriousness of the problem. A modern mogul line is not a machine. It is a city block of equipment laid out end to end. Starches are conditioned and laid into tray after tray of moulded impressions. A heated liquid sweet mixture is deposited into those impressions to set. The trays are stacked, cured for hours in temperature-controlled rooms, returned, emptied, dusted, sorted, polished, sometimes sugared, sometimes oiled, and finally packed into the bags and jars that find their way onto a retailer's shelf. Every step has a tolerance. Every tolerance is small. And every variation at the front of the line compounds itself at the back, where the consumer pack is supposed to be uniform and the retailer's complaint book is supposed to stay empty.

The installation Tiger Brands had paid for was, on paper, world-class. The civil works had been done. The starch handling was in place. The depositors had been commissioned. The drying tunnels and the curing rooms had been built. Every piece of capital equipment that two hundred million rand can buy was sitting in that hall. And yet the line was producing a fraction of its nameplate, and on most days it was producing scrap.

I walked it the way I have always walked a plant. I did not write a report in the first fortnight. I did not promise the executive committee a date. I stood at each station, sometimes for an hour, and watched. I had lunches with the maintenance fitters. I read the commissioning logs that had been quietly filed away in cupboards. I asked the operators, who had been treated as the problem, what they would do if they were given the keys.

What I found was, in the end, not exotic. The line had been installed by competent contractors against a specification that was internally inconsistent. The starch-moisture range coming off the conditioning step did not match the depositor's preferred operating window. The cooling profile in the curing rooms had been set against an assumed ambient that the Gauteng summer did not respect. The changeover routines between products had not been written down by anybody, which meant every shift improvised its own. The maintenance schedule had been copied from a sister plant in another country whose water and humidity bore no relation to ours. None of these were dramatic failures. Each of them was a few percentage points of efficiency. Stacked together, they were a plant that did not run.

I attacked them in the order in which they hurt us. We rewrote the starch conditioning specification first, because the rest of the line could not be settled until the front of the line behaved. We re-mapped the cooling profile to the conditions that actually existed in our curing rooms rather than the conditions a European brochure said should exist. We sat the operators down, in small groups, and wrote the changeover procedures together, so that the words on the laminated sheets matched the work being done by the hands. We rebuilt the maintenance schedule around our real failure modes, identified by reading two years of breakdown reports rather than by importing somebody else's PM schedule from another country. None of this was glamorous. All of it was necessary.

Six months from the day I walked through that gate, the mogul line was producing at the volumes the original business plan had committed to. The board, which had braced itself for a write-down on the asset, was quietly relieved. The executive who had hired me did not make a speech about it, which I appreciated, but he did the thing that mattered more than a speech. He extended the contract.

## Promotion to Head of Supply Chain for Confectionery

When the mogul line had been steady for a full quarter, and the maintenance crew was running it without me standing behind them, the same executive came back to my office with a larger offer. The confectionery group as a whole was underperforming. The candy and chocolate operations sitting under the same roof as the gums-and-jellies plant were not in the same critical condition the mogul line had been, but they were not what they needed to be either. The volumes were soft. The costs were drifting upwards. The quality reject numbers, on certain product families, were in the wrong place. Would I take responsibility for the supply chain across the entire confectionery business.

I accepted. I have always preferred to be measured against a broader mandate rather than a narrower one, because the broader the mandate the more honest the conversation about leverage. A plant manager can argue, sometimes correctly, that he is doing well within the constraints he has been given. A supply chain director cannot. The supply chain director is responsible for the constraints themselves.

That promotion turned a six-month brief into a four-year stay. By the time I closed out my first Tiger Brands contract I had been inside the group for forty-eight months, and I had developed an affection for the people in that confectionery business that has, in its quiet way, never quite left me. They were not the easiest workforce I have ever inherited. They were, in the end, one of the proudest I have ever led.

## The Twenty-Eighty of It

There is a principle that every operating mind eventually rediscovers, whether they have read Pareto in a textbook or simply worked in enough factories. Twenty percent of the causes drive eighty percent of the consequences. The mathematics is uneven by design. A few root causes are responsible for the great majority of the loss, and the great majority of root causes contribute only marginally. The instinct of the inexperienced manager, faced with a poorly performing plant, is to try to fix everything at once. The instinct of the experienced manager is to find the vital twenty percent and to attack it without distraction.

I drew a Pareto chart for every plant in the confectionery group inside my first quarter as supply chain director. Some of those charts were drawn on whiteboards. Some were printed and stuck on the wall outside the supervisor's office. All of them carried the same blunt question across the top: what are the three things that, if we fixed them this month, would deliver more than half of the available improvement.

The answers were rarely surprising once you saw them. In one plant it was changeover time on a single high-volume line. In another it was the reject rate on a particular packing format that nobody had bothered to track properly. In a third it was the unscheduled breakdowns on a single piece of equipment that had been limping along for years because the capital case to replace it had never quite made the cut. The discipline was not in finding the answer. The discipline was in refusing to be drawn into the other eighty percent of the problems until the twenty percent had been closed out.

I held that line as a leader. There are always people in a large business who want to spend a meeting talking about the problem they find most interesting rather than the problem doing the most damage. I have nothing against intellectual curiosity, but a confectionery business with thin margins cannot afford a leadership team that fights every battle. I made a habit, in those four years, of beginning every operating review with the same question. Which of our top three loss categories are we closing this month, and what is stopping us from closing them faster. The first time the question is asked, the room looks uncomfortable. The tenth time, the answers become specific. The hundredth time, the question has become the culture.

## The Fourteen-Month Transformation of Candy and Chocolate

The mogul line had been a contained problem. The candy and chocolate operations were a different kind of brief. The plants were larger, the workforces were bigger, the product families were more diverse, and the cultural inheritance was longer. I gave myself fourteen months to transform them, which on paper looked optimistic and in practice turned out to be the right window.

The numbers matter, because the numbers explain why the work was so demanding. I had roughly fourteen hundred wage staff reporting up through the supply chain and roughly forty management staff sitting between me and the lines. Fourteen hundred wage staff is not an abstraction. It is fourteen hundred people whose mortgages, families, school fees and Sunday plans depend on the decisions made in the head office. Forty management staff is not an abstraction either. It is forty individuals with their own histories in the business, their own ways of doing things, and their own quiet judgements about whether the new supply chain director was going to be worth listening to.

I have always believed that a transformation programme is, before anything else, a leadership programme. You can install every measurement system in the modern operating playbook, but if the supervisors on the floor do not believe in what you are doing, and if the wage staff behind them feel they are being managed at rather than managed with, the measurements will be obeyed for a quarter and forgotten by the second year. The capital cost of putting in new systems is small. The leadership cost of making them stick is everything.

I began with the supervisors. I did not bring in consultants. I sat with them, in groups of six or eight, and I asked them what they would change in their own area if they had a free hand. I wrote their answers down. I read the answers back to them at the next session and asked which of those changes they would take responsibility for delivering in the next ninety days. By the end of that first cycle of conversations I had a transformation programme that, in its essentials, had been written by the supervisors themselves, with my role being to remove the obstacles they could not remove for themselves.

The wage staff felt the shift quickly. People on a confectionery line know, very quickly, whether the senior managers are interested in them or interested only in the numbers. I made a point of walking every shift on every plant at least once in those first months. I stood at the wrapping machines. I sat in the cafeteria at lunch. I learned the names of the long-service operators and I learned the names of their children, in the way one learns anything that matters, by asking and remembering. Within the first six months the air in the plants had changed, and you could feel it before any of the spreadsheets caught up.

The technical work, against that background, became simpler. We rationalised the product range on the highest-cost lines. We tightened the changeover routines, which in confectionery are everything because the recipes change often and the down time between recipes is where the money leaks. We attacked the reject rates one product family at a time, working from the top of the Pareto chart and refusing to be distracted by the categories below it. We installed proper shift-handover discipline, so that the early shift could not leave the line in a state the late shift would have to spend the first hour repairing. We brought the food safety standards up to and beyond the audit thresholds, because in any branded confectionery business a single safety incident can do more damage to a brand than a year of poor service can do.

At the end of fourteen months the candy and chocolate operations were running differently. The volumes were where they were supposed to be. The costs had come down materially. The reject rates on the worst-performing product families had been more than halved. The workforce had a quiet pride in what they were producing. None of that was the work of one man, and I will not pretend otherwise. It was the work of fourteen hundred people who had decided, somewhere along the way, that they wanted their plants to be the best in the group.

## Putting OEE on the Wall

The technical centrepiece of all this work, the single piece of equipment I trust more than any other in a manufacturing turnaround, was the real-time production monitoring system. We installed Overall Equipment Effectiveness measurement across the confectionery plants and we put the screens where every operator and every supervisor could see them. Not in a control room behind a locked door. Not on a manager's desktop computer in an office down the corridor. On the wall at the end of the line, in numbers large enough to be read across the floor, updated in real time.

I have, in the course of my career, watched many factories try to argue with their own performance. Once the OEE numbers are on a screen at the end of the line, the argument ends. The machine tells you what it is doing. The operators see it. The supervisors see it. The plant manager sees it. The site director sees it. The conversation, which until that moment had been about blame and explanation, becomes a conversation about improvement. Where did we lose the ten minutes between half past two and quarter to three. Why is this product family running below its standard rate. Why is the changeover taking longer than we agreed last month. The questions become precise, and precise questions get specific answers, and specific answers can be acted on.

The OEE work was not glamorous. It was, in the truest sense, the kind of operating discipline that does not photograph well for an annual report. But by the time my first Tiger Brands contract ended, that monitoring system had become embedded in the culture of every plant in the confectionery group, and the supervisors who used it had stopped thinking of it as a new initiative and started thinking of it as the way work was done.

## Black Cat and the Lesson of the Peanut

The last problem of that first Tiger Brands era arrived from outside the confectionery business altogether. The Black Cat peanut butter plant, in Randfontein, was misbehaving. Outputs were below where they should have been. Quality reject rates were stubborn. The technical team had been chasing the symptoms for months. The executive committee asked whether, in the time I had left on my contract, I would take a look.

I went out to Randfontein the following week.

Peanut butter is a deceptively complicated product. To a consumer it is one ingredient, ground and salted, perhaps sweetened. To a manufacturer it is a chemistry experiment that depends on the raw material more than most processors are willing to admit. The peanut comes in graded by size, by oil content, by moisture, by the proportion of broken kernels, by the residual aflatoxin position, by the season, by the supplier and by the field. Get the wrong peanut and there is no roasting profile in the world that will rescue the product downstream.

When I walked the Black Cat line I could see, within a few hours, that the engineering team had been trying to fix a raw-material problem with manufacturing tools. They had been adjusting screws on grinders. They had been reworking roasting profiles. They had been studying the maintenance logs of the depositing equipment. All of that work was honest, and all of it was beside the point. The peanut coming through the goods-receiving doors was variable, and a variable peanut produces a variable peanut butter no matter what the downstream machinery is asked to do.

I worked the problem at its source. We tightened the incoming specifications. We changed the way we tested peanuts at the goods-receiving stage, with the testing now being done before the load was tipped rather than after. We worked with the procurement team to put pressure on suppliers whose deliveries had been inconsistent for years, and in one or two cases we changed suppliers altogether. Inside the plant we installed the same OEE measurement system that had become standard across the confectionery group, so that the operators could see, in real time, what the machinery was doing once the raw material had been brought into specification.

The improvement came faster than even I expected. Outputs lifted dramatically. The quality reject position stabilised and then improved. The plant that had been a quiet headache for the executive committee became, within a few months, a plant the executive committee no longer had to discuss. There is no greater compliment a transformation specialist can receive than to have his work removed from the operating review agenda because it has stopped being a problem.

## What That First Tiger Brands Era Taught Me

When my four-year contract ended, I closed it on the high note I have insisted on for myself in every assignment of my contract years. The mogul line was running. The candy and chocolate plants were transformed. The OEE screens were on the walls and being watched. The Black Cat plant was producing. The handover documentation was clean. The supervisors I had developed were stepping into the roles they had earned.

The lessons of those four years have stayed with me, and it is worth setting them down here, plainly, because they have shaped every contract I have signed since.

The first lesson was that capital is not the same as capability. A two-hundred-million-rand installation that does not run is a more expensive problem than a smaller installation that does. The accountants will tell you that the capital has been spent and cannot be recovered, which is true. The operating mind will tell you that the only thing worse than a capital project that has failed is a capital project that has failed and been left in its failed state. Restoration is always cheaper than replacement. But restoration requires that somebody be willing to walk the line, station by station, and find the inconsistencies the original commissioning missed.

The second lesson was that the Pareto chart is the most undervalued tool in modern management. It is not new. It is not fashionable. It does not photograph well in a consulting report. But it is the single most reliable instrument I know for keeping a leadership team focused on the twenty percent of the work that delivers eighty percent of the improvement. Every operating review I have run since those Tiger Brands years has begun with the same question, asked in the same way, until the question itself has become the culture.

The third lesson was that the people are the system. I had walked into a confectionery business with fourteen hundred wage staff and forty managers, and I had walked out with the same fourteen hundred wage staff and most of the same forty managers, but the business they were running had been transformed. The capital had not changed. The buildings had not changed. The machinery, in the main, was the machinery I had inherited. What had changed was the way the people in those plants worked together, watched their own performance, and held each other to a standard. That was not a piece of equipment I had installed. It was a habit I had spent four years building.

The fourth lesson was about raw materials. I have stood in many factories where the conversation about poor performance focused entirely on the downstream machinery. Black Cat reminded me, in a way I have never forgotten, that the most expensive variable in any food plant is the one that walks through the goods-receiving door before the first machine has even been switched on. If your raw material is wrong, no amount of downstream skill will save you. If your raw material is right, even modest machinery can produce a product you can be proud of.

The fifth lesson, and perhaps the one that has mattered most to me as a leader, was that finishing on a high note is not a sentimental gesture. It is the foundation of the next contract. I had walked out of Tiger Brands with the standards higher than when I had arrived, with the people better trained than I had found them, and with the books in better shape than they had been on the day I signed. That habit had cost me nothing extra in those four years. The work to leave on a high note was the same work I would have done anyway. But the consequence of that habit was a phone, sitting in my study, that would continue to ring for years afterwards.

I did not know it on the morning I packed up that office. But the executive who had hired me would, within a few years, pick up that phone himself, and he would do so from a beverages company that needed a hand it remembered. The lessons of the first Tiger Brands era were, in that sense, not the end of a chapter. They were the opening line of the one that followed.
