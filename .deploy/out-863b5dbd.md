# Sparks in the Dust

The first thing you notice, before you ever pick up a tool, is the smell.

It is not one smell. It is three or four of them, layered the way woodsmoke is layered into an old coat, and you do not separate them out until you have been on the floor long enough to know which one means what. There is cutting oil, slightly sweet, slightly metallic, with a thin chemical edge that catches in the back of your throat the first week and then, after a while, just becomes the smell of going to work. There is hot steel, which is its own thing entirely: a clean, hard smell with no sweetness in it at all, the smell of a thing that has been pushed past patience. There is the dry, faintly bitter smell of grinding dust, which settles on your forearms and into the corners of your eyes whether you wear safety glasses or not. And underneath all of it, holding it together, there is the smell of men who have been working since six in the morning, who have stopped sweating because there is nothing left to sweat, and who have made peace with the fact that they will smell of this until they get home and stand under a shower for fifteen minutes, and even then the smell will still be on the clothes they have left folded by the door.

That is the smell of the place I am inviting you into in this book. If you have lived in it, you already know it. If you have not, I am going to ask you to take a breath of it now, in your imagination, because almost everything I am going to tell you over the next fifteen chapters happened inside that smell, or under its long shadow, and the shadow is where most of the lessons that matter to me actually live.

This first chapter is not about a triumph. It is not about a turning point. It is about a starting line, which is something that looks very small at the time and only becomes interesting later, when you turn around and see how far you have walked from it.

## A shop floor, very early in the morning

Let me put you in a specific place.

It is the early hours, before the day shift properly begins, in a fabrication workshop somewhere in industrial South Africa. The exact town does not matter, because there were a dozen towns just like it, and the differences between them were smaller than they looked from the outside. What mattered was the shed. The shed was always more or less the same. A concrete floor, oil-stained in patches that no amount of sweeping ever fully lifted. A roof high enough that the sound of an overhead crane travelling down its rails could be heard from anywhere on the floor, a deep mechanical sigh that the older men no longer registered and the apprentices flinched at for about a week. Roller doors at one end, big enough to drive a truck through, usually half open, the early light coming in low and grey across the floor and making the dust visible in the air.

The dust is important. You learn, quite early, that the dust is always there. You do not see it most of the time. You only see it when the angle of the light catches it, the way you only see the floaters in your eye when you stare at a blank wall. But the moment a beam of sunlight cuts across the workshop at the right angle, you can see that the air itself is full of fine particles, drifting, hanging, slowly settling on every surface. That dust is metal and oil and abrasive and skin, in some ratio you would rather not think about, and it is what your lungs have been doing quiet, patient work on since the day you walked in.

Around the floor, at this hour, you would see the same scene any morning of the year. A few of the older tradesmen already there, kettle on, mugs out, the radio low. Someone, always, leaning on a workbench in no particular hurry, finishing a cigarette before clocking on. A foreman somewhere with a clipboard and a slightly tired expression, working out which job had to be finished today regardless of what anyone had been promised yesterday. The night shift, if there had been one, drifting out in twos and threes, saying very little, the way people who have been awake for ten hours under fluorescent light say very little to anyone who has not.

And somewhere in the middle of all this, holding a broom or a wire brush or a tagged-out box of consumables that an artisan had sent him to fetch, the apprentice.

That apprentice, in this book, is me. But I want to be careful about that word *me*, because the boy I am describing was not yet a person with a story. He was a person waiting for one. He had no idea any of this would be worth writing down. He had no idea, frankly, whether he would still be in the same workshop in six months. What he had, instead, was a set of overalls that were slightly too big, a pair of boots not yet broken in properly, and the next shift.

The next shift was the entire plan.

## A boy with no plan beyond the next shift

I want to dwell on that, because it is one of the few things I would say to a young person now without any caveats.

When I started, I did not have a plan. I did not have a five-year vision. I did not have a *career*, in the sense that the word is used by the kinds of people who buy hardback business books at airports. I had a job, and the job had a shift, and the shift had a start time, and the start time was the only horizon I needed.

I want to be honest about why that was, because the temptation, when you write a book like this one, is to make the younger version of yourself look a little more thoughtful than he actually was. To suggest that even then he had a quiet sense of where it was all going. To imply some hidden ambition that simply had not surfaced yet. That would be a kinder story. It would also not be true.

The truth is that I started where I started for the reasons most young men in that world started where they started. The trade was available. The wages, modest as they were, were real money. The work was honest. And, importantly, nobody else in the room was particularly interested in what I wanted to be when I grew up, because everybody in the room was already grown up, and most of them were too tired at the end of the day to ask that kind of question of anyone, themselves included.

If you had asked me, on my first morning, what I wanted from a working life, I would not have been able to give you a useful answer. I might have said something about finishing my apprenticeship. I might have said something about making my parents proud, in that mumbled, half-embarrassed way young men say such things when they are afraid of being teased for them. I would not have said anything about leadership. I would not have said anything about running a business. I would not have said anything about sitting in a boardroom, because I did not, at that point, know that boardrooms were a place where actual work happened. I thought a boardroom was something you saw in films.

What I had, instead of a plan, was the next shift. And what I have come to believe, after several decades of watching young people start in industries I now sit at the top of, is that this is, very often, a better starting position than the one we now insist young people should arrive with.

The boy who arrives with a five-year plan and a clear narrative about himself is, in my experience, the boy who is most fragile when the plan does not survive contact with the actual work. He has invested in a story before he has invested in the craft. The boy who arrives with no story and no plan, only a willingness to be on the floor at six in the morning and to do whatever is asked of him properly, has no story to protect. He can let the work shape him. The other boy is too busy trying to make sure the work fits his story.

I am not romanticising the absence of ambition. Ambition came later, and it was important when it came, and I will not pretend otherwise. But I do want to say, plainly, in this opening chapter, that the absence of a grand plan in my early years was not a weakness. It was, although nobody around me would have used the word at the time, a kind of openness. I was available to be taught. I was available to be corrected. I was available, in some sense, to be moulded by what I now know to call the culture of the shop floor, because I had not yet hardened around any idea of who I thought I should become.

That openness, more than any particular skill I learned in those first months, is the thing I have spent a working life trying to protect in myself and to find in the people I have hired. It is harder to find than you would think.

## What the workshop taught before it taught anything

Long before the workshop taught me how to cut, weld, or read a drawing, it taught me a handful of other things. None of them were written down anywhere. None of them were said out loud. They were simply enforced, quietly, by the way the place ran, and by the way the men in it behaved when nobody was watching.

The first thing it taught me was time.

Time, in a fabrication workshop, is not what it is in a school or in an office. In a workshop, time is physical. Steel is being heated. Joints are being cooled. Pressure is being held. A weld has to be done in a window that does not negotiate with you. A crane lift has to happen when the rigging is set and the area is clear and the operator is ready, and if any one of those three things is not true, nothing moves. You learn, very quickly, that being five minutes late to your station is not a minor administrative failing. It is a thing other people have had to absorb. Somebody else has had to stand and wait. Somebody else has had to start something they would rather not have started. The cost of your lateness is not in your pay packet. It is on the face of the man who covered for you, and you will see it there for the rest of the day, whether he says anything or not.

I learned to be on time before I learned to weld. I learned, more importantly, that being on time was not a personal virtue. It was a form of respect for other people's work. The two are not the same thing, although they look the same from the outside. A man who is on time because he is proud of his discipline is one kind of worker. A man who is on time because he does not want to make the rigger wait is another kind, and it is the second kind you want next to you when the day is hard.

The second thing the workshop taught me, very early, was that the work has a voice.

I know how that sounds. I am not being mystical. What I mean is something quite practical. If you stand on a workshop floor for long enough, paying attention, you start to notice that the machines and the materials are constantly telling you things, in small, undramatic ways, and that the good tradesmen are listening to them and the bad ones are not. A grinder sounds slightly wrong before it fails. A weld sounds different on the second pass when the heat is off. A piece of steel, when you put a square against it, will tell you within half a second whether the cut is true. The information is always available. You only have to be quiet enough, inside your own head, to receive it.

The men I most wanted to be like, in those first months, were the ones who were quiet on the floor. Not silent, exactly. They talked. They joked, sometimes drily, sometimes not. But they were not noisy in the way some apprentices were noisy, filling the air with chatter to cover up the fact that they were not really paying attention to what their hands were doing. The good men worked with a kind of attentive economy. They moved less than you would expect them to. They said less. And what they did say, they meant.

I did not, at the time, think of this as leadership. I thought of it as competence. It is only much later, sitting in management meetings in carpeted rooms a long way from any cutting oil, that I came to understand what I had been watching in those quiet men on the floor was a complete philosophy of work, and that almost everything I would later need to know about leading people was already on display in front of me, in steel-toed boots, before I was old enough to vote.

The third thing the workshop taught me, and this is the one I want to be most careful with, was the difference between a job done and a job *finished*.

A job done is when the work appears, from a polite distance, to be complete. The cut is made. The weld is laid. The piece is on the trailer. Somebody less experienced, walking past, would assume the work is over.

A job finished is something else. A job finished has been cleaned up. The slag is chipped off. The edges are dressed. The off-cuts are stacked, not scattered. The tools have been wiped down and put back in the place they belong, not the place that was convenient. The drawing has been marked up with the as-built changes, if there were any, so the next person who has to deal with this piece is not chasing ghosts. A job finished, in other words, takes the person who comes after you into account. A job done does not.

The difference between the two is usually about half an hour of work that nobody is going to pay you extra for, and that nobody is going to thank you for, and that most clients would not even notice if you skipped. Nine out of ten apprentices, left to themselves, would skip it. The tenth one would not, because somebody, at some point, had stood next to him while he was packing up and said, *no, my boy, we do not leave it like that*. The tenth one is the one who, fifteen years later, ends up running the workshop.

I was lucky. I had a few of those *no, my boy* moments, early. I will spend a later chapter on the men who delivered them, because they deserve a chapter of their own. For now I will only say that this third lesson, the lesson about the half-hour at the end, has done more for my career than any qualification I have ever held, and I do not think that is an exaggeration.

## The world I am inviting you into

I have been writing for a few pages now without saying clearly who I am writing this book for, and I want to do that before this chapter closes, because the answer is going to shape everything that follows.

I am writing for two people, mainly.

The first is the apprentice on the floor right now. He, or she, because the world has changed in some ways that are entirely for the better, is reading this on a phone in a break room, or on a screen at the kitchen table after a shift, or on the bus on the way in. They have no real sense yet of whether the trade they are in is going to be a job, a career, or simply the thing they did before they did something else. They are tired. They are sore in places they did not know could be sore. They are not sure whether they are any good at this yet, and the people around them have not told them, because the people around them grew up in a culture that does not hand out reassurance for free. To that reader, I want to say: this book is for you, and the boy in the overalls in the first part of this chapter was, once, exactly you.

The second person I am writing for is the manager who started on a floor like the one I have just described, or in a kitchen, or in a garage, or behind a counter, and who is now sitting in an office with a glass door and a calendar full of meetings and a quiet, private suspicion that the most important things they know about their work were learned long before any of those meetings began. That manager is sometimes nostalgic for the floor and sometimes ashamed of the nostalgia, because the language of modern business does not really know what to do with the kinds of lessons that get learned at six in the morning around a kettle. To that reader, I want to say: you are not wrong. The floor did teach you most of it. This book is, in part, an attempt to put words around things you have always known but have not always had permission to say out loud.

There are also, I suspect, people in between those two. People who never worked a trade themselves but who manage people who did. People who run businesses that depend on hands they have never shaken. People who want to understand, from the inside, what a working life looks like when it does not start in a lecture theatre. To them I would say only: you are welcome here, and I will try not to waste your time.

What I am not going to do, in this book, is pretend that the world I came up in was a simple one, or a fair one, or a complete one. It had its cruelties. It had its blind spots. There were good men in it and bad men in it, and the line between the two was not always where you would expect to find it. There were things done on shop floors in those years that we would not, and should not, accept today, and I have no interest in romanticising them. At the same time, the floor I came up on produced, in me and in many of the people I worked with, a set of habits and instincts about work, about other people, and about responsibility that I have not seen reliably produced by any other environment since. Both of those things are true at once. I will try, throughout, to hold both of them honestly.

## A note on the voice of this book

Before I close this opening chapter, a small note about the voice you are going to be reading in for the next four hundred or so pages, because it is going to be a particular voice and you may as well know what to expect.

I am not going to write like a business book. I have read a great many business books over the years, often on aeroplanes, and many of them are excellent and I have learned things from them, but they tend to be written in a register that is not mine. They tend to march. They tend to itemise. They tend to promise, on the back cover, a small number of *key principles* that, once mastered, will *transform* your *approach*. I do not believe in transformation in that sense. I believe in slow accumulation. I believe in the long, undramatic process by which a person becomes, by quiet degrees, slightly better at the actual work in front of them and slightly more useful to the people they work with. That is the only transformation I have ever personally seen, in myself or in anyone I have managed, and so it is the only one I am qualified to write about.

I am also not going to write like a memoir, in the strict sense. I am not going to spend pages on my childhood, or on my family, beyond what is needed to make sense of the working life. There are good books that do that, and this is not one of them. The story this book is telling is a working story. It starts on a shop floor. It ends in a managing director's chair. The personal life, where it touches the working life, will be present. Where it does not, I will leave it alone. That is not because the personal life is unimportant. It is because I am writing about a journey of work, and I want to keep the focus on the work.

What I will try to write like, instead, is a man talking to you over coffee in a workshop canteen, with the door open onto the floor, and the kettle behind him, and forty years of moving up through one industry behind that. The voice will be warm where it can be. It will be plain where it needs to be. It will not, I hope, pretend to know more than it knows, and where I have to make a claim about something I cannot quite prove, it will say so. [unverified] tags, where they appear in this book, are not laziness. They are an honesty about the limits of memory. Some of what I will tell you happened more than thirty years ago, and memory, as anyone who has been in management long enough knows, is a generous editor.

## The starting line, looked at from here

I want to end this first chapter where it began, on a shop floor at six in the morning, but with one small adjustment.

The apprentice I was describing at the beginning, the boy with the overalls slightly too big and the boots not yet broken in, did not know, that morning, that he was at a starting line. There was no banner. There was nobody with a stopwatch. The day looked the same as the day before and the day after. If you had told him then that he was at the start of a journey that would, eventually, take him from a broom to a managing director's office, he would have laughed at you, and not entirely politely. He would have assumed you were either drunk or trying to sell him something. He had no time for either.

But he was at a starting line. He simply did not know it, because starting lines, in working life, are almost never marked. You do not get to know, on the morning, which of your mornings is going to turn out to have been important. You only know, in retrospect, that one of them was. That is part of what I am going to try to do in this book: go back and mark some of those mornings, gently, with the benefit of being able to see now where they led, and suggest, to the young reader especially, that they may very well be standing on one of their own this week without knowing it.

If that is true, and I think it usually is, then the only sensible thing to do is the thing the men on that workshop floor were quietly modelling for me without ever saying it out loud.

Show up.

Be on time.

Pay attention to what the work is trying to tell you.

Stay for the half hour at the end.

That is, in some sense, the whole book. The rest of it is just a longer way of saying those four things, and the story of what happened to one man, over many years, when he tried, with varying success, to live by them.

We will start, in the next chapter, where the real instruction began. Not with grand lessons. With a tool in the hand, and a tradesman next to me with no particular interest in being patient, and the beginning of the long, slow apprenticeship of learning a craft the proper way.

For now, it is enough to be standing in the dust, in the early light, with the kettle on, and the day not yet begun.

That, it turns out, was already more of a beginning than I knew.
