# Exports, Setbacks and the Confectionery Years

There is a particular kind of homecoming that has nothing sentimental about it. After the SA Oil Mills exit and the quiet return of the family from Port Elizabeth to Gauteng, I sat at the kitchen table one Saturday morning and listed the offers in front of me. The list was short, but one name on it carried weight I could not ignore: Adcock Ingram. I had given fourteen years to that company once before. I had walked out as a Technical Director. And now they were asking me to come back, not into the manufacturing role I had built my reputation on, but into something I had circled the edges of my whole career — exports.

I said yes before the end of the weekend.

## Back Through the Same Gate

Walking back through the Adcock Ingram gate as Director of Exports felt like opening a familiar book at a chapter I had never read. The site smelled the same. Half the security guards greeted me by name. A few of the engineers I had hired in the late nineties were now seasoned managers in their own right, and one or two had stories about machines I had personally signed off on that were still running, fifteen years later, exactly as we had commissioned them. There is a quiet pride in that — in seeing your old work still earning its keep long after the contract has ended.

But this time, the brief was different. Adcock Ingram, Tiger Brands and Beacon Sweets were no longer to be treated as independent export islands. The group strategy had matured. Three product portfolios — over-the-counter pharmaceuticals, packaged foods and confectionery — were to be consolidated into a single export basket and pushed out through one coordinated office. I was that office.

On paper it looked obvious. In practice, very few large South African groups had managed to make a unified export programme work. Each business unit had its own pricing logic, its own distributor relationships, its own packaging conventions and, most importantly, its own marketing director with very firm views on what should and should not be sold beyond our borders. My first ninety days were less about exports and more about diplomacy.

## The Logic of a Unified Basket

I called it the basket strategy, partly because it sounded less corporate than "portfolio consolidation" and partly because I wanted the salespeople to picture it literally. A basket is what a distributor carries into the back of a shop. If our distributors could carry one basket with Adcock cough syrup, Tiger Brands maize meal and Beacon sweets all inside it, the cost per kilometre of selling collapsed. One container could leave Durban with three brand families inside it. One invoice could clear three sets of marketing budgets. One relationship in Lagos or Lusaka could open three doors.

The arithmetic was clean. The execution was anything but.

We began with the African corridor because the regulatory environment and the cultural familiarity made it the obvious place to test the system. Botswana, Namibia, Zimbabwe, Zambia, Mozambique and into East Africa — each market had its own quirks, but the basic logistics were within reach. From there, we pushed north into West Africa, where Nigeria and Ghana, despite their well-known port frustrations, offered scale that justified the operational pain.

Then I turned my attention to a smaller market that surprised everyone, including me: Cyprus. A Cypriot importer had taken an interest in our cough syrups and our boiled sweets at one of the European trade fairs. I flew out for what I expected to be a two-day visit and ended up staying nearly a week, walking through pharmacies and small grocery chains, watching how Mediterranean shoppers picked up an over-the-counter product, weighed it in their hand, read the back, and made their choice. The visit gave us our first beachhead in Southern Europe. From Cyprus, the trail led naturally to Greece, where the same distributor had family connections, and where a small but steady volume of cough preparations, antacids and confectionery began to move every quarter.

## Lessons in Negotiating Across Cultures

Anyone who tells you that international business is just domestic business with a longer flight has never sat across a table from a Greek pharmaceutical wholesaler at ten o'clock at night, drinking ouzo and being told, very politely, that the price you quoted on Tuesday morning is now considered an opening bid. Cyprus and Greece taught me three lessons I carried into every export deal that followed.

The first was patience as a pricing weapon. In African and European markets alike, the buyer who is most desperate for a deal usually loses the most margin. I taught my export team to walk into every meeting with two prices in their head — the price we wanted, and the price we would still walk away from happily — and to refuse to feel pressured by the dinner, the hospitality or the warmth of the host. Warm hospitality is a wonderful thing, but it does not pay the freight bill back to Johannesburg.

The second lesson was the importance of the local partner. In every market where we eventually built durable share, there was a local distributor whose name we trusted more than our own contract. The Cypriot family who carried us into Greece were not just our customers; over time they became our scouts, our translators and our risk filter. When they told us a payment term was unrealistic for a particular sub-region, we listened. When they told us a competitor was about to enter, we acted.

The third lesson was about packaging. South African export packs almost always had to be redesigned for international shelves. Languages, regulatory marks, barcodes, child-safety closures, expiry-date formats, font sizes for elderly readers — every market had its own quiet rules, and getting any one of them wrong could mean a full container sitting in customs for six weeks. I learned to start every new market by asking, not "Can we sell here?", but "Can we print here?"

## The Wall We Could Not Push Through

For about two and a half years the basket strategy delivered. Exports across the consolidated group grew in double digits year on year. We doubled our African footprint. We held the Cyprus and Greece volumes steady. We even ran a small pilot into the Middle East. The board liked the trend line. So did I.

And then, slowly, we ran into the wall that no exporter wants to discuss publicly but which kills more export programmes than any tariff or exchange rate: domestic capacity.

The Adcock manufacturing sites were busy. The Tiger Brands plants were busy. Beacon's confectionery lines were busy. When a domestic order from a South African retailer collided with an export order from Lagos, the domestic order won every time. It had to. The domestic market was the cash engine. The local retail customers — the supermarket buyers who could place or remove a brand from a national chain with a single decision — were not going to be told that their stock was sitting on a ship to Mombasa.

I argued for protected production windows. I argued for dedicated export shifts. I built spreadsheets showing the marginal contribution of an export order versus a domestic one, especially when the rand was weak. I won some battles. But the structural truth was unchanged: in a country that had never built spare manufacturing capacity for its export ambitions, exports would always be the buffer. When the local plants got tight, exports got throttled.

I want to be honest about this. The throttling of the export business was not a personal defeat in the way a failed plant project would have been. The numbers were good. The relationships we built lasted. But the limit was real, and I had to make peace with it. There comes a moment in every transformation career when you must distinguish between the things you can fix and the things that are structural. Domestic capacity, in that group at that time, was structural.

## Pivoting Inwards: Retailer Brands

When the export ceiling became clear, I made a deliberate decision not to fight it any longer. Instead, I went to the executive committee with a different proposal: let me take the export discipline — the cost control, the packaging rigour, the customer-led product design — and turn it inwards. Let me run Retailer Brands.

For readers outside the consumer-goods world, Retailer Brands are the products you see on a supermarket shelf carrying the supermarket's own name rather than a national brand. They are usually cheaper, often manufactured by the same factories that make the branded equivalents, and they have become, over the last twenty years, one of the most important profit pools in modern retail. For a group like ours, with serious manufacturing depth, Retailer Brands were a natural fit — but only if you could run them on margins thinner than anything our branded business had ever tolerated.

I took the Retailer Brands portfolio through a transformation that, in style, was very similar to what I had done at SA Oil Mills. I walked the lines. I asked, for every product, the same blunt question: what is the cheapest way we can make this to the agreed specification without breaking any food-safety or pharmaceutical-quality rule? I rationalised SKUs. I renegotiated commercial terms with two of our largest retail customers. I tightened the packing-line changeover times. And I made a point, every quarter, of walking through the stores myself, looking at our products next to the branded equivalents and asking honestly whether the consumer was getting fair value.

Retailer Brands grew. Margins improved. And then a particular contract walked through the door that would teach me one of the harder lessons of my career.

## The Candy Tops Contract

Candy Tops was a confectionery business based in East London. They had won — or, more accurately, been chased by a major retailer until they accepted — a private-label contract that, on the volume numbers, looked attractive, but on the unit economics was wafer-thin. Our group had a strategic interest in extending its confectionery footprint, and Candy Tops looked like the right vehicle.

I was sent down to East London to make it work.

I arrived expecting the familiar transformation playbook to apply. It did not. The plant was undercapitalised. The packaging lines were old and slow. The workforce was loyal but had never been exposed to the kind of operational discipline I had brought into other sites. And the contract itself was harsh: the retailer had negotiated a price that left almost no headroom for inefficiency. Every percentage point of waste, every changeover delay, every quality reject came directly off our margin.

I did what I knew how to do. I put OEE measurement in. I attacked the top three loss categories on the Pareto chart. I trained supervisors in basic shift-handover discipline. We made progress. Output rose. Waste came down. By any normal measure, it was a competent transformation.

But the contract itself was still poor. And here is the lesson I want to underline for any reader who one day finds themselves in a similar position: there are some commercial situations in which operational excellence cannot save you. If the price you have agreed to sell at is below the cost at which your industry can sustainably produce, no amount of factory discipline will close the gap. You will narrow it. You will narrow it impressively. But you will not close it.

I came back to head office and said so, plainly. I had been hired to transform operations, but I refused to let an operational story disguise a commercial one. The contract needed to be renegotiated, the volume needed to be cut, or the business needed to be merged into a larger confectionery footprint that could carry it. That was a hard conversation. It was the kind of conversation that, earlier in my career, I might have softened. By this point I had learned that softening the truth, in a loss-making business, only delays the bill.

## The Mr Sweet Merger and Its Aftermath

The third of those options became the path forward. Candy Tops was merged with Mr Sweet, a larger confectionery business with deeper category exposure, more sophisticated production technology and the kind of scale that could spread the East London plant's fixed costs across a broader revenue base. On paper, it was a sensible deal. The combined entity had a credible product range, real distribution and the prospect of becoming a meaningful player in the South African sweet market.

I stayed with the merged business for a period to see the integration through. We harmonised systems. We rationalised SKUs across the combined portfolio. We took a hard look at every plant in the combined footprint and ranked them on cost-to-produce. There was good operational work done in that period, and I am still proud of it.

But the harsh truth of the confectionery years did not go away. Sweet manufacturing in South Africa, like sweet manufacturing in many parts of the world, is a thin-margin business operating against retailer power, raw-material volatility — especially in sugar, glucose and cocoa — and consumer trends that increasingly pull spending towards healthier categories. We could optimise the factories. We could not change the structural economics of the category.

By the time my contract there ended, I had a private mental file labelled simply *Sweets — Lessons*. It was the most honest document I had written about my own work in years. I want to share its main entries.

## Lessons from the Confectionery Years

The first lesson was about contracts. Be very, very careful what you sign for. In a thin-margin business, the price you agree on day one is almost certainly the price you will be living with three years later, regardless of what the inflation rate does to your inputs. If a contract does not have a sensible mechanism to pass through raw-material movements, it is not a contract — it is a slow leak.

The second lesson was about scale. There is a minimum efficient scale below which a confectionery plant simply cannot compete, no matter how skilled the operators or how disciplined the supervisors. The East London plant, in isolation, was always going to struggle. Inside a larger group, with shared overheads and combined buying power, it had a fighting chance. Below that threshold, no transformation specialist on earth could have made it work sustainably.

The third lesson was about the limits of personal effort. I had spent my career proving that focused, analytical, hands-on work could turn around almost any operation. The confectionery years quietly amended that belief. Personal effort is necessary, but it is not sufficient. The commercial and structural conditions have to give you something to work with. When they do not, the honest thing to do is to name the structural problem out loud and propose a structural solution, even if it is uncomfortable.

The fourth lesson was about humility. Up to that point, every business I had touched had come out the other side stronger and, in most cases, more profitable. The confectionery years were the first time I had to look at a business I had personally led and admit that, although I had made it better, I had not made it good. That admission, in my own private notes, was harder to write than any board presentation I have ever given. But it was, in the end, the foundation of the work that followed at Tiger Brands and beyond.

## The Quiet Hand of Faith and Family

In the middle of all of this, the family stayed steady, as they always had. Antonette had by then become quite practised at the rhythm of my contract life — the long weeks away in East London, the late phone calls about a packing-line breakdown, the occasional Sunday flight back to Johannesburg in time for church. She did not complain about the rhythm. She did, however, remind me, in her quiet and unshowy way, that no contract was worth the children growing up without their father at the dinner table on the nights when I was in town.

I held to that. On the nights I was home, I was home. The briefcase stayed in the study. The phone went on silent. The lessons I was learning about commercial reality in East London were, in their own way, being matched by a parallel lesson at home: that the structural foundation under a long marriage is not romance, and not even shared history, but a thousand small choices to put the people in front of you ahead of the spreadsheet behind you.

When I look back at the export years and the confectionery years together, I do not see them as a low point. I see them as the period in which my transformation playbook grew up. Africa, Cyprus and Greece taught me how to sell across borders. The throttled-export ceiling taught me that not every problem is mine to fix. Candy Tops and Mr Sweet taught me to look at commercial structure before operational symptoms. And the road between East London and Johannesburg, driven and flown more times than I can count, taught me — once again, more deeply — that the family I was working for was also the family I had to be present for.

The next chapter of my contract life was already taking shape. Tiger Brands, in its full operational complexity, was about to ask me a much bigger question. And by the time it did, the lessons of the confectionery years would be exactly the equipment I needed to answer it well.
