# A Miner's Son: Early Home Life and Formative Years

If you want to understand the working life I have written about in the rest of this book, you have to begin in a small, semi-detached house on the East Rand, in the rows of married quarters the mine had thrown up around itself the way a tree throws up shade. Our house was not the smallest on the row and it was not the largest. It had the regulation number of rooms, the regulation patch of garden, and the regulation pair of clothes-lines strung between two metal poles in the back yard. From the kitchen window, on a clear day, you could see the head-gear of Sub Nigel rising above the roofs of the houses opposite. From the front step, on every day of the week, you could hear the shift hooter call my father, and a thousand other fathers, to and from the cage that took them underground.

That hooter was the metronome of my childhood. We did not own a clock more authoritative than it. The morning hooter said the day-shift men were due on the surface. The afternoon hooter said the next shift was filing in. The Sunday morning silence — when the wheels above the shaft turned slower, and the row of houses smelled of polish instead of coal-dust — was, in its way, the loudest hooter of all. It told you which day belonged to the mine, and which day belonged to the Lord.

## The youngest of five

I was the youngest of five children, and that fact alone shaped a great deal of who I became. There is a particular vantage point given to the last child in a large family. By the time you arrive, the household has already been running for some years without you. The older brothers and sisters have already been to school, already been to confirmation classes, already learned which floorboard creaked when you tried to come in after a curfew. You inherit, as the youngest, a set of household rules that have been tested by the people in front of you. Some have been worn smooth. Others have been worn out.

What I inherited, more than anything, was a sense that the family was an enterprise. It had a budget. It had a schedule. It had a quiet operational discipline that was not announced from the head of the table but was simply assumed by everyone who sat at it. My father went down on his shift. My mother kept the house. My brothers and sisters did their chores. And I, as the youngest, slotted in at the bottom of an established order and learned, very early, that you did not interrupt the running of a household to draw attention to yourself.

It is a lesson I have carried into every plant I have ever managed. A factory, like a household, has a rhythm that precedes you. The new manager who walks in and tries to impose himself on that rhythm in his first week is the manager who loses the floor. The new manager who walks in quietly, watches the rhythm for a while, and only then begins to make his adjustments, is the manager the floor eventually adopts as one of its own. I learned that, not on a factory floor, but at the bottom of a five-child rotation in a mine house on the East Rand.

## A father who went underground

My father was a miner at Sub Nigel Gold Mine, and he spent the working life of a man under the surface of South Africa. He was not an educated man in the formal sense. He had been taught what he needed by the church, by the shaft, and by the long, quiet evenings at the kitchen table, and he carried those three educations into every conversation he ever had with his children.

He was, by temperament, the more outgoing of my parents. Where my mother was shy almost to the point of being invisible in a room of strangers, my father had a friendly word for the men on the row and a steady handshake for the elders at our church. He believed that a man's word was the only currency that travelled with him from the surface to underground and back again. A miner who could not be trusted by the men on his shift would not be trusted by the rope, the lamp, or the rock. My father trusted those three things, and he meant to be the kind of man whom they, in their own way, could trust back.

I have written elsewhere in this book about the lung disease that eventually took him at sixty-three. I do not want to dwell on it again here. I want to say only this. Long before the cough that announced the disease, long before the doctors confirmed what the row of houses already knew, my father had given his children something the disease was never going to be able to take away from him. He had given us a way of being a man. Quiet, devout, dependable, on time, and unwilling to make a fuss about any of those qualities. I have spent fifty-seven working years trying, with mixed success, to live up to that description. I am still trying.

There was an order in which he held the two great loyalties of his life. He believed in his shaft and in his Lord, in roughly that order on a weekday and in the reverse order on a Sunday. He saw no contradiction between the two. The Lord, in his account of the world, expected a man to go down on his shift and to do the work he had been given to do. The shaft, in turn, expected a man to keep the Sabbath holy and to bring his family to church when the wheels above the head-gear had been allowed to slow for a day. The two institutions were not in competition for his loyalty. They were, in his mind, two halves of the same instrument.

## A mother who held the line

My mother was the other half of the arrangement, and in some ways the more remarkable half. She was a shy woman by nature, and the world she lived in did not give her many opportunities to outgrow that shyness. She had married a miner. She had raised five children on a miner's wage. She had stretched a household budget around school shoes and church clothes and the occasional small treat that a child remembers for fifty years afterwards because it had cost her a sacrifice nobody told the child about.

She held the line in the way that women of her generation, on the East Rand and elsewhere, held the line. Without complaint. Without theatre. Without the modern vocabulary we now use to describe the invisible labour of a household. The dishes were done because she did them. The shirts were ironed because she ironed them. The school lunches were packed because she packed them. The garden ran because she ran it. The household, in plain language, was a small, well-managed enterprise that posted no annual results, paid no dividend, and yet returned, over five decades, the only return that has ever truly compounded in my life.

She had one habit in particular that I want to record here, because it explains a great deal of what came afterwards. She woke me, in the dark, before the rest of the row had started its day, to study. I was a working apprentice by then. The trade test was years off, the engineering diploma further off still, and the school books and correspondence material were stacked on the corner of the kitchen table where the sugar bowl ought to have been. My mother would have a single light on in the kitchen, a kettle on the stove, and a chair pulled up to the corner where my books were waiting. She did not say much. She did not need to. The chair was pulled up. The light was on. The kettle was warm. The message was unmistakable. The day belonged to the mine. The hour before the day belonged to my studies.

I have, in my working life, written a great deal about discipline. About showing up early. About doing the unglamorous work before the visible work begins. About the man who has already pulled on his overalls in the change-room when the second alarm goes. Every one of those habits was originally an inheritance from a shy woman with a single kitchen light. She did not call it leadership. She called it making breakfast.

She died a year after my father, at fifty-four. I have always believed that fifty-four was the age her body recorded but that her real age, by then, was much older. She had given the household everything she had. There had been nothing in reserve for the year in which her husband was no longer underground and her youngest children had begun to leave the row. I think of her, often, when I see a quiet woman in a meeting room hold an entire family of a business together without being noticed. The world does not name those women publicly. Their husbands and their sons are supposed to. I am naming her here, in print, fifty years after the fact, because I should have done it more often when she was alive.

## Five rooms, two parents, five children and a Lord

The house on the row had five rooms and a verandah. Two parents, five children, and an unseen Sixth occupant whose presence was as real to my parents as the kitchen table. We prayed before we ate. We prayed before we slept. We prayed in the morning before my father went down. We did not pray ostentatiously. We did not pray for the neighbours to hear. We prayed the way you do something that has been done every day for so long that the words have become almost invisible — leaving behind only the orientation of the family, like the lines on a compass that a navigator no longer has to read because his hand already knows the direction.

The moral economy of the row was governed by a small, unwritten code. You did not steal from a man whose lamp had been on the same hook as your father's. You did not gossip about a family whose washing was on the same line as yours. You did not raise your voice in the kitchen if it could be heard in the kitchen next door. You did not take the Lord's name in vain in front of the children, and you did not, under any circumstance, miss church on the Sunday after a payday — because a man who could not put his shilling in the plate on a Sunday after he had been paid was a man who had forgotten the order of his loyalties.

There were five children and a single household budget, and the budget did not stretch to luxuries. Christmas was a real Christmas, but it was a measured one. New school uniforms came in at the start of the year and were expected to last to the end of it. Shoes were repaired before they were replaced. There was a careful, almost engineered husbandry of small resources that my mother practised without ever calling it that. I have walked into businesses, forty years later, that did not husband their working capital with a fraction of the discipline my mother brought to a tin of polish and a pair of school shoes.

## The brother who went in first

Of my four older siblings, the one who left the deepest mark on the trajectory of my own working life was the brother who became an apprentice Boilermaker at Sub Nigel before I did. He blazed the trail, and a younger brother, watching from the row, learned more from his going in than from any career advice the household could otherwise have offered.

There is a particular kind of older brother who, without ever sitting you down to explain anything, teaches you the shape of the next ten years of your life by living the next ten years of his own in front of you. Mine came home in overalls, and the overalls had the unmistakable smell of a mine workshop on them — that mixture of oil, hot steel, sweat and the faintly mineral dust that comes off the rock when a bench saw has been running through it all afternoon. He came home tired in a way that I had not, until then, seen a young man be tired. He came home, after his trade test, with the quiet confidence of someone who had been tested by a panel of older artisans and judged competent. He did not boast. Boilermakers do not boast. Their work boasts for them, in the form of a weld that holds and a bracket that runs true.

I watched him, and I made a decision long before I was old enough to articulate it. I would do what he had done. I would serve an apprenticeship as a Boilermaker. I would go in through the same door he had gone in through, and I would take from the mine the trade test he had taken from it. But — and this is the part of the decision I did not say out loud at the dinner table at fourteen — I would not stay. I would use the mine as a starting point, the way my brother was using it as a starting point, and I would, in due course, find the door that led out of it.

I owe him a debt of a particular kind. He did not know that he was being studied. Older brothers seldom do. They are too busy being themselves to notice that a younger brother is taking notes. He went in, did the work, passed the trade test, and got on with his life. The younger brother behind him absorbed every step of that journey and folded it into a plan he would not fully execute for another four years.

## A decision at sixteen

I left school at sixteen and signed on as an apprentice Boilermaker at Sub Nigel Gold Mine. The decision was not a dramatic one. It was, in the household economy of a miner's family on the East Rand, the obvious decision. The mine was the largest employer in our small world. It paid apprentices a real wage. It taught a real trade. It had taken my father and my brother before me, and it was prepared to take me on the same terms.

What was less obvious — what I did not announce to anyone at the time, not even to my mother, who would have been the only person in the house who would have entirely understood it — was the second half of the decision. I had decided, on the same morning that I had decided to go in, that the mine would be a place I started from and not a place I stayed in. I do not know how a sixteen-year-old comes to a decision of that clarity. I suspect it is a combination of things — the cough my father had begun to develop, the brother who was already a year ahead of me, the chair in the kitchen and the light on at four in the morning, the verses my mother had read to me from the side table in the front room, and the simple, unsentimental arithmetic of a household that did not have enough money for a son who chose the easier path of staying where he had started.

I went in through the door of the mine, as the boys of our row had gone in for two generations. I rode the cage down, on my first morning, with men whose lamps were already old and whose coughs were already the coughs my father would later develop. I learned the trade of a Boilermaker with the seriousness my brother had modelled and my father expected. I did not, on that first morning, tell anyone what I had privately decided. I simply held, against the inside of my chest, the quiet thought that this place was a beginning and not an end. The thought did not have a date attached to it. It did not have a plan. It had only a direction. The direction has been with me ever since.

## What the row gave me

I left the row, in due course, the way the younger children of a long-serving miner are supposed to leave the row — not in any hurry, not with any announcement, but slowly and steadily, on the back of a trade test the mine itself had paid for. The house, eventually, passed out of the family. My parents are buried, side by side, in a small cemetery I still visit when the road takes me back to the East Rand. The row of houses still stands, though the head-gear above Sub Nigel no longer turns the way it once did and the hooter no longer governs the day of the men who live in those rooms.

If I sit, now, at the age of seventy-three, and ask myself what the row actually gave me, the answer is not nostalgia. The row was not picturesque. It was a hard, plain, lower-middle-income environment in which a great deal of hidden labour was being done quietly by people whose names will not appear in any business book of mine. The row gave me three things I have never been able to source from anywhere else, and that I have spent fifty-seven working years trying to repay.

The first was a working definition of duty. My father went down on his shift because the shift required it of him, and because the Lord, in his account of the world, required it of him as well. He did not need to be motivated. He did not need to be inspired. He needed only to be reminded, by the hooter, that the time had come. I have tried, in every plant I have ever managed, to model that posture for the younger engineers around me. The work is the work. The hooter, in whatever modern form it now takes — the ERP alarm, the email at six in the morning, the OEE screen on the wall — is the hooter. You go.

The second was a working definition of stewardship. My mother ran a household budget that should not, on paper, have lasted a single month. It lasted decades. It clothed five children, fed five children, equipped five children with school books and church shoes, and still, somehow, kept a kettle warm in a kitchen at four in the morning for a working apprentice with a correspondence textbook open on the corner of the table. I have not, in any boardroom of my career, met a finance director who husbanded working capital with the seriousness my mother husbanded a tin of polish. I have tried, in every business I have ever helped to transform, to bring some shadow of that seriousness back into the costing meetings.

The third was a working definition of faith. Faith, on the row, was not a marketing instrument. It was not a Sunday performance. It was the orientation of the household, present in the way the family prayed before a meal, present in the way my father carried himself underground, present in the way my mother woke a sixteen-year-old at four in the morning and put a chair beside a kitchen table. The faith was operational. It ran in the background. It governed the order of the loyalties, in roughly the order I have already described — shaft and Lord on a weekday, Lord and shaft on a Sunday. I have, in every difficult decision of my working life, tried to keep that ordering alive. Some days I have done it better than others. The decision to leave mining for AECI, when the time came, was made inside that ordering. So was every retrenchment letter I have ever signed and every reference letter I have ever written for a person I had just let go.

I went into the mine as an apprentice at sixteen, and I went in with the full intention of going out again. The going in is the subject of the next chapter. The going out, eventually, is the subject of the chapter after that. But before any of those chapters could be written, there had to be a row of semi-detached houses on the East Rand, a hooter that governed the day, a father whose lamp was on a hook beside fifty others, a mother with a kettle on the stove at four in the morning, and a brother in oil-smelling overalls who had walked the path one full year ahead of me.

That row is where this book actually begins. Everything that comes afterwards is, in one way or another, an attempt to honour the people who lived on it.
