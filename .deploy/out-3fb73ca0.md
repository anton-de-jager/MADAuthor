# Apprentice to Managing Director: Kolosus and Port Elizabeth

The telephone call came on an ordinary afternoon. I was at my desk at Adcock Ingram, with the kind of paperwork in front of me that a Technical Director acquires the way a workshop acquires sawdust, when a secretary I did not know put through a chairman I did not yet work for. He introduced himself, named his group, named the company inside that group whose Managing Director's seat had become vacant, and asked, in the unhurried way that confident men ask important questions, whether I would consider flying down to Port Elizabeth to meet him for a conversation.

I was forty-two years old. I had given fourteen years to Adcock. I had two grown children settled in Johannesburg, two younger children still at home with Antonette, a tennis court in the back garden, a squash ladder I was climbing every Tuesday evening, and a working week so thoroughly run-in that I could have driven the next decade of it with my eyes shut.

I said yes to the meeting before I had finished saying hello.

That, in itself, taught me something I have since written down for younger engineers. The career-defining call does not announce itself with brass. It comes on an ordinary afternoon, through an ordinary switchboard, from somebody you have never met, and your answer has to be ready before you have had time to be flattered by the question. Mine was. I had been quietly ready, in a way I did not yet have words for, for several years.

## The chairman, the headhunt and the seat

The group that wanted to see me was a large South African export house, and the company they wanted me to run was Kolosus Holdings. The chairman did not waste a meeting. He told me, over a coffee that neither of us drank, that he had been watching the manufacturing turnaround work I had signed my name to inside Adcock, that he had had me discreetly checked out with people whose opinions he trusted, and that he believed the Kolosus operation needed exactly the kind of engineer who treated a plant as a discipline before he treated it as a business.

I asked him three questions. What was the current state of the operation. What had been tried and not worked. And what, in his own view, was the single most uncomfortable truth on the company's balance sheet. He answered all three without hedging. The plant was tired. Several attempts at re-energising it had stalled because each had been built around a personality rather than a process. And the most uncomfortable truth was that the operation, despite being export-led in a market where the rand worked in our favour, was no longer earning the cost of capital his board had committed to it.

I have been in many interviews, on both sides of the table, over the decades since. A great many of them have been theatre. That one was not. By the time the coffee was cold I had been offered the seat. By the time my flight had landed back in Johannesburg that evening I had decided, in principle, that I would take it.

The harder conversation was still ahead. It was the one I had to have at the kitchen table.

## The kitchen-table conversation

A Managing Director's seat in Port Elizabeth, for a Johannesburg family with two grown children whose lives had already begun to root in Gauteng, is not a job offer. It is a relocation request that happens to come with a salary.

I laid it out for Antonette as honestly as I knew how. The opportunity was a step I had been preparing for since the morning I first walked through the Adcock gate. The package was generous. The chairman was a man I could work for. The plant was a challenge in the exact shape I had been trained, by GMP and by fourteen years of regulated manufacture, to take on. And the cost was specific. The two older children, settled in Johannesburg, would not be coming with us. The two younger children would come, and would have to be enrolled in schools in a city neither of us knew. Antonette would have to leave the tennis court, the church congregation and the small, settled rhythm of a household she had spent more than a decade building.

I said all of that and then stopped talking. The decisions in our marriage that have mattered have never been won by argument. They have been arrived at by the quieter process of two people listening to the same question until the same answer surfaces in both of them. We sat with the silence for a long time. Antonette, in the end, said only that if I felt this was the next step, she would make a home in Port Elizabeth as quickly and as completely as she had made every previous home. The faith she carried did not need a long speech to do its work. It simply re-centred the household around what would have to be done next.

I have written, in this book, about the foundations under a career. The Port Elizabeth move was the first time in our marriage that the foundation was tested by a relocation that left some of our children behind. It held. It held because Antonette refused, then as in every season afterwards, to let the family's centre be a postal address. The centre was the faith we shared, and the faith we shared travels light.

## Walking into Kolosus

I arrived at Kolosus in the same way I have arrived at every operation since. Quietly. Without a speech. Without a hundred-day plan announced from a podium. I had learned, by my early forties, that the new Managing Director who walks in and tries to impose himself on the operation in his first week is the Managing Director the floor adopts as an outsider for the rest of his tenure.

For the first fortnight I did not write a single email of substance. I did not make a single promise. I did not propose a single capital project. I walked. I stood next to the tanning drums. I watched a shaving machine for an hour without speaking. I read the maintenance log book before I read the management accounts. I asked the artisans, not the office, what was going wrong with the operation, and I asked them on the floor rather than in a meeting room, in front of the equipment that they would have to live with after I had gone home.

The plant told me what every tired plant tells the engineer who is willing to look. It told me that the operating disciplines had drifted. Procedures existed on paper but were not present at the stations where the work was done. Deviations were closed with "operator error" rather than investigated to root cause. Yield variation that should have been monitored by the hour was being noticed only by the month. Housekeeping had slipped from a daily standard to a Friday standard, and a plant whose floor is only clean on Fridays is a plant whose books are only honest on Fridays.

None of this was anybody's fault in particular. It was the steady, almost invisible erosion that overtakes a plant whose managers have stopped walking it at the start of every shift. I had seen the pattern at Adcock when I first joined as Company Engineer. I had seen it at AECI in Sasolburg. I would see it again, in different costume, at SA Oil Mills, at the confectionery sites, at Black Cat, at Future Life. The pattern is always the same. The remedy is always the same. The only variable is how long the new Managing Director is willing to wait before he insists on it.

## Dorper leather, and the customer behind every hide

Kolosus tanned and finished Dorper sheep-skin leather, and the Dorper is a peculiar gift of the South African veld. It is a hardy meat sheep, raised across the dry interior, and the skin it leaves behind is a remarkable raw material — fine-grained, durable, soft enough to take a high-fashion finish, tough enough to satisfy buyers who would not normally look south of the equator for premium leather. We exported it to Italy, to France, to Japan, to Malaysia, into the broader European market and into one or two niche corners of South-East Asia that I had not previously had to find on a map.

The customer behind every hide changed the way I thought about the plant. In pharmaceuticals, the customer is, in the end, a regulator and a pharmacist standing between the product and a patient. In leather for the European fashion houses, the customer is a buyer who has spent his career running the back of his hand across other people's hides. He can feel a tannage error from across a room. He knows, before he has asked you a price, whether your plant is being run by people who care about what they are sending him.

That changed my walking routine. I walked the wet finishing department the way I had walked the validation suite at Adcock. I asked, of every stage in the process, what the Italian buyer's hand would notice when the consignment landed in Solofra or Vicenza three weeks later. The discipline of writing things down, the procedural rigour I had imported from pharmaceuticals, fitted onto a tannery floor more naturally than I had expected. The product was different. The principle was not.

## Twice a year in Paris

Twice a year, I flew to Paris for the leather fairs. They were, and remain, the marketplaces in which the world's leather industry decides what next season's handbags, jackets, shoes and upholstery are going to look like. The buyers come. The tanners exhibit. The negotiations happen in a dozen languages around small stands with cuttings of finished hides hanging like fabric swatches in a haberdashery.

The first fair was an education in humility. I had spent twenty-six years inside South African manufacturing, and I had imagined that I knew what tough negotiation looked like. The Italian leather buyers taught me, inside half a morning, that I had been negotiating in the shallow end. They walked onto a stand with a complete picture of the global price book in their heads. They knew what the Argentine and Turkish tanneries were quoting. They knew what the dollar had done overnight. They had a position before they had a hello.

I learned a great deal in those Paris weeks that I have carried into every export negotiation since. Four lessons in particular.

- **Walk in with two prices.** The price you want, and the price you would still walk away from happily. If you do not have the second price in your head before the meeting begins, you have already lost margin you did not know you were giving away.
- **Do not be moved by the room.** The fair is theatre. The hospitality is theatre. The man who wines you in the evening is the same man who will quote you a number in the morning that pretends the evening never happened. Warm hospitality is a wonderful thing, but it does not pay the freight bill back to Port Elizabeth.
- **Know what the buyer is going to do with the hide.** A buyer who is finishing the leather for a shoe upper does not value the same things in a hide as a buyer who is cutting it for a jacket panel. If you know his finished product better than he expects you to, the conversation changes.
- **Honour every commitment, especially the small ones.** The fashion industry talks to itself. A South African supplier who delivers a sample two days early, in the exact specification, in the exact volume, will be on three buyers' shortlists by the next fair. A supplier who is late once will be off all three.

The fairs were also, quietly, an education in geography. I had been raised on the East Rand. I had worked in Sasolburg and Johannesburg and now Port Elizabeth. Paris twice a year, with side trips to the Italian tanning districts and occasional flights onward to Japan and Malaysia for customer visits, slowly knocked the parochialism out of my view of the world. South Africa, I came to understand, was a small country with a big plant inventory, and the operating disciplines that worked in our Port Elizabeth tannery worked in Solofra and in Osaka too. The product changed. The machine did not.

## Antonette in Port Elizabeth

While I was on aeroplanes, Antonette built the household in Port Elizabeth from scratch. New schools for the younger children. A new congregation. A new street, a new butchery, a new doctor, a new electrician. The two older children stayed in Johannesburg, and the weekend telephone call became the new substitute for the kitchen table. We flew them down for school holidays. We flew up to see them whenever the calendar allowed. The geographical seam in the family was the price the move had asked, and it was paid every weekend, in airline tickets and in the small ache of a father who was not down the corridor when his older two needed him.

I will say plainly here what I have said in other chapters in other words. The Port Elizabeth years were possible because Antonette made them possible. The schools were chosen, the congregation joined, the household rebuilt, the children's adjustment shepherded, by a woman whose centre was not her postcode. I came home to a settled house at the end of every trip because she had built one. I never had to manage the household and the plant simultaneously, because she managed the household completely while I managed the plant.

## The five years that taught me a Managing Director's job

The Managing Director's job, I learned at Kolosus, is not the same job as the Technical Director's job I had left at Adcock. The Technical Director answers for the plant. The Managing Director answers for the company. The plant is one of his ledgers. The customers are another. The exchange rate is a third. The workforce is a fourth. The board is a fifth. The chairman, if he is a good one, is a sixth. The community in which the company is rooted — in our case, a Port Elizabeth that depended on a handful of large employers — is a seventh.

I made my share of mistakes in those first eighteen months. I underestimated, in the early days, how much of the Managing Director's week had to be spent in the office of the chairman of an important customer rather than on the floor of his own plant. I underestimated, too, how much capital the operating improvements would require before they paid back, and I had one or two uncomfortable conversations with my own board explaining why a tannery cannot be turned around inside a single financial year.

But the discipline I had paid for, in fourteen Adcock years, did its work. The procedures came back to the stations. The deviation register came back to life. The shift-start walk-through came back into the manager's diary. The OEE numbers stopped being a monthly fiction and started being an hourly fact. The yield variation began to narrow. The Italian buyers, slowly, stopped asking me about quality and started asking me about volume. That, in any export business, is the inflection point you are looking for. Quality questions are the questions of a buyer who is testing you. Volume questions are the questions of a buyer who has already decided.

By the third year, we were a different company. By the fourth, we were a company that other companies in the group were beginning to look at with the appraising eye that precedes a corporate event. I had seen that eye before, in other industries. I knew what it usually meant.

## The sale, the severance and the road back to Gauteng

In the fifth year a larger competitor came forward with an offer for Kolosus that the chairman, after the appropriate process, could not in conscience recommend that his board refuse. The numbers worked. The strategic fit was clean. The acquirer's plans for the operation were, on paper, an extension of the work we had done rather than a reversal of it.

I had been in business long enough by then to know what an acquisition does to a Managing Director. The seat is the first piece of furniture the acquirer rearranges. The chairman explained it to me with the directness I had come to expect from him. The deal was good for the shareholders, good for the group and good, in the medium term, for Port Elizabeth. It was, in the immediate term, the closing of a chapter for me. The severance offered was fair. The handover would be orderly. The acquirer's team would be on site within the quarter.

I do not pretend that the news arrived without weight. Five years is a long stretch of a working life. The plant had become the plant my hands knew. The workforce had become the workforce whose family stories I knew at the gate. The Italian buyers had become customers I could read across a stand. Walking away from all of that, on somebody else's timetable, is not a thing an engineer does easily.

But I had also been taught, by the same fourteen years of GMP discipline, that the orderly close of an operation is its own form of professional respect. You do not protest the corporate event. You execute the handover. You write down what the new team will need to know. You walk the acquirer's engineers through the tannage protocols, the yield history, the customer contact list, the open issues on the deviation register, the small unspoken understandings with the chairmen of the customer houses in Solofra and Vicenza. You leave the operation in a condition that any successor can pick up.

I did that, and then I went home. The severance closed five years of my working life and a chapter of the family's life that none of us had quite expected to be temporary when it began. Antonette packed the household with the same quiet competence with which she had unpacked it. The younger children, by then more settled in Port Elizabeth than they had been in Johannesburg, were given the news as honestly as we knew how to give it. The two older children, still in Gauteng, would have us back within driving distance.

The drive north, when it came, was a quieter drive than the one south had been five years before. I had gone down a Technical Director and come back a Managing Director. I had gone down a domestic manufacturer and come back a global one. I had gone down a man whose toughest negotiations had been with regulators in Pretoria and come back a man whose toughest negotiations had been with leather buyers in Paris. The Sub Nigel apprentice who had once been told that boys from mine houses did not run companies was, by then, somebody else entirely.

There were offers waiting on the kitchen table in Gauteng. I sat down with Antonette and began the next sorting. But that, as the chairman had once said to me about a different decision, is a story for another conversation.
