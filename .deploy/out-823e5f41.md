# Foreword: Anything Is Possible

I have written this book for two readers who, on first glance, look as though they could not possibly belong on the same page. The first is sixteen years old. He, or she, is sitting at a kitchen table somewhere in a small town in South Africa, or in a former mining row on the East Rand, or in a township school where the matric results have not been good for a long time, looking at a future that the adults in the room have already half-described as narrow. The second reader is sixty. He has had a full working life behind him and is wondering, now that the calendar has thinned, whether the choices he made when he was sixteen were the right ones, and whether there is anything still to be done with the years he has left.

I am writing for both of them at once because I have been both of them. I was the sixteen-year-old at the kitchen table in 1968, the youngest of five children in a semi-detached mine house, the son of a Sub Nigel boilermaker's mate who had spent his working life underground and who would, in time, die at sixty-three from the lung disease that came with the bargain. I am now the man who is past sixty and has, somewhat to his own surprise, not yet stopped working. I am seventy-three, still under contract as a Business Transformation Specialist, still being asked to walk into operations and rearrange them, and still answering the phone when the call is interesting enough. The same person wrote the rest of this book that wrote this foreword. I want you to remember that as you read on.

## The argument of this book

I am going to make an argument across the chapters that follow, and because I am an engineer by training I am going to make it the way an engineer makes any argument worth listening to. I am going to show my working. I am not going to ask you to take any of what I say on faith. Faith is its own subject, and I will come to it in a moment, but the case I am making for the young reader at the kitchen table is not a case that depends on belief. It depends on evidence.

The evidence is the rest of this book.

The argument is simple, and I want to state it on this first page so that nobody who reads any further is in any doubt about it. **Your background is not your destiny. Your parents' occupation is not your occupation. The income that produced the house you grew up in is not the income that has to produce the house your own children grow up in.** None of those things are destiny. What is destiny, in any working life I have ever observed at close range, is the combination of three quiet things that almost nobody talks about when they are talking about success.

The first is a decision taken early enough to compound. The second is a mindset that refuses to let circumstance set the ceiling. The third is a work ethic that is focused, repeatable and frankly a little stubborn. Those three things, in combination, will beat the inheritance of poverty in nine cases out of ten, and they will do it without any of the noise that we have come to associate with ambition.

I made my decision when I was sixteen. I had just been signed on as an apprentice Boilermaker at Sub Nigel Gold Mine. My father had been at Sub Nigel for most of his working life. My older brother had blazed the apprenticeship trail in front of me. The whole community I lived in had a quiet, gravitational expectation that I would do what the men around me had done — go underground, do my time, raise a family on a miner's wage, and eventually be carried to the surface for the last time on the same cage that had carried me down for the first. I made a different decision. I never said it out loud at the kitchen table. I did not announce it at the church on a Sunday. I made it privately, somewhere between the trade test workshop and the mother who was waking me up before dawn to study, and the decision was this: I would learn everything the mine could teach me, I would accept every certificate it was prepared to pay for, and I would not, under any circumstances, make the underground my life.

That decision, taken in private and kept in private for years, ended up shaping the next fifty-seven years of my working life. It moved me from apprentice to Boilermaker, from Boilermaker to Mechanical Engineering Diploma, from the mine to AECI in Sasolburg, and from AECI to Adcock Ingram, where I would spend fourteen years and finish as a director. It moved me to Kolosus in Port Elizabeth as a Managing Director in my early forties. It moved me to SA Oil Mills, where I wrote the first version of what I now call my turnaround blueprint. It moved me through Tiger Brands, through BEVCO, and into the late-career contract work I am still doing now. None of that happened because I was unusually clever. It happened because a sixteen-year-old at a kitchen table made a quiet decision and then did not, in the fifty-seven years that followed, let himself off the hook for it.

## What the book is not

I should say what this book is not, because I have read a great many memoirs by businessmen and most of them have been about the wrong things.

This is not a chronicle of titles. There are titles in it because the work happened inside organisations that hand them out, but the titles are not the point. They are the receipts at the end of certain stretches of work, not the work itself.

This is not a book of management secrets. There are no secrets. I have run plants for half a century and I have never once encountered a problem that was solved by a secret. Problems get solved by people who have been treated with enough respect to want to solve them, working inside a clear plan, with a manager who is on the floor often enough to know what is actually happening on it. That is not a secret. It is, if anything, the opposite of a secret. It is the most public thing about management, and it has been hiding in plain sight for as long as there have been plants to run.

This is not a self-help book either, although I have been told by a few early readers that it occasionally reads like one. If it reads that way, it is only because the lessons of a long working life cannot help but sound like advice when they are written down. I am not standing on a stage. I am writing on the assumption that you have picked this book up because something in the title — _From Apprentice Boilermaker to Managing Director_ — caught you, and that you would like to know how the distance between those two job titles was actually closed by one ordinary person who started without any of the obvious advantages.

## Two threads you will see running through every chapter

There are two threads that I want to name on this first page, because once I have named them you will see them in every chapter that follows, and it will save us both some time.

The first is a leadership philosophy that I will, in a later chapter, set out in full. It has a name. I call it **Trust and Inspire**, and I deliberately set it against the older school that I grew up under, which was Command and Control. I will explain in due course what I mean by both. For now it is enough to say that every plant I have ever turned around, every loss-making operation I have ever returned to profit, and every team I have ever watched grow into something larger than the sum of the people in it, has been turned, returned and grown on the same two ideas. Trust the people closest to the work. Inspire them toward a shared goal. The rest of management, in my honest experience of fifty-seven years of it, is footnotes.

The second thread is faith. I am a Christian. I have been one since long before I had a business card to put it on, and I will be one long after the last business card is in a drawer. I am not going to use this book to convert anyone, and I am not going to use it to argue theology either. What I will do, because honesty requires it, is tell you when faith was load-bearing in a decision I made, and I will not pretend it was load-bearing when it was not. My wife Antonette has been the steadier Christian of the two of us for fifty-one years of marriage, and the household we raised four children in was a household whose Sundays were not negotiable. That fact has shaped this book in ways that even I cannot completely separate from the rest of the engineering.

If you are the sixteen-year-old at the kitchen table, you do not need to share my faith to find the rest of this book useful. The argument about background and destiny works whether you pray or not. I am telling you faith is in the book because it would be dishonest of me to write a foreword that left it out. If you are the sixty-year-old with the calendar thinning, you already know whether faith is going to be load-bearing for you in the years that are left. I will not press the point. I will simply let it show up in the chapters where it actually showed up in my life.

## A note to the younger reader

If you take only one sentence from this book and carry it with you out into the next forty years of your working life, let it be this. **Anything is possible.** Not as a slogan on a poster. As a statement of evidence, written by a man who started underground and is finishing above it, who was raised on a miner's wage and who has, in the seventy-three years since, never once been able to find a ceiling that his background was supposed to have put on him.

There are ceilings. I have hit some of them. They are not, in my experience, where the adults at the kitchen table told you they would be. They are higher up than that, and they are softer than that, and most of them give way under a steady, polite, well-prepared push.

Push.

I am going to spend the rest of this book showing you how.
