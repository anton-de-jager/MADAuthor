# Faith, Family and the AI Age: Life Learnings and What Comes Next

There is a temptation, when a man sits down to write the last chapter of a book about his working life, to make the closing pages sound grander than the man himself. I am not going to do that. I am seventy-three. I have been married to Antonette for fifty-one years. We have four children, nine grandchildren and three great-grandchildren. I still read my bible most mornings, I still go to the gym on a regular basis, and I still answer the phone when a business that needs transforming asks whether I have the time. Those four sentences, taken together, are the truest summary I can give of what has actually mattered. Everything else in this book has been the scaffolding underneath them.

I want to use this final chapter to braid the three threads that have run, mostly quietly, behind the trade tests and the turnarounds and the leather fairs in Paris. The first is the family that has been the foundation of every contract I have ever signed. The second is the explicit Christian faith that has anchored the decisions no boardroom could decide for me. The third is the leadership question sitting on my desk this year, and on yours, whether you are forty or sixty or twenty-two — what does it mean to lead in the age of artificial intelligence, when the technology in front of us is shifting faster than any new machine I ever commissioned, and the people behind us still need exactly the same thing they have always needed, which is to be seen, trusted and inspired.

I will close, as I have closed every chapter of my career, with a high note and a clean office.

## Antonette: the foundation under everything

I have written, in earlier chapters, about contracts and capex and exports. I have written about the German foreman in Dusseldorf, the leather fairs in Paris, the candy lines in East London, the peanut butter raw-material problem at Black Cat, and the seventy-three-year-old who is still being asked to lead Business Transformation contracts. None of those pages would have an author behind them if not for one woman.

Antonette and I have been married for fifty-one years. That is not a number I write lightly. Fifty-one years is more than two-thirds of my life. It is older than democratic South Africa. It is older than the company that first sent me to Germany, older than the apprentice who became a Boilermaker, and older than every job title that ever appeared on a business card with my name on it. The marriage came first chronologically, and it has come first in every important sense ever since.

I have said in earlier chapters that on the nights I was home, I was home — briefcase in the study, phone on silent, eyes at the dinner table. Let me expand that, for the younger reader, into something a little more practical. A long marriage, especially a marriage stretched across the contract life I lived, is not held together by anniversaries. It is held together by a thousand small, undramatic decisions taken in the middle of ordinary weeks, when no one is watching — and almost all of them are decisions to put a person ahead of a problem. The packing line will be there in the morning. Antonette will be there in the morning too, but only if you have, in the years before that morning, treated her as the senior partner she actually is.

We raised four children together. Each of them has, in their own way, surprised me. You have already met my youngest in passing in the earlier sport chapter — my son Anton, who was chosen to play squash for South Africa and became a prefect at his high school. The other three have built their own lives, each with the same quiet, undramatic work ethic that I would like to think Antonette and I tried to model. The arithmetic now stands at nine grandchildren and three great-grandchildren, and the family WhatsApp group on a Sunday evening produces a level of noise and good news that, frankly, makes a board call feel quiet by comparison.

I want to say something here that I have not, perhaps, said often enough in print or in person. The career you have read about in this book is, in any honest accounting, half hers. The years I was away in Port Elizabeth while two of our grown children stayed behind in Johannesburg; the years I was driving back and forth to East London for the confectionery contracts; the early mornings before flights to Munich and Boston and Solofra and Seoul; the late evenings at OEE screens in Tiger Brands — Antonette held the home steady through every one of them, without complaint, and without ever letting the children feel that their father had stepped back. Whatever transformation work I have been able to do in the boardroom is, in its quieter dimension, a transformation she made possible in the living room. The debt is not one I can repay in this lifetime. I can only acknowledge it, here, in the part of the book that is going to outlive both of us.

## The faith that anchored every decision

I grew up in a mine house on the East Rand, the youngest of five, with a devout Christian father who spent his life underground at Sub Nigel Gold Mine and a shy, industrious mother who held the home together on a miner's wage. Sunday church was not a debate in our home; it was the rhythm of the week. The bible was on the side table, and the side table was where the bible belonged. I have, by the grace of God, never lost that rhythm.

I am not, and have never been, a man who quotes scripture in a meeting to win an argument. I do not believe that is what scripture is for. But I will tell you, candidly, that the difficult decisions of my career — the retrenchment letters I had to sign at SA Oil Mills, the closure decisions at Kolosus, the wage negotiation that nearly broke down at midnight, the contract I turned away because the ethics did not sit right — were made with two columns on the page. The first column was the numbers. The second column was a quieter question: whether I would be at peace with this decision when I knelt down beside the bed at the end of the day. The numbers can be argued about. The second column cannot.

A few verses have done particular service over the decades. *Whatever you do, work at it with all your heart, as working for the Lord, not for human masters* — that one, from Colossians, has been with me since the apprentice mornings at Sub Nigel, when my mother woke me before dawn to study and I did not always feel like opening the book. It is the same verse I quote, gently, to a young engineer who tells me he is just doing his job. There is no such thing as just doing your job. There is doing your job with all your heart, or there is doing it badly. The middle position does not exist.

*Trust in the Lord with all your heart, and lean not on your own understanding* has been the other steady companion, particularly in the seasons when the right next step was not visible. Every major transition in this career — the AECI leap from the mine, the Adcock decision after a casual Sunday-newspaper job application, the Kolosus headhunting call at forty-two, the move into Business Transformation contracts in the third act — has been taken at a moment when the path ahead was not obvious. I have learned, slowly, that the path becomes obvious only one step into it. You have to be willing to take the step before the visibility arrives. That is what faith, in working clothes, actually looks like.

And then there is the verse that has done the most work in the negotiation rooms — *do unto others as you would have them do unto you*. It is the oldest line in the book, and it remains the most useful management principle I have ever found. The shop steward across the table, the contractor whose schedule you are tightening, the manager you are letting go on Friday afternoon, the supplier whose price you are testing — all of them, without exception, deserve the same treatment you would want if the chair were swapped. I have signed industrial relations agreements I could have squeezed harder. I have left small margins on the table in supplier negotiations. I have, on at least three occasions in my career, written a personal reference for a person I had just retrenched. None of those decisions cost me a cent in the long arc. Every one of them came back, years later, in the form of a phone call, a reference, a contract, or a quiet recommendation in a room I was not in.

Faith, lived honestly, is not a marketing instrument. It is an operating system. It runs in the background of every decision, and the people around you can feel whether it is on or off, even if you never mention it.

## The leadership question of the AI age

I want to turn now to the question that, in 2026, is on the desk of every leader I speak to. The technology in front of us — large language models, autonomous agents, AI copilots embedded in every productivity tool — is moving faster than any new technology I have commissioned in a fifty-year working life. I have stood next to a German extrusion machine in 1980. I have signed off on a R200-million mogul line at Tiger Brands. I have lived through the introduction of ERP, of OEE, of FSSC, of Poka-Yoke as a daily discipline. I have seen change. This is not the same.

I have a phrase I have started using in the mentoring sessions I run with younger managers, and I will put it here so that you can quote it back to me when I am wrong. The leaders who use AI will replace the leaders who do not. That is not a prediction; it is already happening. A young manager who is fluent in a modern AI tool — who can interrogate a large data set, draft a board paper, summarise a hundred-page contract, build a financial model and stress-test it in an afternoon — has a leverage that the same manager, ten years ago, could not have dreamed of. The reverse is also true. A senior manager who refuses to learn the tool, who insists that this is something for the IT department or the next generation, is a senior manager who is choosing, by inaction, to be replaced.

I want to be very clear here, because I do not want to be misread. I am not saying that AI replaces the human leader. It does not. I am saying that AI replaces the human leader who refuses to use it, in favour of one who does. The difference is everything.

I have, at seventy-three, made a deliberate decision to learn. I use the tools. I draft, I summarise, I research, I prepare. I run my reading list through them. I ask them to argue against me. I sometimes do not like the answer, which is when I know the tool is doing what it is supposed to do. The same engineer who once learned to read a vibration trace on a cyanide reactor in Modderfontein is now learning to read the output of a model that can summarise three decades of pharma manufacturing standards in eight seconds. The instrument has changed. The discipline of learning to use the instrument is the same.

But — and I want to put this in capital letters in your mind, because it is the one warning I want to leave with the next generation — the discipline of being a *leader* in the AI age is not the same as the discipline of being a *user*. The two are different qualifications.

Let me be specific. A model can draft your retrenchment letter. It cannot sit across from the person who is receiving it. A model can summarise a wage negotiation position. It cannot read the room at one a.m. when the shop steward needs to save face. A model can build you a perfect performance scorecard. It cannot recognise Sipho the fitter at the Friday morning team meeting in a way that makes him stand a little taller for the next six months. A model can write you a board paper that sounds correct. It cannot tell you whether you would be at peace with the decision when you kneel down beside the bed.

Empathy. Integrity. The human touch. The capacity to be present with another person in a difficult conversation. The capacity to look a worker in the eye and tell him that you trust him. The capacity to celebrate success down to the shop floor, to give a senior manager wings to fly, to hang a *Work Smart and Have Fun* banner above the office door in Port Elizabeth and mean every word of it. None of that is going to come out of a model in 2026, or 2036, or in my lifetime. It is going to come out of a leader who has, in the years before the meeting, done the unglamorous, repeated, daily work of becoming the kind of person another person can trust.

The Trust-and-Inspire philosophy I have laid out earlier in this book — over Command-and-Control, with the Nadella turnaround at Microsoft as the proof — is not weakened by the arrival of AI. It is sharpened by it. The technical leverage of the tool means that the only durable differentiator left for a senior leader is the quality of the relationship with the team. Everything technical can be automated. Almost nothing relational can be. If you are a young manager reading this, that is the most practical piece of career advice I can give you. Learn the tool until you are dangerous with it. Then put the tool down and learn to be present in a room. The combination of the two — fluency in the model, and presence with the people — is what the next generation of managing directors is going to look like.

## Key Focus Areas and Life Learnings — a final distillation

Over the years I have kept a quiet list of what I would call my Key Focus Areas — the principles I would want a young manager to take with them out of a mentoring session. I have referred to most of them in passing in earlier chapters. Let me put them in one place, in plain language, for the reader who has come to the back of the book and wants the recipe on a single page.

- **Make the early decision and stay with it.** I decided, at sixteen, that the mine would be a place I started from and not a place I stayed in. That decision shaped fifty-seven years. Whatever your equivalent is, make it early and refuse to renegotiate it.
- **Under-promise and over-deliver, every time.** Thirty days early in Dusseldorf saved roughly R30 million in 1980s rand. The habit, repeated across a career, becomes a reputation. The reputation, repeated across a career, becomes a phone that keeps ringing.
- **Structure follows strategy.** Do not reorganise before you know what you are reorganising for. I have walked into businesses that had been through three restructurings in two years and still did not know what they were trying to be.
- **Hire for attitude, train for skill.** Qualifications tell you what a person has done. Attitude tells you what they will do. I have hired more people on the second than the first, and I have rarely regretted it.
- **Use Pareto. Always.** Twenty percent of the root causes will, almost without exception, account for eighty percent of the loss. Spend your weeks on the twenty.
- **Celebrate success down to the shop floor.** A small word of recognition to a fitter is worth more than a long memo from a director. Sipho will remember the handshake long after the bonus letter has been filed.
- **Industrial relations is not a side function — it is the function.** Every senior manager will, in the fullness of a career, learn this the hard way. Learn it the easy way, in your thirties, by sitting in on every negotiation you can.
- **Finish on a high note.** Walk out of every company with the standards higher than when you arrived, with the people better trained, and with a clean office. You may never set foot in the building again. Or, more often than you think, you may walk back in on a contract ten years later.
- **Relationships compound.** The colleague you treat well in your thirties is the colleague who will recruit you in your sixties. There is no other asset class in a career that compounds at that rate.
- **Lead with Trust-and-Inspire, not Command-and-Control.** People will give you their hands for a salary. They will give you their heads and their hearts only for a leader they trust.
- **Stay healthy.** The body and the mind are one balance sheet. You cannot run a complex factory if you cannot run yourself.
- **Read your bible. Or whatever your equivalent is.** Have a source of authority outside the spreadsheet, against which you measure the difficult decisions.

That, as plainly as I can put it, is the list. None of it is original. All of it has been tested.

## The unique-life-moments list

I want, briefly, to come back to a small, undeclared list I have kept in my head over the years — what I called, in the earlier sport chapter, my list of unique life moments. It is not a brag list. It is a list of moments that would have astonished the sixteen-year-old apprentice who was riding the cage down at Sub Nigel on his first morning underground.

The day Mr Mandela accepted a cheque for his seventy-fifth birthday celebrations in Port Elizabeth, with me standing in the small group of business leaders who had organised it, is on that list. To hand a cheque to the man who had walked out of Victor Verster eight years earlier and asked an entire country to choose forgiveness over the easier emotions — that is a moment a miner's son does not forget. The 1995 Rugby World Cup final at Ellis Park, where I sat with my management team as managing director and watched Francois Pienaar lift the Webb Ellis Cup from a President in a Springbok jersey, is on that list. The afternoon I stood in the centre of the field at Murrayfield in Scotland is on that list, for reasons that have less to do with rugby and more to do with how unlikely my life had looked from a mine house on the East Rand. The mornings I have spent at home with grandchildren on my lap are also on that list, undeclared, alongside the cheques and the cups.

When I look at the list now, I see one consistent thread. Every moment on it was made possible by a decision taken earlier, often much earlier, by a younger version of myself who had no idea the moment was coming. That is the only secret I have ever found to a life of unique moments. You cannot plan them. You can only build, in the quiet years, the kind of life into which they eventually arrive.

## A word from the people I worked with

The testimonials that have come in over the years, from managers and shopfloor staff and shop stewards and chairmen, have humbled me more than any title ever did. I will not quote them at length here — they belong to the people who wrote them, and to the contracts under which we worked together. But I want to share, in summary, what they tend to have in common.

They almost never mention the financial outcome. They almost never mention the capex projects or the OEE improvements. What they mention is the way the work felt while it was being done. They mention being trusted. They mention being given wings to fly. They mention the *Work Smart and Have Fun* banner. They mention the senior manager who learned their name on day one, the engineer who stayed back to walk them through a procedure, the director who came to the shop floor on a Friday morning to hand-shake the team after a difficult week. They mention, in short, the things no model can do.

That is the legacy I would most like to leave on the record. Not the cheques, not the cups, not the company logos. The way it felt to work with us.

## What comes next

I do not know what comes next. I say that with the same curiosity I felt as a sixteen-year-old apprentice at Sub Nigel, walking into the first morning of the trade and wondering, quietly and privately, just how far this might go. The clipboard has been many things since then — a German specification, a packing line drawing, a Paris leather negotiation, a chemical plant in Sasolburg, a peanut butter procurement spec, a porridge factory in Durban. The next clipboard may very well be an AI dashboard on a laptop, with a remote first conversation with a board I have not yet met. That is fine with me. The work is the same.

What I do know is what I would like the closing principle of this book to be, for the young reader and the older reader alike. Anything is possible. That is the opening line of the foreword, and it is the closing line of the epilogue, and it is the only sentence I would want underlined on the inside cover of every copy that leaves the printer.

Anything is possible for the person who decides early, works consistently, builds relationships, stays healthy, stays faithful, learns the new instrument when it arrives, and refuses to let the ladder they are currently on tell them what ladder they are allowed to climb to. That sentence is not a piece of motivational rhetoric. It is a working description of a fifty-seven-year career, written by a man who started in a mine house on the East Rand and is, at seventy-three, still being asked to lead.

The Lord has given me a faithful wife, four children, nine grandchildren, three great-grandchildren, fifty-one years of marriage, a body of experience that, by His grace, still has something to offer, and the privilege of having spent a working life in the company of decent, hard-working South Africans.

The cake has long since been eaten, the balloons have long since come down, and the phone, for what it is worth, is still ringing.

I am going to keep answering it.

And to the apprentice Boilermaker reading this in his overalls on a Sunday afternoon, with his trade test ahead of him and the rest of his life ahead of him after that, I would like to say one last thing, in the plainest English I can muster.

The ladder is yours. Climb it.
