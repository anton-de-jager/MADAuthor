# Learning the Numbers

There is a moment, in any working life, when you realise that the thing you are now being paid to do is no longer the thing you were trained to do. For me that moment did not arrive in a meeting and it did not arrive on a payslip. It arrived in a small office at the end of a long week, with a costing sheet in front of me, a pencil in my hand, and the slow, embarrassing realisation that I did not really know what most of the lines on the page meant.

I had been a tradesman for years by then. I had been a supervisor. I had been through retrenchments and bad weather and at least one project that had gone backwards faster than any of us had thought possible. I knew the floor in a way that no qualification could ever fully describe. I knew which weld looked right under torch-light at half past four on a Friday. I knew what tone of voice a foreman uses when he has decided not to argue with you about something he is going to do anyway. I could read a fabrication drawing in a glance and tell you, within a kilogram, what it was going to weigh when it came off the table.

What I could not tell you, with anything like the same confidence, was what it was going to cost. Not really. Not in the way the men with the company cars and the cleaner shoes could tell you. I could give you a number. The men I worked for could give you a number and then explain, slowly and patiently, what was inside it, what had been left out, what had been carried over from somewhere else, and what the company would still own at the end of the job if everything went the way they expected. That second kind of answer was a different language. I had been on the floor long enough to understand that, on the wrong morning, that language was the only one that actually mattered.

This chapter is about teaching myself, in my thirties, the language I had been working around for fifteen years without ever properly learning. It is about costing, margins, contracts, scheduling, the difference between *busy* and *profitable*, and the difference between an opinion strongly held and a position that can be defended on paper. It is also, in the end, an argument. The argument is this. **Management without numeracy is just opinion in a clean shirt.** I have seen too many otherwise capable men promoted out of the workshop and then quietly defeated by a spreadsheet they were too proud to ask anyone to explain to them. I did not want to be one of them. I had to put down the grinder, in a sense I had not yet really understood, and pick up something else.

## The day I noticed I did not know

The honest place to start is the day I first noticed.

There was a job. The exact job does not matter, because the lesson would have arrived eventually whether it had been that job or one of three others I could have picked instead. It was a medium-sized fabrication contract, the kind that should have been straightforward, with a delivery window that had a little bit of slack in it and a customer we had worked with before. We won the work on a quote that felt comfortable. We started the work on a Monday. We finished the work, near enough on time, four weeks later.

In the meantime, the floor had done what a good floor does. The men had not complained much. The cutting had been clean. The welds had passed inspection on the first walk-through. The truck had left the yard with the right load on it and the right paperwork in the cab. From any angle you would have looked at on a Friday afternoon, that job was a success.

When the costing came back, it had made almost no money.

Not a loss. We had not lost on it. We had, in the language the directors used at the time, *broken about even, plus a little something for the diary.* That phrase, said in the tone in which it was said, was meant to be a kindness. It was meant to make it clear that I had not done anything wrong. The men had not done anything wrong. The job had been delivered the way we said it would be delivered. The numbers had simply, quietly, not added up to what we had thought they would when we sent the quote out.

I remember sitting with that piece of paper for a long time. The director who had given it to me had moved on to something else. The kettle was on somewhere down the corridor. I had a pencil in my hand and a feeling in the chest that I would learn, much later, to recognise as the feeling of being in a room you have not earned the right to be in.

The thing on the page was not a mystery in any deep sense. The lines were in English. The numbers were the kind of numbers I added up every day. **But I did not know which of them I could move and which I could not.** I did not know which were assumptions that someone had typed in three years ago and which were laws of the universe. I did not know which had been pushed around to make the bottom line look better and which had been left alone out of habit. I did not know, in any honest way, why this particular job had not made the money the quote suggested it should make.

I had been, the whole time, in the wrong relationship with the page. I had treated the costing the way an apprentice treats a fabrication drawing in his first month — as a fact, handed down by someone who knew more than him, to be obeyed rather than questioned. The drawing, of course, you eventually learn to read with one eye half closed, checking the welder's notes against your own understanding of the joint and the steel and the heat. You stop being afraid of it. The costing sheet was still, to me, a drawing in my first month.

That afternoon, sitting in that office, I made a small private decision that I think is the actual starting point of any management worth the name. I decided I was going to stop pretending I understood things I did not understand. The cost of that pretence, over a working life, is enormous. It is paid in worse decisions, in lost authority, and in the quiet erosion of trust by people who can see you bluffing and have the manners not to mention it. I was going to learn the page. Not in a course. Not by reading a book on a beach. By taking one job at a time, one line at a time, until the language stopped being foreign.

## The four languages of a business

It took me longer than I would now admit to notice that what I was actually doing, in those evenings with the costing sheets and the calculator, was learning four different languages at once. They are related. They are not the same. A manager, if he is going to be useful, has to be at least conversant in all four of them.

The first language is **the cost of the work**. This is the one most people from the trade think they already understand, because they have spent their lives near it. They are wrong, almost without exception, but the mistake is forgivable. If you have been on the floor, you know what a length of steel costs to the meter, more or less. You know roughly what an electrode costs in a box of two hundred. You know that a man on a grinder for eight hours is not the same expense as a man on a CNC for the same eight hours, and you know that a forklift in the yard is not free. That feels like cost. It is not. It is the visible part of cost. It is the part you can point to.

The invisible part is where most jobs go quietly bad. There is the cost of the shed itself, sitting there, with its roof and its lights and its rates and its insurance, and that cost does not care whether the floor is full or empty on a Wednesday. There is the cost of the foreman's time, the storeman's time, the bookkeeper's time, the time of the man who walked the job for ten minutes on Tuesday to check that something was happening. There is the cost of money — the fact that the steel was paid for in week one and the customer is going to pay you, if you are lucky, in week eight, and the difference between those two dates has a price tag whether you write it down or not. None of those costs sit on a drawing. All of them are real, and the company is paying them every day whether you bill them out or not.

The second language is **margin**. This is the relationship between what you charge and what you actually keep after you have paid for the work. I had spent years assuming that margin was a number the directors made up over lunch, and then the rest of us were supposed to chase it. That is a working-class reading of margin, and I do not blame the boy I was for having it. It is not quite right. Margin is, in honest practice, the small piece of room a business has to do anything other than survive the week. It is what pays for the next machine, the next training course, the cushion against the next bad year, the dividend if there is one, and the basic dignity of being able to put up wages when the time comes. A business with no margin is not running. It is being dragged along by inertia, and the moment the wind changes it stops.

The third language is **contracts**. By which I do not mean the document. I mean the actual deal — written and unwritten — between you and the people you have promised work to, and between you and the people who have promised you work. This is the one most floor-trained managers are weakest at, and I include the man I was at thirty in that. We tend to think of a contract as a piece of paper that lives in a drawer until something goes wrong. That is not what a contract is. **A contract is the shape of the risk you have agreed to carry, on behalf of your company, in exchange for the price the customer has agreed to pay.** If you have not read it carefully, you do not actually know which risks are yours and which are theirs. You think you do. The drawer thinks otherwise. The drawer wins every time.

The fourth language is **scheduling**, and this is the one that most surprised me, because I had always thought of scheduling as a clerical activity. It is not. Scheduling is where the other three languages get tested against reality. A job that is profitable on paper, with a clean contract and a healthy margin, can still ruin a quarter if it lands on the floor in the wrong week. A job that looks tight on margin can be the best money a workshop makes in a year if it fits cleanly into a gap nobody else can fill. Scheduling is the music. The other three are the notes. A manager who can read notes but cannot hear the music is going to keep being surprised by the way the orchestra sounds.

I am going to take each of those four in turn, and tell you, as honestly as I can, how I learned them. Not perfectly. Not academically. By the only method I had available to me, which was the method any man without the right education has available to him at that age: by sitting next to people who already knew, by asking questions that I was sometimes embarrassed to ask, and by paying close attention to the jobs in front of me until the patterns started to show.

## Costing, one job at a time

The first thing I did, after that quiet afternoon with the broken-even costing sheet, was ask a question that I should have asked years before.

I went to the bookkeeper.

This sounds, written down, like a small thing. It was not, at the time. The bookkeeper at that company was a woman in her fifties who had been there longer than any of the directors and who ran the back office with the same patient efficiency the foreman ran the floor. The unwritten rule in a workshop of that era was that the men in overalls and the woman with the ledgers occupied separate kingdoms. You did not bother her, and she did not bother you, and the company functioned in the small overlap where the foreman handed over a clipboard at the end of the week and she turned it into invoices on a Monday.

I went and asked her if she would, when she had time, sit with me for half an hour and walk me through what actually happened to a job once it left the floor. Not the philosophy. Not the policy. The arithmetic. Where did the numbers go. What did each line mean. Why were some of the lines fixed and some of them flexible. Why did some jobs make more money than others, when from the floor they looked the same.

She did not laugh at me. She gave me, instead, a small smile of recognition that I have since seen on the faces of several other women in finance offices in several other workshops, and which I now understand to mean *you are not the first man in overalls to come and ask me this, and you will not be the last, and I am glad it is finally your turn.*

She showed me, that afternoon, three jobs from the previous quarter. One had made good money. One had broken about even. One had quietly lost. From the floor they had looked, to me, indistinguishable. I had supervised parts of all three. I had thought, walking past, that they were the same kind of work. On the page, they were not the same at all.

She did not lecture me. She did the thing that good teachers in any field do, which is point and ask. *Look at that line. What do you think that is.* *What do you think the difference is between this column and that column.* *Why do you think this number is bigger here than there.* In about forty minutes, without anything that could honestly be called a course, she taught me more about how the business made money than I had picked up in a decade of being inside it.

The single biggest thing I learned that day, and which I have never forgotten, was this. **Most jobs that go quietly wrong do not go wrong on the floor. They go wrong on the quote.** They go wrong before the steel is cut, before the welder is rostered, before the foreman has even seen the drawing. They go wrong in the small assumptions that get made at the costing stage by someone who is not quite paying attention, and then they get baked into a number, and that number gets sent out under a letterhead, and from that moment on the floor is being asked to deliver something at a price the floor cannot actually deliver it for. The floor does its best. The men work hard. The job comes in on time. And then, weeks later, somebody sits down with a calculator and discovers that the money was lost before any of them had picked up a tool.

That changed how I looked at quotes for the rest of my working life. Where I had previously thought of a quote as a document that came from the office and arrived on the floor as instruction, I started to think of it, properly, as the most important moment in the whole job. **The quote is where the job is won or lost, financially, before any work has been done.** Everything afterwards is either making the most of a good quote or trying to soften the blow of a bad one.

I started, with the bookkeeper's quiet permission, to look at the quotes before they went out, for the kind of work I knew. Not to second-guess the people whose job it was to send them. To check, in the only way I could, that the assumptions underneath them matched the reality I had seen on the floor for the last decade. I would point at a line and ask the man who had written it where the number had come from. Sometimes he had a clean answer and we both went on with our day. Sometimes he did not, and the conversation was useful, and the next quote went out at a slightly different number.

It took maybe a year of that, done quietly, before I could read a costing sheet end to end without help. That was the first language.

## Margin, and the trap of busy

The second language, margin, took me longer than I expected, because I kept confusing it with two other things that look like it from a distance.

The first thing margin is not is **turnover**. Turnover is how much work has come through the door. It is a useful number, in its place. It is not, by itself, a sign of health. A workshop can run a record turnover and still be in serious trouble, if the work it ran was the wrong kind of work at the wrong kind of price. I have seen, more than once, directors stand up in front of a room and report a good turnover with a tone of voice that was warmer than the bank balance justified. The men listening felt better for hearing it. The bookkeeper, sitting at the back, did not.

The second thing margin is not is **busyness**. A floor that is full looks healthy. A foreman with no slack in his Monday looks competent. A schedule with no gaps in it looks like good management. None of those things are the same as margin. A floor can be full of work that is not making money. A foreman can be running himself ragged on jobs that should have been turned down. A schedule with no gaps in it can be a schedule with no room to take the better job when the better job arrives, and *the better job always arrives at the wrong moment*.

I had to be taught, by experience and by the quiet correction of older men, that the question to ask of a workshop is not *is it busy*. The question is **is it profitably busy**. There is a kind of busy that earns. There is a kind of busy that only burns. The first kind looks tired at the end of the week and goes home satisfied. The second kind looks tired at the end of the week and cannot quite say what it is that has been achieved.

Margin teaches you, slowly, to be willing to say no. That is, I think, the hardest single thing about it. The whole emotional architecture of a tradesman, by the time he becomes a manager, is built around saying yes. You take the job. You finish the job. You take the next one. Saying no, in any context, feels like failure of nerve. It is not. It is the most useful sentence the manager has at his disposal, when it is said for the right reasons.

You learn to say no to the customer who is, kindly but persistently, pushing your price down to a place where the work cannot be done properly. You learn to say no to the job that fits the floor but does not fit the year. You learn to say no to the temptation to fill an empty Wednesday with anything that walks in the door, when the cost of doing so is that you will be unable to take the better job that is going to walk in on Friday. **A manager who cannot say no is not really making decisions. He is just answering the phone.**

The way I taught myself to think about margin, in the end, was by imagining the year as a single very long job. At the end of it, the company has either made enough money to do the things it needs to do next year, or it has not. Every job that comes in is either contributing to that long job or eating into it. Some jobs contribute by being directly profitable. Some jobs contribute by keeping a customer warm who will come back with the right work later. Some jobs contribute by keeping a key skill in the building that you do not want to lose. And some jobs, which on a Friday afternoon look fine, are actually eating into next year without anyone noticing. The discipline is to be honest about which is which. The numbers help with that. They are not the whole of the honesty, but they are the part of it that cannot be argued with on a Monday morning.

## Reading a contract like a tradesman

The third language, contracts, was the one I was most afraid of, and for the longest the one I avoided.

Part of that was class. I had grown up around men who treated paperwork the way they treated weather — as something that happened to you, that you had no real influence over, and that you mostly had to endure. A contract was the kind of document that arrived in a courier envelope and was signed somewhere upstairs. The men in overalls did not read contracts. They executed them, in the literal sense of the word, by doing the work the contract had promised.

Part of it was vocabulary. The first contracts I tried to read in earnest were full of phrases that meant nothing to me, and a lot of them looked, from the outside, deliberately designed to keep me out. *Notwithstanding the foregoing.* *Time of the essence.* *Liquidated damages.* *Consequential loss.* *Force majeure.* I had spent fifteen years building a vocabulary around steel and electricity and human behaviour. I had to build a second one, in middle age, around risk and obligation and remedy.

I am told [unverified] that there are many good books written on the legal side of construction and fabrication contracts. I am sure they are useful. I did not read them, at the time. I did what I had always done with anything I did not understand. I went and found someone who knew, and I asked.

In this case it was the company's outside attorney, who came in once a month for half a day and who I had previously regarded with the same neutral distance I regarded the auditors. I asked his secretary, very politely, whether he would mind if I sat in on the part of his visit that was about contracts, and if afterwards he could spare twenty minutes to walk me through the document we had just been talking about, line by line, as if I were not a manager but a curious apprentice. I told her the truth, which was that I knew I needed to understand this and I did not.

He, like the bookkeeper, did not laugh. He gave me, instead, a slightly amused look that I have since seen on the faces of more than one professional who has been asked, late, by a working man, to explain a thing the working man should have had explained to him years earlier. He said yes. The first session was about an hour. There were maybe a dozen more over the next two years, scattered across the months.

The single most useful thing he taught me, and the thing I would urge any tradesman who finds himself in a manager's chair to learn early, is this. **The most important parts of a contract are not the ones written in big letters at the top. They are the ones written in small letters near the back.** The headline price is usually the thing the parties argue about most loudly. It is rarely the thing that decides whether the job ends well or badly. The job is decided by who carries which risk when the world does not behave the way the quote assumed it would. Who pays if the steel price moves. Who pays if the site is not ready on the day. Who pays if the customer asks for changes after the work has started. Who pays if a strike, a storm, or a supplier failure pushes the delivery date out. Who pays if the finished work, six months later, has a problem nobody anticipated.

Those clauses, read closely, tell you exactly what kind of job you are agreeing to. A contract that looks like a generous price can hide a structure that puts every conceivable bit of bad weather onto your side of the table. A contract that looks tight on price can, sometimes, hide a structure that lets you walk away cleanly when something goes wrong that is honestly not your fault. The number at the top is, in that sense, only ever half the story.

I started, after those sessions, to do something I had not done before. Before any job over a certain size, I would sit down for an hour with the contract and read it. Not skim. Not glance. Read. With a pencil. I would underline the things I did not understand and ask about them. I would underline the things I understood but did not like and raise them. I would underline the things that, in my reading, would put the company on the wrong side of a problem if a problem arrived. I was, as far as I was concerned, simply doing what the floor had taught me to do with a fabrication drawing. Treat the document as if it were going to be used. Trust nothing on it until you have understood it. Ask questions until the questions stop.

That habit, more than any single bit of cleverness I ever brought to a deal, kept the companies I worked for out of a number of bad outcomes. Not all of them. Nobody reads themselves out of every problem. But enough of them that I came to think of contract literacy as one of the basic, unsexy skills of management, in the same category as the foreman's ability to read a weld, or the tradesman's ability to listen for the change of note in a machine that is about to go wrong.

## Scheduling, or the music underneath the notes

The fourth language was scheduling, and it took me longest to hear.

I had been on enough floors by then to think I understood scheduling. I knew what a Gantt chart looked like. I knew what *critical path* meant, more or less. I had spent years being either the recipient of a schedule or, later, one of the men putting one together for the floor. I would have told you, with confidence, in my early thirties, that I knew how to schedule.

I did not. I knew how to draw a schedule. That is a different skill.

The thing I had to learn — and this is the bit nobody quite tells you in a course, if you ever go on one — is that a schedule is not a description of when work will happen. It is a series of bets about the future of a lot of independent things that you do not, in fact, control. The truck will arrive on Tuesday. The plates will be the right size. The customer's site will be ready. The welders will be there. The drawings will not change. Each of those is a bet. None of them is a certainty. The discipline of scheduling is the discipline of knowing which of your bets are safe, which of them are risky, and what you are going to do when, inevitably, two or three of them go against you in the same week.

A clean schedule, in a workshop of any size, is a beautiful object on a Friday afternoon. It is also, by Wednesday of the following week, almost always wrong in at least one important way. The question is not whether the schedule will survive contact with the world. It will not. The question is whether the manager who built it has built in the small bits of slack, the small bits of sequencing intelligence, the small overlaps and buffers, that let the floor absorb the shocks without panicking.

I learned scheduling, in the end, by doing it badly several times and watching what broke.

The first time I built a schedule that pleased me on paper, I scheduled the workshop to within an inch of its life. Every machine had a job on it every hour. Every welder had a queue behind him. Every truck had a slot. It looked, on the wall, like the kind of timetable a serious manager builds. It was. It was also brittle. The first time a delivery was a day late — and a delivery, in that industry, was always a day late at some point — the whole thing concertinaed. Jobs that should have been simple got rushed. Welders worked into the evening on the wrong work. The foreman had to make small decisions about which customer to disappoint, and the only criterion he had to go on was who had shouted last, which is not a criterion at all. We delivered. We delivered tired. The margin on three of the jobs that month was worse than it should have been, because we had paid for it in overtime and rework.

I learned, after a few months of that, to leave room in the week. Not lazy room. Not idle room. *Considered* room. A morning where the welder I trusted most was not booked, because if anything went wrong anywhere on the floor I was going to want him available. An afternoon a week where a machine that was due for maintenance was actually allowed to be maintained, instead of being squeezed in at month-end when it broke. A small, deliberate hesitation between two big jobs that needed the same fitter, so that one did not eat the other. **A schedule with no air in it is not a schedule. It is a fantasy.**

I also learned to look at the schedule as a sequence of *handovers* rather than as a sequence of *tasks*. The thing that most often goes wrong, in a workshop, is not a task. The men know how to do the tasks. The thing that goes wrong is the moment where one task finishes and another picks up, and a piece of information, or a piece of steel, or a piece of paperwork, does not quite make the jump cleanly. Once I started looking at the handovers — between the office and the floor, between the cutting bay and the welding bay, between the welding bay and the inspector, between the inspector and the loaders, between the loaders and the driver — I started to see where the lost hours actually lived. They were not in the work. They were in the spaces between the work.

That single shift in attention — from tasks to handovers — improved the throughput of the floor more than anything else I did in those years. I did not have to make any man work harder. I had, instead, to make the spaces between the men a little quieter, a little better lit, and a little better attended to. The men noticed. The numbers, eventually, noticed too.

## What the numbers did, and did not, change about me

It would be easy to write this chapter as if learning the numbers made me a different kind of man. It did not, really. I was still a tradesman by formation. I still walked the floor. I still kept a pair of boots in the back of the car. I still found, at the end of a long week of meetings, that the part of the day I most looked forward to was the half hour I would spend in the workshop after the office had emptied out, watching the last of the second shift finish a job under good light.

What the numbers did was change what I could honestly say in a room.

Before I had learned them, I could only ever speak in the room from one side of my experience. I could tell you, with authority, what the work would look like when it was done. I could tell you, with authority, what the men were and were not capable of. I could tell you, with authority, where a particular drawing was going to give us trouble and where it would go through clean. Those were not small things. They were, in fact, the things a lot of the people in the room could not say at all. But they were also, on their own, not enough.

After I had learned the numbers, I could sit at the same table and say something quite different. I could say *yes, we can do that, and here is what it will cost us, and here is what we will need to charge to do it properly, and here is the part of the risk that will come back to bite us in month six if we are not careful with this clause.* I could say *no, we cannot take that job at that price, and here is why, in numbers, not in feeling.* I could say *I would rather we did this smaller job, on these terms, than that bigger one, on those terms, and here is the arithmetic that tells you why.*

That change, in what I could say, changed the rooms I was allowed into. It also, more importantly, changed the weight of what I said in the rooms I had already been allowed into. **A man who can describe both the work and the money carries twice the authority of a man who can only describe one of them.** I had not understood that, at thirty. I understood it, slowly, through my thirties. I have not forgotten it since.

There is a temptation, when you finally learn this kind of thing, to overcorrect. To start treating the numbers as if they were the whole of management. To walk into a workshop with a spreadsheet under your arm and lecture the men about cost per hour. I have seen managers who came up that way, who arrived in the seat through a finance route rather than a trade route, and the failure mode is the mirror image of the one I have been describing. They can describe the money perfectly. They cannot describe the work. The men know, within a day, that they cannot. They listen politely. They do what they were going to do anyway. Authority leaks out of the office under the door and pools quietly on the floor where the foreman picks it up.

The point of learning the numbers, if you come from the trade, is not to stop being from the trade. It is to add a second instrument to the one you already play. The trade gives you the right to be heard. The numbers give you the right to be believed when you say something about the future of the business, rather than the present of the bench. **You need both.**

## A short list, for the desk drawer

If you are the kind of reader who likes to be able to find the practical part of a chapter again on a long Sunday afternoon, here is what I wish someone had handed me on the day I sat with that broken-even costing sheet.

- **Go and ask the bookkeeper.** Whoever, in your business, actually understands where the money goes. Go and ask her, plainly, to walk you through one job, one line at a time. You will learn more in an hour with her than in a year of staring at summary reports.
- **Read a quote like a drawing.** Before any job of any size leaves your desk, treat the costing the way you would treat a fabrication drawing on the first morning. Trust nothing on it until you have understood it. The job is won or lost here, before any tool has been picked up.
- **Separate turnover from margin.** A full floor is not the same as a profitable floor. The question to ask of a workshop is not *is it busy* but **is it profitably busy**. Learn to feel the difference in the diary, not just on the page.
- **Practise saying no.** Saying no is not a failure of nerve. It is the most useful sentence the manager has, when it is used for the right reasons. Say no to bad price. Say no to wrong work. Say no to the empty Wednesday that will cost you the better Friday.
- **Sit down with the contract.** Read it. Pencil in hand. Underline what you do not understand and ask. The headline price is half the story. The small letters near the back are where the risk actually lives.
- **Watch the handovers, not just the tasks.** Most lost hours, in a workshop, are not in the work. They are in the spaces between the work. Look at the seams. Light them properly. The throughput will follow.
- **Build air into the schedule.** A schedule with no slack is not a schedule, it is a fantasy. A good morning unbooked, a known buffer between two big jobs, a machine allowed to be serviced before it breaks. The room you leave in the week is the room the week will need.
- **Add the numbers to your trade, do not replace it.** The trade gives you the right to be heard on the floor. The numbers give you the right to be believed in the boardroom. Carry both. The men who only carry one of them are limited in ways they cannot always see.
- **Treat your own ignorance as a problem to be solved, not a fact to be hidden.** The cost of pretending to understand things you do not understand, over a working life, is enormous. Asking the small embarrassing question early is one of the cheapest investments you will ever make.

I will tell you what comes next. Having learned, in those years, to speak both languages — the language of the work and the language of the money — I started to find myself in rooms with people I had not previously known how to deal with. Other managers. Other departments. People above me and people alongside me, with their own priorities, their own pressures, and their own ways of measuring whether a thing had gone well. The trade had taught me how to lead the men under me. The numbers had taught me how to think about the business as a whole. What I had not yet learned was how to be useful sideways and upwards in an organisation — how to manage the people who were not, in any formal sense, mine to manage. That is the work the next stretch of the story is about.
