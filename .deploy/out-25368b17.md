# Hard Years, Lean Years

There is a particular kind of morning that you only learn about after you have lived through a few bad years. It is not the morning of the disaster. The morning of the disaster has its own awful clarity. You know what has happened. You know roughly what to do, even if you do not yet know how. Adrenaline arrives uninvited and gets you to lunchtime.

The mornings I am talking about come later. They are the ones in the middle of a long, grinding stretch when nothing is on fire and nothing is being rebuilt, when the order book is thin and the bank is patient but not friendly, and the people on the floor have started asking, very politely, whether the rumours are true. Those mornings do not announce themselves. They just arrive, one after the other, for months. You wake up. You drink the same cup of coffee you drank yesterday. You drive in. And somewhere on the road you realise that the job today is, again, just to keep the thing standing for one more day, and that nobody is going to congratulate you for it because most of them will not even know you did it.

That is the work this chapter is about. Not the moment of the storm. The long, dull weather afterwards, when the storm has gone but the damage has not, and the people around you need you to behave as if you still have a plan.

When I left the bays for the clipboard, I had thought the hard part was learning to stop using my hands and start using my voice. The hard part, I would discover in the years that followed, was something quite different. It was the weather of the industry itself, and what it asked of a man with a clipboard when the wind turned and refused to turn back for a long time.

## What a lean year actually looks like

I want to start with a description, because the phrase **lean year** can mean almost anything. People throw it around in boardrooms with the same casualness that they throw around **challenging environment** or **headwinds**, and the words have become so polished that they have stopped doing any useful work.

A lean year, in the kind of business I was working in, looked like this.

The phone rang less. That was the first sign, before any of the numbers caught up. You did not need a spreadsheet to feel it. The reception area was quieter. The reps were back earlier. The quotes you sent out did not turn into orders as quickly, and the ones that did came back with the price line circled and a small, polite question mark next to it.

Then the existing work started to behave differently. Clients who had always paid in thirty days started paying in forty-five, then sixty. Not because they were dishonest. Because their phones had also gone quiet. The whole chain was breathing in at the same time. Cash that had moved through the business like water through a pipe started moving through it like syrup through a straw.

Then, after a month or two of that, the conversations changed. Suppliers phoned to **check in**, which in that industry meant *we have noticed you are stretching us and we are politely asking if we should worry*. The bank manager, who had previously been a face you saw at year-end, started suggesting coffee. The auditors became, very slightly, more thorough. None of it was hostile. All of it was a small tightening.

And then, finally, you started looking at the wage bill on a Friday and doing arithmetic in your head, about other human beings' lives, that no human being should have to do.

That is a lean year. Not one bad day. A slow, steady compression of the room you have to move in, until the decisions in front of you stop being about growth or strategy and start being about survival, dignity, and time.

## The temptation to perform

The first thing that happens to a manager in a lean year, if they are not careful, is that they start to perform optimism.

I do not mean lying. Most of the people I respected did not lie about the state of the business when it was bad. But there is a softer, more dangerous thing than lying, which is the habit of putting a small, professional smile on every conversation about it. *We are tight, but we are managing.* *It is a difficult quarter, but we have plans.* *We are leaner now and that is a good thing.* All of those sentences can be true. All of them can also be a costume.

The reason performing optimism is dangerous is not that your team will see through it. They will, but that is not the worst of it. The worst of it is that you start to believe it yourself. You repeat the line about being **leaner and stronger** so many times in so many meetings that, somewhere around the eighth or ninth repetition, the part of you that was honestly worried goes quiet, and you stop hearing the small, useful alarms that were telling you the truth.

A leader who cannot hear their own alarms in a lean year is a leader who is going to make a confident, well-presented decision in about six weeks that they would have refused outright in a good year. I have made some of those decisions. I have watched other people make them. They almost always come dressed up as **bold moves**, and they almost always cost more than they save.

So the first habit of a lean year, before any of the practical ones, is to keep your own hearing intact. You can be calm in public. You should be. The room takes its temperature from you, and a panicked principal makes a panicked team. But in private, with the door closed and the notebook open, you have to let yourself say the thing out loud. *This is bad. I am worried about this. I do not yet know what to do about that.* If you cannot say those sentences to yourself, you cannot think clearly about them, and you will end up managing the appearance of the problem rather than the problem.

## The arithmetic nobody trains you for

There is a specific kind of arithmetic that a manager has to do in a lean year, and almost nobody is trained for it.

It is the arithmetic of people.

In a good year, the numbers and the people are aligned. A new order comes in, you put a person on it, the order pays the person, the person produces work that wins the next order. The line goes one way. The arithmetic is mostly addition.

In a lean year, the line stops. You have, on one side of the page, a wage bill that does not shrink on its own. On the other side, a revenue line that has. Between the two there is a gap, and the gap is measured in months of cash. You count the months. You count them again. You count them at three in the morning when you should be asleep. And you start to realise that, at some point, the arithmetic will require subtraction. Of people.

I want to say something carefully here, because I think the way most business books talk about this is dishonest.

A retrenchment is not a strategic decision. It is a failure with a paper trail.

I do not mean that every retrenchment is the fault of the person making it. Sometimes markets collapse. Sometimes a single client folds and takes a third of your revenue with them on a Tuesday afternoon. Sometimes a government changes the rules of the game and a sector that fed thousands of families becomes uneconomic in a quarter. You can be a faultless manager and still have to look at that page and do that subtraction. The world is not always fair to people who have done everything right.

But the act itself, of telling a human being that the work they came to on Monday will not be there on Friday, is a failure in the system somewhere. Maybe not in you. Maybe in the market, or the economy, or the chain of decisions that got the business to this point. But it is a failure. And calling it a **strategic restructure** does not change what it is. It only protects the language used by the people who do not have to do it themselves.

The reason I want to insist on the word is that it changes how you behave in the meeting. If you walk into a retrenchment meeting believing you are making a strategic decision, you will treat the person across the desk as a line item. If you walk in knowing that you are managing a failure, you will treat them as a person who is being asked to absorb some of the cost of something that was not, in any meaningful sense, their fault. Those are two different meetings. Only one of them lets the person walk out with their dignity intact.

## The first time I had to let people go

I am not going to tell you a sanitised version of this.

The first time I had to oversee a real reduction in headcount, I was younger than I should have been for the job, and I had not slept properly for about three weeks. The numbers had been getting worse for two quarters. We had tried the obvious things first, the way you are supposed to: we cut overtime, we paused recruitment, we renegotiated with suppliers, we sent the directors back to clients with sharpened pencils to win work at thinner margins than anyone wanted. None of it had been enough. The gap had not closed. The bank had been patient and had stopped being patient. The decision was no longer whether. It was how many, and who, and how soon.

I remember sitting at my desk with the list in front of me. It was not a long list, by the standards of these things. It was long enough.

I had known most of the names for years. Some of them I had hired myself. Two of them had been at my wedding. One had taken me aside, when I was an apprentice, and shown me a small trick with a torch that had probably saved my hands. They were not, on that piece of paper, abstractions. They were the people my working life had been built around.

I did three things, in those days, that I still think were right. I want to put them down here, because I have seen too many managers go into that meeting with no idea what they were doing, and the cost of getting it wrong does not fall on them.

The first thing I did was refuse to delegate the conversations.

There is a tradition, in some companies, of having HR do the actual telling. The line manager sits in the room as moral support. The script comes from a folder. The handshake at the end is from a stranger. I understand why companies do this. It is more efficient, and it protects the line manager from a conversation they do not want to have. It is also, in my view, a small act of cowardice that the people on the other side of the desk feel for the rest of their lives. They do not forget who told them. They do not forget who could not look at them while it was being said.

So I told them myself. Each one. In a room I had booked for the purpose. With water on the table, and time on the clock, and nothing scheduled after the meeting so that whoever needed to sit there for an hour afterwards could sit there.

The second thing I did was tell them the truth about why.

Not the press-release version. The actual version. *The order book has dropped by this much. The cost base is this. The gap is here. We have done these things to try to close it and they have not been enough. Your role is one of the ones that goes, and the reasons it is yours and not someone else's are these, and I am willing to walk you through the reasoning if you want me to.* Some of them did want me to. Most did not. But the offer was there, because the offer is what tells a person that they are still being treated as an adult.

A retrenched person who understands why is angry but oriented. A retrenched person who is fobbed off with corporate language is angry and adrift, and they go home with a story that will sour for years. The cost of telling the truth, in that meeting, is about forty minutes of your time and a great deal of discomfort. The cost of not telling it is paid by the other person, for much longer than that.

The third thing I did was stay available for a while afterwards.

Not for everyone. Some of them did not want to see me again, and that was their right. But for the ones who did, I made it clear that the reference call was real, the introductions were real, the cup of coffee in three months was real. Not as charity. As an acknowledgement that we had worked together, that the work had been good, and that the ending of the employment was not the ending of the respect.

I will not pretend this fixed anything. It did not. People still lost incomes they needed. Families still had hard months. Marriages were strained. One man, whose face I still see sometimes, took it harder than I knew at the time and disappeared from the trade for a year. I think about him.

But within what was possible, those three habits — own the conversation, tell the truth, stay available — meant that the people who left did so with their dignity, and the people who stayed did not have to watch us treat their friends like packages being mailed out. That second part matters more than people realise. A lean year does not only damage the people who go. It damages the people who remain, sometimes more. They watch how you behaved, and they decide on the basis of that behaviour how much of themselves they are willing to give you in the next year.

## Protecting the team when there is no easy answer

There is a phrase I want to put in front of you, because I have come to believe in it more strongly the older I get.

**A leader's job in a lean year is to absorb anxiety, not transmit it.**

That does not mean hiding the truth. We have just been through why that is wrong. It means holding the worry in your own body long enough to convert it into a clear, specific instruction for the people around you, so that they have something to do with their hands rather than something to do with their nerves.

A worried welder does worse welding. A worried foreman gives worse instructions. A worried admin clerk misses things on invoices. None of this helps the lean year. The anxiety, if you let it run free through the company, becomes its own multiplier on the original problem. Bad work is more expensive than good work. Mistakes cost money you do not have. Resignations from your best people come at precisely the moment you can least afford to replace them.

So the leader stands in the middle and does a particular kind of internal labour. You take in the bad numbers, the worried suppliers, the difficult bank conversation, the difficult board conversation. You let yourself feel it, privately, because pretending not to is its own kind of damage. And then you walk out onto the floor and you give people clear, narrow, doable work for that day.

*This week, we are focusing on these three jobs. By Friday, I need these two things from your team. I will be back through tomorrow afternoon. If anything is in your way, tell me before lunch.*

Notice what that does. It does not deny the situation. The team knows there is a situation. They are adults. They read the same newspapers and they look at the same car park and they hear the same rumours. What it does is give them a frame inside the situation. *Today, this is what I need from you. Tomorrow, this. The bigger thing is being handled by people whose job it is to handle it. Your job is the part of it that is in your hands.*

People can carry a great deal of worry when they have something definite to do. They cannot carry very much of it at all when they are standing around waiting to find out what is going to happen to them. The leader's job is to keep converting the worry into work, every day, for as long as the lean year lasts.

## Resilience as a daily practice

The word **resilience** gets used so often now that I have started to flinch at it. It has been printed on posters. It has been turned into a leadership cliché. There is, somewhere out there, a workshop you can attend in a conference room with sandwiches at lunch, in which someone with a slide deck will teach you, in four hours, how to be resilient.

The trouble with all of this is that resilience is not a quality. It is not a thing you have or do not have, the way you have brown eyes or you do not. It is a habit, and like all habits, it is built one ordinary day at a time and lost the same way.

The resilient managers I knew in lean years were not unusually tough people. Some of them were quite gentle. They were not fearless. Most of them were quietly afraid, the way any sensible person is afraid when the numbers are bad. What they had was a small set of daily practices that they refused to let the lean year take away from them.

They got up at the same time. Even on the bad mornings. Especially on the bad mornings. The body has its own opinion about how long this is going to last, and if you let your routine collapse, the body starts to think the crisis is permanent. Keep the routine. Even if half of it is a fiction in the first week, keep it. The fiction becomes real with use.

They ate properly. I do not mean this as a joke. A manager running on coffee and the slice of pizza somebody left in the boardroom is a manager who will, by Thursday, make a decision they would not have made if they had eaten breakfast. The body is part of the equipment.

They moved their body. Some of them walked. Some of them ran. One man I knew, much older than me at the time, swam thirty lengths of an awful municipal pool three mornings a week for the whole of a two-year downturn, and he told me, much later, that it was the only thing that kept him sane. Movement washes the chemistry out. Sitting in a chair worrying does not. Pick your form. Do it on the bad days too.

They protected the boundary between work and home. Not perfectly. Nobody does this perfectly in a real crisis. But they did not let the lean year colonise every meal, every weekend, every conversation with their children. The reason was not work-life balance as it is now sold in glossy magazines. The reason was much more practical: the person who has not had a single un-work conversation in three weeks is the person whose judgement begins to narrow. They start to see every problem as the same problem. They stop being useful exactly when you need them to be useful.

They had someone they could be honest with. Not necessarily a coach. Not necessarily a therapist, though sometimes that. A spouse, a brother, an old friend, a mentor from earlier in the career, a pastor, a person in a similar role at a different company with whom there was a quiet, mutual confidentiality. *Today was hard. I do not know what I am going to do about Thursday. I am more scared than I have been in a while.* You need to be able to say those sentences, out loud, to a face, at least once a fortnight, or the pressure backs up inside you and starts to come out as bad temper at the people who least deserve it.

They kept showing up.

That is the centre of the whole thing, in the end. Resilience is not a posture. It is the unglamorous decision, made on a particular Monday morning, that you are going to get in the car and go to work and do today's piece of the job, even though you do not feel like it and even though it is not clear it will be enough. You do that on Monday. Then you do it on Tuesday. Then you do it on Wednesday. None of those days, in isolation, looks heroic. The sum of them, over eighteen months, is the only kind of heroism that actually matters in a long downturn.

If you ever meet a manager who has come through a lean year well, you will probably notice that they are not especially loud or especially confident on the subject. They will not lecture you about it. They will, if you ask, tell you a few small, practical things about routines and breakfasts and walking around the building. They will not promise you a method. They will say something like, *You just keep going.* It will sound like a cliché. It is not a cliché. It is the actual answer, and the reason it sounds like a cliché is that everyone who has lived through it arrived at roughly the same answer, by a different road.

## What a lean year does to relationships

I want to spend a few paragraphs on the human cost, because most books about business skip this, and the skipping is part of the problem.

A lean year takes a tax on every important relationship in a manager's life, and the tax is not optional. You can be wise about how you pay it. You cannot refuse to pay it.

It takes a tax on your marriage or your closest partnership. You bring home a tiredness that does not look, from the outside, like tiredness from physical work. You did not lift anything. You did not sweat. You sat in meetings and made phone calls. To the person you live with, who has had their own difficult day, it can look like you have done nothing and are nevertheless somehow used up. Explaining the kind of tired you are without making it sound like a competition is harder than it should be, and most of us do not do it well in the first lean year. We learn, slowly, that we have to. The alternative is two people quietly resenting each other for things neither of them is doing wrong.

It takes a tax on your children, if you have them. They will not understand for years what was happening in the house. They will only remember that you were quieter in that period, or shorter-tempered, or away more, or present but not really there. The best you can do is be honest at a level they can use. *Work is hard right now. It is not your fault. I am still your father. I will be back in this conversation soon.* That is not a perfect speech. It is a hundred times better than disappearing into a study for months without explanation. Children can carry truth. They cannot carry mystery.

It takes a tax on your friendships. The ones outside work, the ones that have nothing to do with the business, become harder to maintain because you have less of yourself left for them. Some of them will quietly drift. A few of the better ones will hold on through it, often by being unreasonably patient with you. Notice who those people are. When the lean year ends, go and find them, and tell them, plainly, that you noticed. You will need them again, in a different shape, in the next one.

And it takes a tax on the relationships with the people you lead, in a way that is not always obvious until later. You will, in a lean year, have to disappoint people who trust you. You will have to say no to pay rises that were deserved. You will have to delay equipment that the floor needed. You will have to ask people for more output than is fair from a workforce that has just absorbed a round of departures. Each of those is a small withdrawal from an account of goodwill that was built up over years. The account can take it, but only if it sees those withdrawals as withdrawals — that is, as costs that you, the leader, are aware of and intend to pay back when you can.

The managers who get this wrong are the ones who treat the lean-year compromises as the new normal. They forget that the team did them a favour by absorbing the bad year. They do not remember to thank them in a real way when things turn. Two or three years later, when the order book is full again, they wonder why the culture feels brittle and why their best people are quietly entertaining other offers. The lean year did not damage the culture. The lean year is something cultures can survive. What damaged it was the absence of repair afterwards.

When the year ends — and it will end, even if it does not feel like it from inside — you have to be deliberate about the repair. Pay back what you can. Promote the people who carried the weight. Buy the equipment you put off. Sit down with the partners and friends and children and say, *That was a hard stretch. Thank you for staying. I want to be different now that we are through.* And then actually be different. The repair is not a speech. It is six months of behaviour.

## The things that only became clear later

I want to be careful here, because hindsight is a flattering mirror and I do not want to use it to make the past easier than it was.

But some things did become clear to me, in those years, that I do not think I could have learned any other way.

The first was that the business was never as fragile as it felt at three in the morning, and never as safe as it felt at two in the afternoon. The night-time fears were exaggerated. So were the daytime confidences. The truth was almost always somewhere in between, and finding it required talking to a small number of trusted people on the same day, not on different days, and triangulating between their views. The mistake of the lean year, for me, was usually to make a decision on the basis of a single conversation that had happened to land at a moment when I was either too low or too high. I learned, the slow way, to wait until I had three views from three different angles before I acted on anything that mattered.

The second was that the people who panicked first were almost always the people with the least real information. The shop floor knew the business was tight. They did not know the cash position to the day. They did not know what the bank had said in the last meeting. They did not know what the order book looked like in eight weeks' time. In the absence of that information, they filled the gap with their own fears, which were usually worse than the reality. The cure for this was not to give them the full cash position — that would have been irresponsible and, in some cases, illegal — but to give them more information than they had a strict right to. *We are tight, here is the shape of it, here is what we are doing, here is what I will tell you next month.* A small, regular, honest update did more for morale than any motivational speech, and it cost nothing except discipline.

The third was that the lean year revealed who was who. I do not mean this in a hard way. I mean that, under sustained pressure, people's actual character came forward, often quite differently from the version they had presented in the good years. Some of the loudest performers in good times went quiet, or worse. Some of the quietest grinders turned out to have a kind of stoic, dependable steel that I had not seen until the pressure was on them. I made hiring and promotion mistakes in the good years that the lean year corrected for me, sometimes painfully. After it ended, I trusted a slightly different set of people than I had trusted before, and on balance the new set was better. That was an expensive education, and I would not have chosen it. But I cannot say it was wasted.

The fourth, and the one I want to underline, was that there is a particular kind of competence that only develops in lean years and cannot be acquired any other way. A manager who has only ever managed in good times is, in a real sense, an apprentice manager. They have never had to make the hard subtraction. They have never had to look someone in the eye and tell them what they did not want to hear. They have never had to walk a quiet shop floor on a slow Tuesday morning, knowing that the next order had to come in by Friday or there would be consequences, and find a way to be honest with the people there without crushing them. Until you have done that, you have not yet been tested in the part of the job that the badge is really for. You may be very good at the rest of it. But the rest of it is not the whole of it, and the part that is missing is the part that matters most.

I would not wish a lean year on anyone. I have lived through enough of them now to know that they cost more than the spreadsheets show, and that the cost is paid in parts of yourself you cannot see on a balance sheet. But I would also not, in honesty, want to take back the ones I have lived. They taught me the job in a way the good years never could have. They installed a small, useful weight in the middle of how I make decisions, which keeps me from being too cheerful when the numbers turn up again and from being too despairing when they turn down. That weight is, I think, one of the things people are paying for when they hire someone who has been through it.

## What I would tell the younger me

If I could go back to the version of myself sitting at that desk with the list in front of him, the first lean year I really had to manage, I would not try to make it easier for him. I do not think you can. The hardness of those years is part of how they form you, and softening them, even in retrospect, would be a kind of cheating.

But I would put a few quiet sentences in his ear, before he walked into the first conversation.

I would say: *This is not a strategic problem. It is a human one. Treat it that way and you will get fewer of the technical bits wrong, not more.*

I would say: *The people you are about to disappoint are not your enemies. They will not enjoy what you say, but most of them will respect you for saying it cleanly. The ones who do not, you cannot help, and you cannot let them set the standard for the others.*

I would say: *Keep your routine. Walk the floor. Eat your meals. Phone your wife. Do not become a man who only exists in the office. The office cannot hold a whole person, and a half-person is not enough for what this job is about to ask of you.*

I would say: *You will not get every decision right. You will make at least one call this year that you will think about, badly, for years. That does not mean you were the wrong person for the seat. It means the seat was hard and you are human. Forgive yourself in advance, so that the guilt does not stop you making the next decision when you need to.*

And I would say: *This will end. Not soon. Not on the schedule you would prefer. But it will end. The work, today, is to be the kind of leader who is still standing, with the team still around him, when it does.*

## What stayed

Years later, when the markets had recovered and the order book was healthy again and the cars in the car park were newer than the ones we had been driving during the bad stretch, I noticed something about the team that I had not expected.

The people who had been with me through the lean year had a different relationship to the company than the ones who had joined afterwards. It was not better in a sentimental way. They were not more loyal in the dog-like sense that some companies want from their staff. It was more grounded than that. They knew, because they had seen it, what the business actually looked like under pressure. They had watched the leadership make hard decisions and, in most cases, make them with their dignity intact. They had been part of carrying the weight, and they knew it.

That knowledge made them harder to spook in the next downturn, when it came. It made them better at training the new people, because they had a sense of proportion the new people did not. It made them more honest with me, because we had already had the kinds of conversations that strip away the small polite distances between a manager and the people on the floor. We had been in a room together when nobody was sure what was going to happen next, and we had not, in the end, let each other down. That is not a small thing.

If you are sitting in the middle of a lean year right now, I want you to know that this is what is being built, underneath the bad numbers. It does not feel like it from inside. From inside it feels like loss, and exhaustion, and a long, narrow corridor with no obvious end. But the corridor is also where the real culture of your business is being made, by your behaviour, day after day, in front of people who are watching more closely than they let on.

Do the daily things. Tell the truth as cleanly as you can. Protect the dignity of the people leaving and the morale of the people staying. Keep your routine. Keep your relationships. Walk the floor. Eat your breakfast. Call your wife. Show up tomorrow.

That is most of the job, in a lean year. The rest is just timing, and timing is one of the things that is not in your hands.

## A short list, for the desk drawer

If you are the kind of reader who likes to be able to find the practical part again on a hard morning, here it is, compressed.

- **Keep your own hearing.** Do not perform optimism so hard that you stop noticing your own honest worry. The worry is information.
- **Triangulate before you decide.** Three views from three different angles, on the same day, before any decision that matters. Never act on a single conversation at three in the morning.
- **Own the hard meetings yourself.** Do not delegate the telling. The person across the desk will remember who looked at them. So will the people who hear about it afterwards.
- **Tell the truth at the level they can use it.** Not the press release. Not the cash position to the cent. The shape of the situation, what is being done, what they can expect next.
- **Absorb anxiety, do not transmit it.** Convert the bigger worry into smaller, doable work for the people in front of you. Daily. Weekly. Without stopping.
- **Keep the routine.** Body, sleep, meals, movement. Especially on the bad mornings. The routine is not vanity. It is equipment.
- **Pay the human tax knowingly.** Notice the cost to your marriage, your children, your friendships. Plan to repair it, in real behaviour, when the year turns.
- **Repair afterwards.** Do not let the lean-year compromises become the new normal. Pay back what you can. Promote the people who carried the weight. Buy the equipment you put off. Say thank you in a way that costs you something.
- **Forgive yourself in advance.** You will get a call wrong this year. It does not mean you were the wrong person in the seat. It means the seat is hard.
- **Keep showing up.** This is, in the end, the whole of it. Tomorrow morning, again. Then the morning after that.

The next stretch of the story is not about the storm or its aftermath. It is about what I had to teach myself, in those same years and the ones that followed, when it became obvious that craft and character were not, on their own, going to be enough. The numbers in the business had a language of their own, and until I learned to speak it, I was going to keep making good decisions for the wrong reasons and bad decisions with very confident faces. That is where we go next.
