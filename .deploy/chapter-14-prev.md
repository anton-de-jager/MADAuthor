# Becoming Managing Director

There is no ceremony for it. That is the first thing you should know. After all the years, after the apprenticeship and the ticket and the supervisor's clipboard and the office door and the long, slow climb through every box on the chart, the moment at which you become the person at the top of the chart arrives, almost always, in a manner that would disappoint a screenwriter. A letter is handed to you. Or an email lands. Or a chairman, who has known you for fifteen years, leans back in his chair and says, "Right. It is time." And then he asks how your daughter is doing at university, because he does not know what else to fill the silence with.

You sit in that silence for a moment and you wait for something to feel different. It does not. The chair you are in is the chair you sat in yesterday. The view through the window has not improved. The cup on the desk in front of you is the same cup you have been drinking from since Tuesday. Somewhere in your chest there is a tightening that you cannot quite locate, and somewhere else, lower down, there is a small, quiet voice that says, with what sounds suspiciously like fear, *now we shall see*.

I want to write this chapter honestly, because I have read too many books in which the arrival at the top of an organisation is described as a kind of summit moment, with thin air and a view and a flag. It is not like that. There is no flag. There is, instead, a long Monday morning, and a diary that has been quietly filling up while you were not looking, and a list of people who would now like to speak to you for reasons that have not yet been explained.

This is the chapter about that Monday morning, and the months that followed it. What changes when you take the top operational seat in a business. What, more usefully, does not. And why arriving at what looks from the outside like the end of the climb is, in practice, the moment at which the climb quietly raises the bar on you and steps back to see what you will do.

## The day the title arrives

I will not dramatise the day mine arrived. There was a board meeting. There were the right words said in the right order. There was a handshake that I remember as being a little firmer than it strictly needed to be, from a man I respected, who had decided, on balance, that I was the person to carry this thing forward. He did not say very much. He did not need to. The look on his face contained, in compressed form, an instruction and a warning and a small, dry blessing, and I understood all three.

I walked back to my office and closed the door, the way I had closed it on the day I first sat behind a desk with my name on it, years before. The door made the same soft click. The corridor smelled the same. The kettle in the small kitchen at the end of the floor was still, as it always was, very slightly too loud, and someone, somewhere, was on a phone call that was not going well.

I sat in my chair and I waited to feel like a managing director. I did not feel like one. I felt like a boilermaker who had, by a long and not always graceful route, ended up wearing a tie in a room that smelled of carpet. The brass plate on the door had been changed, presumably overnight, by someone whose job that apparently was. The chair I was sitting in was new — they had given me a different one, slightly higher, with a back that supported a part of me I had not realised needed supporting. That was, on the day, the only physical evidence that anything had changed. A chair. A line on a piece of paper. A handshake.

> The day the title arrives is almost never the day the job arrives. The job arrives quietly, in pieces, over the weeks that follow, and most of it is not what you were expecting.

I want to be careful, here, about a particular kind of false modesty. There is a school of writing about leadership which insists that the senior man must always present himself as humbled and uncertain on the day he is promoted, as though confidence were a kind of bad manners. That is not honest. There was, in me, on that day, a small and entirely real sense of satisfaction. Forty-odd years of work had pointed in this direction. The boy on the shop floor, the apprentice with the wrong gloves, the young man with the freshly issued ticket, the supervisor learning to discipline his former drinking partners — all of them, in some way, had been walking towards this chair. To pretend that I sat in it without any feeling at all would be a lie. I felt it. I allowed myself, for about half an hour, to feel it.

And then the phone rang, and the phone was the new phone, and the job began.

## What changes

Some things do change, and you may as well be told plainly what they are, because the books will not tell you. They will tell you, instead, about *vision* and *stewardship* and *the captain at the helm*, and you will read those words and recognise none of them in the life you are actually being asked to live. Let me describe the changes I noticed first, in the order in which I noticed them, and you can decide which of them surprise you.

The first change is that the calendar stops being yours. You had, in your previous roles, a working assumption that your time belonged to you and that the demands on it were yours to triage. That assumption goes. Your time, from the moment the title settles, belongs to the business. There will be meetings you did not arrange and could not have prevented. There will be calls from people you have never met who somehow expect to be put through to you immediately. There will be functions, evenings, breakfasts, charity boards, industry events, that arrive on your diary not because you said yes but because someone, somewhere, has decided that the managing director ought to be there. The skill is no longer to *manage your time*. The skill is to choose, ruthlessly and without apology, which of the things demanding your time will actually have you.

The second change is the quality of attention you receive when you speak. People listen differently to a managing director than they do to a senior manager. They listen more carefully and, often, less critically. A throwaway comment made in a corridor will be repeated, by lunchtime, on the other side of the building, with an authority you did not intend it to carry. A question you asked out of curiosity will be interpreted as an instruction. An observation about the colour of a wall will become, by the end of the week, a redecorating project. You learn very quickly that you have lost the right to think out loud. Whatever leaves your mouth, in any setting in which someone from the business can hear it, is now a small, weighted thing that lands harder than it used to.

The third change is the loneliness. I have read about it. I had been warned about it. I did not, until I sat in the chair, fully understand it. It is not the loneliness of having no one to talk to. There are, if anything, more people in your day than ever before. It is the loneliness of having no one above you in the company who carries your specific load. You can talk to your chairman. You can talk to your board. You can talk to a peer at another business. None of them is responsible, in the way that you are now responsible, for the daily, hour-by-hour weight of this particular organisation. When you go home at night, you carry it on your own. There is no manager above you to whom you can hand the last unsolved problem of the day with the quiet relief of saying, "Over to you." There is only you, the dog, and the dark garden, and the problem.

The fourth change, and this one I had not been warned about, is the way your relationship to bad news shifts. As a manager, bad news used to come to you and you used to take it upward. There was always someone to take it to. As managing director, bad news comes to you and stops. It does not move. It sits in your office, in your inbox, on your phone, and waits for you to decide what is to be done with it. There is no further address. You are the last desk in the building. Everything that has not been solved by someone else, eventually, becomes yours. That is, in the end, what you are paid for: to be the desk at which the unresolvable comes to rest.

The fifth change is one I did not expect, and which I think is rarely written about because it sounds a little ungrateful when said out loud. It is the way old friendships in the business shift their shape. People you had worked beside for years, men with whom you had shared bad coffee in worse weather, suddenly speak to you with a small, careful pause before each sentence. They do not mean to. They do not even know they are doing it. But the title sits between you, like a third person at the table, and although neither of you wishes to acknowledge it, it changes the temperature of the conversation. You can still go for a beer with the men you came up with. You can still laugh at the old stories. But there is a layer of the friendship that has, quietly and through nobody's fault, been closed. They cannot complain about management to you any more, because you are management. You cannot grumble about the board to them, because the board is now your responsibility. The friendships survive, in my experience, if both sides are honest about the new geometry. They do not survive at all if either side pretends nothing has changed.

The sixth change is the one I noticed last, and only because my wife pointed it out to me. The chair follows you home. I do not mean the work. The work had been following me home for years before I became managing director. I mean the chair itself — the posture of the chair, the responsibility of the chair, the small constant weight of being the person who is always, somewhere in the back of his mind, asking whether the business is all right tonight. You do not switch it off at six o'clock. You do not switch it off on a Saturday. You can be at a wedding, or in a hospital corridor, or watching your grandson kick a football into a flowerbed, and a part of you is still, quietly, listening for the phone. It is not, in itself, a bad thing. It is what you have, in some sense, signed up for. But it is real, and the people who love you will feel it before you do, and you owe them the honesty of admitting that the chair has, in this small invisible way, moved into the house with you.

## What does not change

But — and this is the part of the chapter I most want you to hear — the things that actually run a business do not change at all. Not on the day the title arrives, not in the months after, not in the years that follow. They are the same things they were when you were a charge-hand. They are the same things they were when you stood, freshly ticketed, on the floor of your first proper job. They are the same things they were when you were an apprentice, watching an old tradesman wipe his hands on a rag and explain, for the third time, why the weld you had just put down was not yet good enough.

The work still has to be done well. That does not change. The customer still has to be told the truth. That does not change. The people on the floor still have to feel that the man in the office sees them, knows their names, knows what they are paid, knows when their wives have been ill. That does not change. The numbers still have to balance, the suppliers still have to be paid on time, the apprentices still have to be taught properly, the safety procedures still have to be followed even on the day no inspector is coming. None of that changes when your title changes. If anything, it presses harder, because there is no longer anyone above you whose job it is to remember it on your behalf.

I want to say something here that I think is genuinely important, and which I have not heard said often enough by people in my position. **The trade does not leave you.** The years on the floor do not become irrelevant the moment you sit behind a larger desk. They become, in fact, the single most useful possession you bring into the room. The instinct that tells you, without being able to explain why, that a project plan is dishonest — that instinct was forged in a workshop, not in an MBA. The capacity to look at a man's hands and tell, before he opens his mouth, whether he has worked today — that came from the floor. The ability to walk into a building and feel, in the first thirty seconds, whether the place is healthy or whether something is wrong — that is not a managerial skill. That is a tradesman's nose, refined by time.

> The longer I sat in the managing director's chair, the more I leaned on the boilermaker who had been waiting, all along, inside the suit.

I do not want to overstate this. There are skills the chair demands that the trade did not teach me. Reading a board pack at the depth it deserves. Holding a conversation with a banker who has come to test whether you understand your own balance sheet. Listening to a chairman explain, in language that does not quite touch the surface of what he means, that he has concerns about a particular shareholder. Sitting through a legal opinion without flinching at the cost of it. Those skills I had to learn, and I learned them slowly and with help, and there is no shame in saying that for the first year there were rooms in which I felt like a guest. But the spine of the work, the part of the job that actually mattered when something went wrong, was always the part that the floor had taught me long before. The chair did not replace the trade. It rested on it.

## The quiet raising of the bar

When you arrive at a place that, from the outside, looks like the destination, a strange thing happens. You expect the climb to be over. The view, from this chair, ought to be the view. You have arrived. You can, surely, exhale.

Instead, almost the moment you sit down, the standard you are being held to changes. You realise this not all at once but in small encounters. A supplier who used to be content to deal with your operations manager now wants to be received by you, and is not flattered by an office, only by an undivided hour of your attention. A young man at the company social event tells you, quite openly, that he came to work for the business because of you, and you understand that the careless promise you have just made him over a glass of warm beer will be remembered, by him, for the next twenty years. A regulator writes to you, not to the company, by name. A journalist phones the switchboard and asks for you specifically. A customer who has had a quiet, polite grievance for two years now decides, in light of your appointment, that it is time to tell you about it personally.

You begin to grasp that the bar has not stopped rising. It has only moved out of sight. There is no further title to chase, but there is a continually rising expectation of the quality of person you are, in the chair, and that expectation is the thing that goes on testing you for as long as you sit in it.

This is, I think, the central confusion that newly appointed senior people have to work through, and the sooner they work through it the better. The promotion is not the prize. The promotion is the entry ticket to a longer, lonelier examination, the marking of which will be done quietly, over years, by people who will not always tell you how you are scoring. You will, on most days, not know whether you are passing. You will have to settle for behaving, as best you can, in a way that you would still respect if you could read the marking sheet.

I do not say this to discourage anyone. I say it because I would rather have known it on day one. I spent, I confess, the first six months in the chair quietly waiting to feel as though I had arrived. The feeling did not come. It was not going to come. I had been waiting for the wrong feeling. What actually comes, if you are paying attention, is a different and more useful sensation: the sense that there is now no one else whose job it is to set the standard for this particular organisation. The standard is whatever you, day by day, decide to behave as though it were. If you behave well, the organisation slowly comes to behave well. If you allow yourself a small piece of laziness on a Tuesday, the organisation will, by Friday, have allowed itself a larger one.

That is what arriving means. It does not mean you have stopped climbing. It means the rope is now tied around your waist, and the people behind you are looking up to see where you put your hand next.

## Things I tried to keep doing

I want to be practical, because the rest of this chapter would dissolve into philosophy otherwise, and I have always distrusted philosophy that cannot be acted on by Wednesday. So let me describe a handful of small disciplines I tried to hold onto in the months and years after taking the chair. None of them is original. None of them is sophisticated. All of them, I think, were what kept the seat from changing me into someone I would not have liked to meet.

The first was that I walked the floor. Every working week, without exception, I walked through the operations of the business in the clothes I was wearing that day, and I stopped and spoke to people. Not in a contrived way. Not with a clipboard. Not with a communications person hovering. I walked through, I asked how the job was going, I listened to the answer, and I went on. Sometimes I learned something useful. Sometimes I learned nothing. What mattered was that I had been seen, in person, by people who needed to know that the managing director was a man, not a rumour.

The second was that I kept answering my own phone, within reason, for as long as it was possible. I know this is unfashionable. There are people who will tell you that a managing director's time is too valuable to be intercepted by an unscreened call. I understand the argument. I never agreed with it. A small percentage of the calls I picked up directly, over the years, contained information I would not have received any other way, and the cost of the wasted ones was a price I was content to pay. There is something in the human voice on a live line that an email cannot reproduce. I did not want to lose access to it.

The third was that I went home. I know how strange that sounds in a chapter about the top job, but I mean it as a discipline. The chair will, if you let it, expand to fill every hour. There will always be one more report, one more email, one more situation that *only the managing director can handle*. Almost none of it cannot wait until morning. I made a quiet decision, early on, that the lights in the building would not be off later than mine on most evenings, and that on weekends I would, except in true emergencies, be out of the building entirely. Not because I was lazy. Because a managing director who is exhausted is a managing director who makes bad decisions, and the business cannot afford bad decisions from that chair. Rest, in the end, is not self-indulgence. It is part of the role.

The fourth was that I kept, in a drawer of my desk, a small notebook in which I wrote, in plain language, the kind of leader I had told myself I would try to be. I did not show it to anyone. I did not write in it often. Once every few weeks, when something had gone wrong or right in a way that I wanted to remember, I would open it and add a line. Then I would close it and put it back in the drawer. I think every senior person should have a document of that sort, somewhere out of sight, written for an audience of one. The chair will, over time, present you with a hundred small invitations to become someone else. The notebook is the way you remember who you said you would remain.

The fifth was that I kept one piece of the trade physically near me. In my office, on a shelf behind my desk, I had an old pair of welder's gauntlets I had used as a young man, blackened on the palms, with the leather gone hard and shiny where my grip had worn it. I did not put them on display in a glass case. I did not point at them when visitors came. They were simply there, on the shelf, where I could see them out of the corner of my eye when I was reading something I did not believe, or being told something by a consultant that did not match what I had seen with my own eyes on the floor. They were a small, quiet reminder that the world the consultant lived in and the world the gauntlets came from were not, in the end, the same world, and that of the two, I trusted the second one more. I would not insist that every managing director needs gauntlets on his shelf. I would insist that every managing director needs *something* on his shelf that he cannot lie to.

The sixth, and last of these small disciplines, was that I tried, however imperfectly, to be the same man in every room. There is a particular temptation, in the chair, to become a slightly different version of yourself depending on whom you are with. Warmer with the floor. More clipped with the board. More polished with the press. More cautious with the bankers. A small adjustment, in each case, to suit the room. I am not a saint, and I made those adjustments too, more often than I would like to admit. But I noticed, over time, that the senior people I most respected did not. They spoke the same way to the cleaner in the corridor as to the chairman in the boardroom. Not identically — the words were different — but the *posture* was the same. The same level of attention. The same willingness to be honest. The same refusal to perform. That, I came to think, was the deeper meaning of integrity. Not a set of rules. A consistency of self across rooms.

## On not believing your own organogram

There is a final thing I want to say, and it is the thing I think senior people get wrong more often than any other, including me, in the early days.

Do not believe your own organogram.

The organogram is a useful diagram. It shows you, roughly, who reports to whom, and which boxes carry which responsibilities, and which signatures are needed for which decisions. It is a piece of infrastructure. It is not a portrait of the business.

The real business runs on a different map, and that map is not drawn anywhere. It is the map of trust, of unwritten favours, of who actually picks up the phone when something is on fire, of which two people in the company can, between them, solve almost any operational problem if they are simply allowed to talk. That map is invisible from your chair, unless you make a deliberate effort to see it. The organogram will tell you that you are at the top. The real map will tell you that you are, very often, at the edge — far from where the work actually happens, far from where the relationships actually live, far from where the next problem will actually be solved.

A good managing director, in my view, spends a serious portion of his time refusing to be at the top of the organogram. He goes back down it, again and again, into the rooms and the corridors and the workshops where the unwritten map still runs the place. He keeps his name on the brass plate and his body somewhere else. The chair is where he signs things. The business is where he lives.

> The title puts you at the top of the chart. The work, if you are doing it honestly, keeps putting you back at the bottom of it.

I have known managing directors who forgot this. They sat, year after year, at the apex of the diagram and slowly came to believe the diagram was the company. They were always surprised, when something went wrong, that no one had told them. They had stopped being where telling happened. They had become the last person in the building to know anything that mattered, and the last person in the building to know anything that matters is, almost by definition, no longer running the business. The business is running them.

## What I would say to the version of me who sat down on that Monday

If I could write a short note to the man who closed the door behind him on the day he became managing director, I would not say very much. He was, I remember, not in the mood for advice. He was in the mood for the next thing on the list, because the next thing on the list was the thing his whole life had taught him to attend to.

But if he would allow me a paragraph, I would say this.

You have not arrived. You have, instead, been handed the longest and most patient examination of your life, and the people marking it will not always be kind. The chair is not a reward. It is a position. Hold it the way you held the boilermaker's torch — steady, with both hands, with respect for the heat. Do not pretend that the trade is behind you. It is not behind you. It is the only honest thing in the room, and you will need it on the worst days more than on any of the others. Do not believe the corridor when it tells you that you are now important. The corridor is wrong about almost everything. You are important only insofar as the people on the floor still trust you, and they will only go on trusting you if you remain, in some unmistakable way, one of them.

Walk the floor. Answer your own phone where you can. Go home in the evening. Keep the notebook. Refuse the organogram. And when the title comes off, as titles do, leave the chair in a better state than you found it. That, in the end, is the whole job.

You will not, on the day you stand up from it for the last time, be handed a flag either. There is no ceremony for leaving the chair, any more than there was for sitting in it. There will only be a quiet handshake, from some younger person whose name is now on the brass plate, and a long walk down a corridor that smells, as it always did, very faintly of printer toner and instant coffee.

That walk is not the end of the climb either. But that is a chapter for another time.
