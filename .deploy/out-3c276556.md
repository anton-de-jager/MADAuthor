# Trust and Inspire: A Management Style for the Modern Workforce

There comes a point in a working life when a man stops collecting management techniques and starts trying to name the one thing that has held them all together. I am at that point. I have read most of the books the airport bookshops have been pressing on executives for forty years. I have sat through more leadership seminars than I am willing to confess to in print, and on a number of occasions I have been the man at the front of the room. None of that, in the end, is what I would put on the first page if I were asked to summarise a half-century of running operations. The first page would have two ideas on it, and the rest would be blank. Trust and Inspire. Not Command and Control.

I want to spend this chapter explaining what I mean by that, because it is the philosophy underneath every turnaround I have ever signed my name to. It is not a slogan I picked up from a course. It was learned, slowly and unfashionably, on chemical plants and pharmaceutical sites and leather floors and confectionery lines — from the people I tried to lead, and from the people I watched fail to lead. By the time I sat in my first Managing Director's chair, in Port Elizabeth in my early forties, I already knew what I would not be. The harder work, then and since, has been making sure I am the other thing instead.

## Two styles, and only one of them works

Command-and-Control is the easier style to describe, because every one of us has worked under it at some point. It treats a workforce as a problem of compliance. Instructions come down. Reports go up. The manager's job is to set targets, monitor variances, and bring the hammer when the variances are unfavourable. Staff under that style work with one eye on the spreadsheet and the other over their shoulder. They keep their heads down. They volunteer nothing. They wait to be told.

I have run plants where that was the inherited culture, and I have always recognised it within a week of arriving. The signs are everywhere. The shift supervisor will not speak up in the morning meeting. The operator on the line will let you walk past a problem rather than flag it. The maintenance team will sit on a known defect because the last time they raised one they were blamed for finding it. The whole organisation is, very quietly, choosing not to think. And a plant whose people have stopped thinking is a plant whose output is, sooner or later, going to fall off the back of the truck.

Trust-and-Inspire is harder to describe, because to an outsider it looks as though very little is happening. The manager seems to be doing less. The reports are quieter. The instructions are fewer. What is actually happening is that the people closest to the work have been given the dignity of being treated as the experts they very often are. They are trusted with the problem. They are inspired toward a shared goal. They are then permitted, within sensible boundaries, to find their own way to it. The plant, if you have hired and trained the right people, runs better than it ever ran under the man with the megaphone.

I have used a phrase about this with younger managers for many years. Mahatma Gandhi, in one of his less-quoted observations, said that human beings only use a small percentage of what they are capable of. He was making a point about humanity in general, but it has always read to me as a point about management. The secret, in any organisation, lies in extracting people's capability, and you do not extract it by shouting at them. You extract it by trusting them and by giving them something worth being inspired by. The leader who cannot do those two things is, in my honest experience, leaving most of his workforce's value on the table.

## The proof from Redmond

I have always been wary, when I make this argument in a boardroom, of sounding like a man preaching philosophy at people who have a P&L to defend. So for some years now I have kept a single contemporary example in my pocket for those conversations. It is the example of Microsoft.

When Satya Nadella took over the company in 2014, the market valued Microsoft at about three hundred billion dollars. By any reasonable measure it was already a successful business. The problem was that, by every internal measure that mattered, it had stopped behaving like one. The internal culture had become famously combative. Teams hoarded information from each other. Managers were measured on stack rankings that punished collaboration. The company that had once defined the personal computer industry was, behind the glass doors, eating itself.

Nadella's intervention was not, in the first instance, technical. He did not announce a new operating system or a reorganisation of the product groups. He announced, in language that was uncomfortable for many of the old hands, that the company was going to operate from a different mindset. Curiosity over knowing. Learning over performing. Empathy as a leadership value. Trust as the default. By the time I write this, Microsoft's market value sits somewhere above two trillion dollars. Whatever you think of the man and the company, those are not numbers that argue for the Command-and-Control playbook. They are numbers that argue for what happens when the leader at the top trusts the people underneath him to do their best work, and inspires them toward a vision they can see themselves in.

I do not raise the Microsoft story because I think small South African operations should imitate Silicon Valley. I raise it because it puts to bed, in the most public possible way, the lazy argument that Trust-and-Inspire is a soft style for a soft economy. It is not. It is the operating model of the highest-performing technology company in the world. If it is good enough for two trillion dollars in market value, it is good enough for the confectionery plant in Durban or the porridge factory in Pinetown.

## Emotional intelligence, and the kind of mentor you should have

Trust-and-Inspire is not a personality trait, and it is not a slogan you can hang in the reception area. It is built on two more practical skills, and a manager who lacks them cannot fake it for long.

The first is emotional intelligence. I am not, by temperament, a man who was raised on that phrase. I was raised on the East Rand by a churchgoing father and an industrious mother, and they had a different vocabulary for what is now called EQ. They called it being decent. They called it knowing how to read a room. They called it not embarrassing people. Whatever the language, the underlying skill is the same. It is the ability to notice what is going on inside yourself and inside the other person, in real time, and to manage your behaviour in light of it. A manager who cannot do that will, sooner or later, blow up a relationship that took years to build, and he will not even know which sentence did it.

I have come to believe that emotional intelligence is the single most under-discussed competence in modern engineering and operations management. Most of the technical people I have trained alongside, my younger self included, were taught very thoroughly how a process runs and very poorly how a human being runs. The result is a generation of competent engineers who become incompetent managers the day they are first asked to lead a team. People will forget what you said. People will forget what you did. But they will never forget how you made them feel. That sentence, which I picked up from somewhere a long time ago and have never been able to improve on, is the entire syllabus of management in one line.

The second practical skill is the willingness to use, and to be, a mentor. I have had mentors all my life, and I have tried, with mixed success, to be one to others. The criterion that has mattered most to me, when I have chosen the mentors I trust, is integrity. A mentor must be someone who will tell you the truth, even when the truth is awkward, and who you would in turn be willing to tell the truth to. A network of high-integrity colleagues like that, built up patiently over decades, is the single most valuable professional asset I have. It is more valuable than my CV. It is more valuable than any award I have received. The phone calls that have come to me in this third act of my career have all come from inside that network, and most of those people I first earned the trust of in the 1980s.

If you are young as you read this, find one or two of those people early. Not flatterers, not seniors you hope will sponsor you, but peers and superiors you can trust to push back when you are wrong and who you will, in your turn, push back on. Then keep them, all your working life. They are the cheapest insurance policy a career has on offer.

## Mission, vision and the disciplined plan

There is a particular failure of management I have walked into more often than I expected, in the second half of my career, and it is a failure of strategy rather than execution. I have arrived at companies, more than once, that had no documented strategy at all for the function I was being asked to take over. The annual budget would be carefully prepared, with line-item discipline that would have impressed an actuary, but the budget would not be anchored to any actual strategic plan. There would be no clear answer to the question of where the business was trying to go in three years' time, or why, or how. The mission statement, where one existed, would be a paragraph of generic phrases that nobody on the shopfloor could have repeated under oath.

In every business I have led, I have insisted on something different. Before the budget is signed off, there must be a strategy. Before the strategy is signed off, there must be a clear mission and a clear vision that the workforce can repeat in their own words. The mission tells you what you are for. The vision tells you what you are aiming at. The strategy tells you how you intend to get there. The budget then funds the strategy. In that order. Not the other way around. Companies that build their budgets first and look around for a strategy afterwards are companies that, in my experience, will eventually drift into a slow operational decline that nobody can quite explain.

There is one further point on this, and it is one I have repeated in many boardrooms because it is regularly forgotten. **Structure follows strategy.** When the strategy of a business changes, the structure has to change with it. The reporting lines, the functional boundaries, the spans of control, the way roles are described — all of those have to be redrawn to support the new strategic direction. Many companies do not do this. They announce a new strategy on the first slide of the year, and then they try to deliver it through the same organogram that supported last year's strategy. Implementation, when this happens, becomes a problem nobody can pin down. It is not a problem of people. It is a problem of architecture.

## The cost of a poor manager

I have spent so much of this chapter on what good management looks like that I should be honest, too, about what poor management costs. Across my career, when I have walked into a part of a business that was underperforming, I have looked first at the people. Without exception, when the staff were the problem, the underlying cause was the manager. The wrong person at the top had been hired, or retained, or promoted, and the cascade of disengagement underneath that person was a perfectly predictable consequence.

People do not leave bad jobs. They leave bad bosses. I have seen good operators resign within weeks of a new shift manager being appointed who treated them poorly. I have seen technical brilliance disappear from a department within a year of a senior leader being installed who did not respect the function. Bad managers are bad for business, and they are even worse for their employees. Any senior leader who does not move quickly to address poor management in their organisation is, in effect, choosing to subsidise it.

The other awkward truth I have observed, particularly later in my career, is the asymmetry between the floor and the office. Shopfloor staff often stay for twenty years, raising their families on that pay cheque, while the management above them changes every two or three. Each new manager arrives with new ideas, holds a few impressive meetings, sometimes commissions a few impressive consultants, and is then gone within twenty-four months. The new ideas disappear with them. The next manager arrives and starts the cycle again. The shopfloor staff, who have been watching this for two decades, are too polite to laugh out loud, but they have stopped taking any of it seriously. Only a small number of senior leaders can build something with their people that lasts beyond their own tenure. Those are the leaders who tend, in my experience, to lead through Trust-and-Inspire rather than through edict.

## Hire for attitude

I will say something here that I have said many times in interviews and in mentoring sessions, and which still sometimes raises an eyebrow in an HR department. Where two candidates are close, hire for attitude over qualifications. I do not mean that qualifications do not matter. Of course they do. I am writing this as a man who studied at night for years to earn the diplomas that opened my doors. But beyond a sensible threshold of competence, the deciding variable is attitude, every time. The candidate with the better paperwork and the wrong attitude will cost you more than the candidate with the lesser paperwork and the right one. The wrong attitude takes up a disproportionate share of your management time, and worse than that, it lowers the standard of every team it joins.

The right attitude, by contrast, is teachable but not transferable. You can teach a person with the right attitude almost any technical skill your business needs. You cannot teach a person with the wrong attitude to care. The market has known this for a very long time. *Ability is what you are capable of doing; motivation determines what you do; attitude determines how well you do it.* That sentence has hung in my head for most of my management years, and it has saved me from several hires I might otherwise have made.

## Celebrating success, all the way to the floor

Trust-and-Inspire is not complete without one further habit, and it is the one most often left out of the management courses I have attended. You must celebrate success, and you must celebrate it down to the shopfloor.

In too many companies the celebration of a good year stops at the management level. The directors get the bonus. The senior managers get the certificate. The floor staff, who actually moved the boxes and ran the machines and made the product that delivered the result, get a memo. That asymmetry is felt. It is not a small thing. People at every level of an operation want to know that what they did mattered, and they want to know it in a form they can take home and show to their families.

I have made it my private rule, at every site I have run, to recognise good work at the level it actually happens. I have walked the floor at the end of a successful project and shaken the hands of the operators who delivered it. I have asked supervisors to put up the names of the shift teams who hit a difficult target that week. I have personally invited artisans into management meetings, not to present, but to be thanked. None of those gestures cost the business anything meaningful. All of them changed, in small but cumulative ways, what people felt about coming to work the next morning.

## The banner in Port Elizabeth

When I took over as Managing Director in Port Elizabeth, I had a banner put up at the entrance of the company. It was not subtle. In large clear letters you could not miss as you walked through the gate, it read: **WORK SMART AND HAVE FUN**.

I meant every word of it, and I made sure the company meant it too. Work Smart was not a soft phrase. It was an instruction. It meant that effort without direction was not what we were paid for. It meant we were going to plan our work, sequence it properly, automate what should be automated, and stop confusing motion with progress. It meant we were going to respect each other's time. It meant we were going to deliver against clear targets and measure ourselves honestly against them.

Have Fun was the other half of the instruction, and it was just as important. A workplace where people are afraid to laugh, afraid to put up a photograph of their children on the noticeboard, afraid to be themselves between the morning briefing and the end of shift, is a workplace that has quietly stopped being a place where excellent work is possible. I wanted my plant to be a place where people enjoyed their work as much as they were proud of it. We held social events. We celebrated milestones. We made room for the small humanities of a working week. The banner was, in its way, a contract between me and the workforce: we will work hard and intelligently, and we will treat each other like the human beings we are.

## Sipho, the artisan fitter

I want to tell one story before I close this chapter, because no philosophy of management is worth more than the people it produces. The story is from my Adcock Ingram years, in the middle of a supply chain overhaul. We had been struggling for some weeks with downtime on one of our critical production lines. A persistent maintenance fault had become a thorn in the side of the operations team, eating into output and into morale. The leadership group was, at the time, consumed with the broader turnaround plan. The fault on that one line was, in the cold balance of priorities, a problem we had not yet given proper attention to.

It was Sipho who solved it. He was a soft-spoken artisan fitter, and he took it upon himself, in his own time and without being asked, to diagnose the root cause. He went through the manuals. He consulted retired colleagues whose names he had kept from earlier projects. Within a few days he came forward with a small, elegant redesign of the sealing system that eliminated the fault entirely. The line returned to full productivity. There had been no fanfare. There had been no memo. He had simply taken ownership of a problem the rest of us had not yet reached, and he had fixed it.

When I found out what he had done, I invited him to the next management meeting. He was not invited to report. He was invited to be recognised. I told the broader team his story in front of him, in plain language. I described his ownership, his curiosity, his willingness to use his own evenings to dig into something nobody had asked him to dig into. We presented him with a framed certificate, which was the easy part. But more importantly, in that room, we gave him a voice. He walked out of that meeting a different man, and I have always believed that a future leader was born in our company that afternoon.

That moment taught me, more clearly than any course I have ever attended, what recognition is actually for. Recognition is not about ceremony. It is about dignity. Celebration is not a distraction from work. It is what gives work its meaning. The framed certificate cost us almost nothing. The dignity it carried changed how Sipho saw his place in the company, and how every colleague of his at that meeting understood what we were prepared to notice.

## Wings to fly

I will end this chapter with the words of a senior manager rather than my own. When I left Tiger Brands at the end of my contract there, he came to find me in the corridor on my last day. He shook my hand. He thanked me for what he called my delegating style. And he said something I have never forgotten. He said I had given them wings to fly.

I have, in the years since, often turned that phrase over in my head. I do not think he was being sentimental. I think he was reporting, honestly, what the Trust-and-Inspire style had produced inside his own working life. He had been trusted with real authority. He had been inspired by a vision he could see himself in. He had been given the space to grow into a manager he might not have become under a different leader. By the time I walked out of the building he was a more capable, more confident, more useful executive than he had been when I arrived. None of that growth had cost the company anything. All of it would compound, in his career, long after my own name had faded from the staff lists.

That, in the end, is what this style is for. Not for the manager who practises it. Not for the leader whose picture might one day go on a wall. It is for the people on the other side of the table, who deserve to be trusted with the work they were hired to do, and who, when they are, will rise further than any of us, including themselves, would have predicted on the day they were first interviewed.

If I am remembered for one thing in this long career, I would like it to be that. Not the turnarounds, not the contracts, not the OEE screens, not the four-day weeks, not the export growth or the safety records. Just the small, quiet sentence in a corridor at Tiger Brands, from a man who is now leading his own team somewhere in this country, saying that he was once given wings to fly.

That, more than any market valuation, is what a management style is worth.
