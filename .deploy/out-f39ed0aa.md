# The Compass

There is a small brass compass somewhere in a drawer in my house, of the kind that costs almost nothing and would not survive any serious use. I do not remember who gave it to me. I think it came in a Christmas cracker, one of those plastic-pull affairs at a company lunch, and I kept it because I was the sort of person who kept things. The needle is lazy. It swings about for a long moment before it settles, and even then, if you tilt the case, it forgets itself and has to find the line again. It is not, by any honest measure, a useful instrument. You could not navigate a car park with it.

I have thought about that small, unreliable compass a great deal in recent years, because it is, in the end, a reasonable picture of what I have to offer you at the close of this book.

I cannot give you a map. The country you will walk in is not the country I walked in. The metal is different. The economy is different. The people you will lead were born into a world I would have struggled to recognise at their age, and the world you will lead them into is not yet finished being built. A map would lie to you. Anyone who hands you one is either selling something or has not been out far enough to know better.

But a compass is a different kind of instrument. A compass does not tell you where to go. It only tells you, when you have lost your bearings, which way is which. It will not walk for you. It will not protect you from the weather. It will not promise that the path it suggests is easy, or short, or even, on a given day, the right one. It just, quietly, finds the line again when you have forgotten where the line was. That is all I am trying to leave you with here. A handful of needles that have, over forty years and change, kept finding their line when I had lost mine.

This last chapter is the smallest in the book and, I hope, the most useful. I am going to tell you, in plain language and without any varnish, the principles the journey actually produced. Not the ones I wish it had produced. Not the ones that would look good on a coffee mug. The ones that, when I was tired and frightened and wrong, kept me oriented well enough to take the next decent step.

If you are still on the floor, this chapter is for you. If you are somewhere in the middle, with a title that has grown faster than your sense of yourself, this chapter is especially for you. And if you are sitting where I sat at the end of it — in a chair you did not entirely plan to occupy, with people you are responsible for and very few people left above you who will tell you the truth — this chapter is for you too, because the compass does not stop being useful once you have a corner office. If anything, it becomes the only thing that still works.

## What a compass is not

Before I give you the needles, I want to be honest about what this is not, because the world is loud with people promising things in this space and I do not want to add to the noise.

This is not a system. I do not have seven habits, or four disciplines, or a pyramid with my surname on it. Anyone who tells you they have solved leadership has either led nothing important or stopped paying attention to what they have led. The work is too various and the people too irreducible for any system to hold for long. The most you can do is keep a few small instruments on your person that, used together, will tell you roughly where north is.

This is not a guarantee. Following these will not make you rich, or admired, or even, on a long enough timeline, secure. Some of them have cost me money. Most of them have, at one point or another, cost me sleep. They are not a hedge. They are a way of being in the work that lets you, when the year is finished, look at yourself in the bathroom mirror without flinching. That is a smaller promise than the books usually offer, and a larger one than most of them deliver.

This is not new. None of these ideas were invented by me. I learned almost every one of them from someone older, often without realising at the time that I was learning anything at all. What I can offer is not novelty. It is the slightly unusual perspective of having had to use these things in a place where the metal does not care about your tone of voice, and then having had to keep using them later, in a place where almost everyone around me had forgotten what the metal was. That doubling is, I think, the only thing about my view that is genuinely mine.

So take what is here as a small brass instrument from a drawer. Not a satellite. Not a system. A compass.

## The needles

I am going to give them to you as short, plain sentences, each followed by a few paragraphs of what I actually mean by it. I have tried to keep these in the order I learned them, which is roughly the order in which a working life tends to put them in front of you. You will not need all of them on any given day. You will need at least one of them every day.

### One. Do the work in front of you, properly.

This is the oldest needle and the one I trust most. It came from the trade and it has never let me down, in any seat I have sat in since.

Most days, in any job, do not call for vision. They call for the next thing to be done, and done properly. The weld laid down without porosity. The email answered without bluster. The conversation had without flinching. The report checked once more before it goes out. Vision is for the rare hours when the weather changes. The other ninety per cent of your working life is craft, and craft is just the next small thing, done as well as you can do it, again, and then again.

I have watched a great many careers come apart, and almost none of them came apart because the person lacked vision. They came apart because the person stopped doing the work in front of them properly. They started outsourcing the boring parts. They started signing things they had not read. They started saying yes in meetings where they should have said *let me come back to you*. They started, in small ways, being someone you could not entirely rely on.

It is very hard to recover from that, because reputation, in any trade or business, is built one small honoured promise at a time and lost one small dishonoured promise at a time, and the losing is much faster than the building.

So if you remember only one needle out of this chapter, remember this one. Do today's work, properly. Do not be heroic. Do not be visionary. Just be reliable, on the small thing, for long enough that people stop having to wonder whether you will be.

The rest of the compass is built on top of this needle. Without it, none of the others will hold their direction for you.

### Two. The metal does not lie. Listen to it.

This needle came to me through boilermaking, but it is not really about boilermaking. It is about the relationship between a person and the truth of their work.

In the trade, you have an honest teacher. A weld is good or it is not. A measurement is right or it is wrong. A bracket fits or it has to be cut and re-set. There is no boardroom in which you can talk a bad weld into being a good weld. The metal will tell you. It will tell you immediately, and it will tell you in front of witnesses, and it will keep telling you for as long as the thing you made is in service.

Most jobs, the further you go from the floor, lose this honest teacher. The signals get slower. The feedback gets political. By the time you are sitting in a director's seat, the difference between a good decision and a bad one may not show up for two years, and by then a dozen other decisions have been layered on top of it, and you can tell yourself almost any story you like about what happened. The metal has been replaced by mirrors.

The needle, then, is this: find your metal. In every role, there is something that does not lie to you, if you are willing to look at it. In production, it is the line. In sales, it is the order book and the cancellations. In finance, it is the cash position and the ageing. In people work, it is who stays, who leaves, and what they say on the way out the door. In your own life, it is your sleep, your spouse's face on a Sunday evening, and the look on the dog when you come home.

Find your metal. Read it weekly. Do not let yourself be talked out of what it is telling you by a slide deck. The further up you go, the more important this needle becomes, because the mirrors get larger and more flattering, and almost no one around you will be paid to tell you when the metal is screaming.

### Three. The title is a tool, not a verdict.

I have written a whole chapter on the office door and what it felt like to close it for the first time, and I will not repeat that here. But the principle distils to a single sentence, and it belongs on this compass.

A title is a description of where you have been placed in a system. That is all it is. It is not a measurement of your worth. It is not a promise about your competence. It is not a permanent feature of your identity. It is a job to learn, and a job to do, and one day a job to hand on. Treat it that way and it will serve you. Treat it as a verdict and it will either inflate you or hollow you out, often both.

The people I most respect, looking back across the working years, were almost without exception people whose sense of themselves did not depend on the line on the org chart. They could lose the title tomorrow and still know who they were. They could be promoted tomorrow and not become someone harder to live with. They wore the role like a uniform you put on at the start of the shift and hang up at the end. It was real while it was on. It was not them.

If you can hold that — which is harder than it sounds, especially in seasons when the role is going well and people are flattering you — you will be a steadier kind of leader than the average. You will also be much less likely to make the kind of decisions, when threatened, that come from defending a self-image rather than serving the work.

### Four. Promote the people who do the work, not the people who explain it.

I learned this one slowly and at some cost.

In any organisation past a certain size, two kinds of people start to separate out. There are the people who do the work — quietly, competently, often without much commentary — and there are the people who can explain the work in a meeting in a way that makes the room feel clever. These are not always the same people. In fact, after a certain point, they are very seldom the same people, because explaining the work brilliantly takes a particular kind of attention that, if you are spending on the explanation, you are not spending on the work.

For most of my early management years, I rewarded the explainers more than I meant to. I did not do it on purpose. I did it because the explainers were the people I saw most. They came to my meetings. They sent me confident emails. They were articulate in a way that, on a busy week, felt like competence. Meanwhile, the doers were on the floor or at their desks, finishing the actual job, and I was, without realising it, slowly tilting the rewards of the company towards the wrong end of the room.

The cost of this is not visible for a long time. Then, one year, you look up and find that the people who built the place are leaving, and the people who remain are very good at telling you, in great detail, why the place is no longer working.

The needle is this. Whenever you are making a decision about money, title, or trust, ask yourself who actually did the work this quarter. Not who talked about it. Not who took the meeting. Not who was in the room when it was praised. *Who did it.* Promote that person, or at minimum pay them properly, or at minimum thank them in a way they will remember.

If you do this consistently, your organisation will, over years, become heavier in the right places. The explainers will leave for somewhere that values them more. Let them. The doers will stay, because they have finally found a place that sees them. That is the only durable competitive advantage I have ever observed at close range.

### Five. Absorb anxiety; do not transmit it.

I gave this needle to you in an earlier chapter, in the context of the bad years, but it belongs on the compass because it applies to every season, not only the lean ones.

A leader who transmits their anxiety downwards turns one frightened person into many. A leader who absorbs it, and converts it into the small concrete tasks of the next week, turns the same fear into work. The fear does not disappear. It just stops being the operating system of the building.

This is not stoicism, and it is not pretending. The people around you are not fools. They can tell when you are worried. The discipline is not to hide the worry, which is impossible past a certain age. The discipline is to be the place where the worry stops being a feeling and starts being a plan.

In practice it looks like this. You go home. You feel everything you feel — properly, with someone who loves you, or alone in the car, or on a long walk where the dog does not require a coherent narrative from you. You let the worry move through. Then, in the morning, you come back in and you say to your people, *here is what we know, here is what we do not know, here is what we are doing this week, and here is the next thing I will tell you, and when.*

That last bit is the part most people skip. The next thing you will tell them, and when. Anxiety in a team is mostly the absence of a clear next promise. Give them the next promise, and keep it, and then the next one. They will follow you a long way for that. Much further than they will follow you for charisma.

### Six. Keep the routine. Especially when you do not want to.

This is the most boring needle on the compass and one of the most important.

A working life past forty is, at the level of the body, mostly a long argument with sleep, meals, and movement. The argument gets louder the higher you climb, because the calendar will eat anything you do not actively defend, and the role will, if you let it, expand to fill every hour of your day and most of the night.

The routine is the equipment that keeps you in the fight. I do not mean a routine in the influencer sense, with cold plunges and breath work and a particular brand of supplements [unverified, since I have not tried any of that and would not know]. I mean the basic, unglamorous, repeated structure of getting to bed at roughly the same hour, eating roughly the same kinds of food, moving your body for roughly the same amount of time, and protecting the small rituals that mark the start and end of your working day so that there is, in fact, a start and an end.

When the year goes badly, your instinct will be to abandon the routine because it feels frivolous against the size of the problem. Resist. The routine is not frivolous. It is exactly what allows you, six months into the bad year, to still be standing in front of your people with something useful to offer. The leaders who do not keep the routine, in my observation, do not last. They burn very brightly for a season and then they break, and the break is usually invisible from the outside until it is very visible indeed.

Eat. Sleep. Walk. Keep one day a week that is not the work. Pay this tax even — especially — when the work seems to demand otherwise. It is not weakness. It is maintenance. The machine that does not get maintained does not get to run for long, and you are, in this season, the machine.

### Seven. Tell the truth at the level they can use it.

I have spent a working life around plain-spoken people, and one of the gifts of that has been a deep suspicion of corporate language.

There is a particular kind of speech that creeps into organisations as they grow. It is the language of *cascading*, of *strategic alignment*, of *journeys* and *spaces* and *headwinds*. None of it is exactly lying. It is, more accurately, a fog that has been let into the room because the fog is more comfortable than the weather underneath it.

The needle is to tell the truth at the level the listener can actually use. Not the press release. Not the cash position to the last cent, which will only frighten them. The shape of the situation, in language they would use to their spouse over dinner. *We have lost a large customer. We will know within four weeks whether we replace the work or whether we cut. Here is what we are doing in the meantime. Here is what I am asking of you.*

This sounds easy. It is not. It is uncomfortable, because the moment you speak plainly, you can be held to it. The fog has the virtue, for the people generating it, of being unfalsifiable. Plain speech is not. Plain speech makes a promise.

But plain speech is also the only kind of speech that actually transfers information through an organisation. Fog gets reinterpreted at every level until, by the time it reaches the floor, it bears no relation to what was decided. Plain speech survives the journey. It also, over years, builds a particular kind of trust that no amount of polish can buy. People will follow a plain-spoken leader through a great deal of trouble, because they at least know what kind of trouble it is.

Speak plainly. Especially upwards, where the fog is thickest. Especially downwards, where the fog is most expensive.

### Eight. Remember what it felt like to be on the other side of your own desk.

I have said this elsewhere and I will say it again here, because it is the single most important habit of mind for anyone who has come up from the floor.

You were treated, at some point, as a number. You remember exactly what that did to your willingness to go the extra mile that day. You remember the manager in the clean shirt who did not know your name, and you remember the one who did, and you remember the difference. That memory is the most valuable management training you will ever receive, and it cost you nothing because it was paid for in advance by the years you spent earning it.

Do not lose it.

You will be tempted to lose it. The further you get from the floor, the more your social world fills up with people who never stood on one. They are not bad people. They are simply people whose data set does not include the experience of being managed badly from a distance. They will, in good faith, propose policies that look fine on paper and feel like punishment in practice. If you have kept the memory, you will catch these in time. If you have lost it, you will sign them, and a hundred small dignities will go out of the building over your signature, and you will not feel it because you are no longer the one paying the cost.

Keep the memory. Visit it deliberately. Walk the floor. Eat in the staff canteen sometimes, not as a gesture, but as a regular fact. Pick up the phone yourself occasionally. Park further from the building than you need to. None of this is theatre. It is calibration. You are keeping the instrument honest.

### Nine. Build people who can do without you.

I came to this needle late, and I almost missed it.

For most of my working life, I quietly believed that the highest form of management was indispensability. To be the one who, if you stepped out for a week, the place would feel it. To be the person whose judgement was the final word, whose presence in the meeting changed the room, whose absence was a problem. I do not think I ever said this out loud. I am not sure I would have admitted to thinking it. But it was, looking back, the operating assumption underneath a great deal of how I worked.

It is wrong. And it is, more importantly, a betrayal of the role.

The job of a leader, properly understood, is not to be indispensable. It is to be the kind of leader whose absence is no longer a problem, because the people you have built can carry the work without you. If you cannot take a month off and have the place run well, you have not built a team. You have built a dependency. And dependencies, when their object retires or falls ill or simply gets bored, collapse the place they were meant to be holding up.

The needle, then, is to spend a portion of every working week deliberately making yourself less necessary. Train your successor while you are still in the seat. Document the decisions only you know how to make. Push down the calls that only you have been making, even when it is faster to make them yourself, because faster is not the metric. The metric is *what happens here if I am hit by a bus.* If the answer is *the place falls over*, you have not done your job, however brilliantly you have done your job.

The leaders I have admired most, looking back, were the ones whose departure barely caused a tremor. Not because they were unimportant. Because they had spent their years pouring their importance into other people, so that when they left, the importance was already distributed, and the place kept running on the strength of it. That is, in the end, the only legacy worth chasing.

### Ten. Pay back what you borrowed.

This is the last needle, and the one I want you to leave the book with.

No one gets where I have got without borrowing. I borrowed time from my family on every late shift. I borrowed patience from older tradesmen who could have closed the door on the awkward apprentice and did not. I borrowed chances from foremen and managers who put their name next to mine when I had not yet earned it. I borrowed years of training from a country, an industry, and a handful of companies that fed and housed me while I was learning to be useful.

You will borrow too. Every career is, in this sense, a long loan, drawn in small instalments from the people and places around you. The interesting question is not whether you are borrowing. You are. The interesting question is what you do about it later.

The honourable answer is that you pay it back. Not symbolically. In the actual currency in which it was lent. If older tradesmen took a chance on you, take a chance on a younger one now. If a foreman put his reputation next to yours, put yours next to someone else's. If a company paid for your apprenticeship while you were learning, train the next apprentice properly when it is your turn to sign the cheque. If your family carried the cost of your career, carry some of theirs now, with whatever you have left of your time and your attention.

This is not a sentimental point. It is a structural one. The whole arrangement that allowed me to walk from the shop floor to the managing director's office only worked because each generation, more or less, paid back what it had borrowed from the one before. Where that chain breaks, the route I walked closes behind me. The next apprentice does not get the chances I got, because the older tradesmen no longer have the time, or the company no longer has the patience, or the country no longer has the apprenticeship system at all [unverified, in any specific policy sense — but anyone who has been near industrial training in the past two decades knows what I am pointing at].

I do not want that route to close. I have written this book partly because I do not want it to close. So this last needle is not only for you. It is, in a way, the asking of a small favour.

Pay back what you borrowed. Then a little more, if you can.

## Using the compass

I have given you ten needles. That is too many for a working morning. No one consults a checklist before answering an email. So let me tell you, plainly, how I actually use this thing.

Most days, I use one of them. The one that the day in front of me happens to need. If I am about to make a hiring decision, I lean on the one about doers versus explainers. If I am walking into a frightened meeting, I lean on absorbing anxiety. If I am tired and tempted to skip the gym, I lean on keep the routine. If I am tempted to soften bad news into language no one can act on, I lean on tell the truth at the level they can use it.

On the rarer days when the weather changes — a real crisis, a real opportunity, a real decision about the next ten years — I sit with all ten. Not in a list. In a quiet half-hour, usually early in the morning, with a cup of coffee and the door closed, the way I learned to close the door deliberately in the office where I first sat as a manager. I ask, in turn, what each needle is telling me about the situation in front of me. Not all of them will speak. Most of them will just point in the same direction, which is itself a kind of answer. The one or two that disagree are the ones to listen to most carefully, because disagreement among honest instruments is information.

If I cannot find a settled direction even after all of that, I do the most uncomfortable thing on the compass, which is to wait. Not forever. Not as an evasion. But until at least one more piece of metal has told me something true. Almost every bad decision I have ever made, looking back, was made before the metal had finished speaking. The few good ones I am proud of were made after I had been patient enough to listen.

That is the whole technique. There is no more to it than that.

## A short word about the chair you have not yet sat in

I want to say one thing to the reader who is not yet anywhere near a director's seat, because I do not want this last chapter to read as if it were only for people who already have one.

If you are still on the floor, or in the middle, or at the start of supervising your first crew, you may be wondering whether any of this compass is for you yet. It is. All of it. None of these needles wait for a title to switch on. The apprentice who does the work in front of him properly is already practising the first needle. The young supervisor who listens to the metal — the actual production line, the actual customer complaints, the actual look on the team's faces — is already practising the second. The charge-hand who tells the truth at the level his crew can use is already practising the seventh. You do not need to wait to be promoted to live this way. In fact, the people I have seen rise furthest are the ones who started living this way long before anyone in authority noticed, simply because it was the right way to be in the work.

The title, when it comes, will only amplify what is already there. If you have built the habits, the title will land on prepared ground and the role will fit you in a way that surprises you. If you have not, no amount of training course or executive coaching [unverified — I am sceptical of most of it, but I have not been through a great deal of it personally] will install in two weekends what should have been installed over twenty years.

So start now. Wherever you are. The compass works the same in a fitter's bay as it does behind a desk. The needles do not care about your job title. They only care whether you are willing to look at them when the day gets confusing.

## A charge to the next apprentice

I want to finish the book where I started it, which was on a shop floor at six o'clock in the morning, with a young man who had no real plan beyond the next shift.

I do not know who you are, but you are out there. You are standing in some equivalent of that shed right now — perhaps in a real workshop, perhaps in a warehouse, perhaps in a call centre, perhaps in a kitchen, perhaps in a place I would not even recognise as work — and you are wondering whether anything you do over the next forty years is going to add up to anything in particular.

I am telling you, from this end of the walk, that it can.

Not by accident. Not by luck, although you will need some of that and most of us have had more of it than we admit. It will add up because you keep doing the work in front of you properly, for long enough, and because you let the metal teach you, and because you remember what it felt like to be on the other side of your own desk by the time it is your desk, and because you pay back what you borrow, and because, on enough of the hard days, you keep showing up.

That is the whole of it. There is no other secret. If there were, I promise you, someone would have sold it to me by now [unverified, but the offers have certainly arrived in the post often enough].

The compass I have given you is small and brass and slightly unreliable, like the one in my drawer. It will not navigate for you. It will only point. You will still have to do the walking, in weather I cannot predict, towards a horizon I am not going to live to see.

But you have everything you need. You have hands that can be taught. You have a back that, at your age, will recover from almost anything. You have time, which is the currency none of us spends well early on and all of us want more of later. You have the freedom, every morning, to choose how you are going to be in the work that day, regardless of what the work is.

Use it.

And when, in some year I will not be here to see, you find yourself in a seat you did not entirely plan to occupy, with people you are responsible for and very few people left above you who will tell you the truth, I want you to do one last thing.

Open a drawer. Find whatever small, ordinary, slightly unreliable instrument you have kept by you on the walk. Put it on the desk where you can see it. Sit in the chair. Close the door, deliberately, the way a tool is closed.

And start again. With the next small thing in front of you. Properly.

That has been the whole of my working life. It is, I now believe, the whole of the lesson.

Welcome to the trade.
