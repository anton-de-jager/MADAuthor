# Men Who Took a Chance

There is a kind of debt that does not appear on any ledger, and it is the only kind I have ever found genuinely difficult to repay.

It is the debt you owe to the people who looked at you, early on, when you were not yet much to look at, and decided, for reasons that were never fully explained to you at the time, that you were worth the bother. They could have ignored you. Most people did. They could have given you the easy answer, the polite nod, the *ja, well, you will get there in your own time, my boy* that is the workshop equivalent of being patted on the head and sent away. Many of the older men I worked alongside in those first years did exactly that, and there was nothing wrong with them for it. They had their own work to do, their own crews to look after, their own tired bodies to get home in one piece at the end of a shift.

But a small handful of them did something different. They stopped what they were doing. They turned around. They looked at the apprentice in front of them properly, the way you might look at a workpiece you were not sure about, and they made a small, deliberate decision to invest a piece of their time and their reputation in him. They had nothing obvious to gain by it. They were not paid for it. Nobody in management would ever know they had done it. They did it anyway.

This chapter is about those men. What they did. Why I think they did it. And what, slowly, over years, I came to understand I owed them in return.

## The shape of mentorship on the floor

I want to start by saying what mentorship was not, in the world I came up in, because the word has been hollowed out lately by the kind of corporate language that turns everything into a programme.

It was not a scheme. Nobody had been **assigned** to me. There were no quarterly check-ins. There was no document somewhere with my **development plan** on it, signed by both parties and filed for review. If you had walked up to one of the men I am about to describe and called him my *mentor*, he would have looked at you as if you had asked him whether he was my therapist. The word would have embarrassed him. The thing itself would not have. The thing itself was just something he did, in the gaps between the work, because that was what older tradesmen did for the apprentices who looked like they were going to stick.

Mentorship on a working-class shop floor, in my experience, looked like four or five small things repeated over years.

It looked like a hand on your shoulder, briefly, before you were about to do something stupid. *Not that way, my boy. Come here a minute.* You would come over. He would not lecture you. He would show you the right way, once, in about ninety seconds, and then he would walk off and let you try it yourself. He would not stand over you. He would not check that you had got it. He had trusted you to absorb the correction and he had moved on. The dignity of that small choreography is something I have never quite seen reproduced in any classroom.

It looked like being given the next harder job a little before you were ready for it. You would be on a routine task, sweating through it, and the foreman would walk past, glance at your work, and say, almost to no one in particular, *when you are done with that, come and have a look at this one with me*. The job he had in mind was, you discovered later, slightly above your level. He had decided you could handle it. He had not asked you. He had not made a fuss. He had just opened a door that he was perfectly capable of leaving closed, and he had walked through it ahead of you to make sure you followed.

It looked like being told the truth about your work in a way nobody else was going to bother to tell you. *That weld is rubbish. Grind it out and do it again. Stop holding the rod like you are afraid of it.* The sentences were short. They did not soften anything. But they also did not contain any contempt. They contained, instead, an assumption that you were capable of hearing them and getting better, which is the highest compliment one tradesman can pay another. Contempt comes when somebody has decided you cannot improve. Honesty comes when they have decided you can.

It looked like being defended, very quietly, when somebody more senior was being unreasonable about you. You would not always know it had happened. You would find out, years later, in an offhand remark at a funeral or a retirement party. *Did you know old So-and-So told the engineer to leave you alone that time, said you were doing fine and the engineer was the problem?* You would not have known. The old man had not told you. He had not wanted the credit. He had simply put his own standing on the line, for you, in a room you were not in, and gone home and said nothing about it.

And it looked like being, somehow, just there. In the canteen. At the locker. Outside in the smoke break you did not yet smoke. You would find yourself standing next to him without any obvious reason, and a small conversation would happen, and a small piece of information would pass from him to you, and the conversation would end as casually as it had begun. Over a year, those small conversations added up to an education that no one could have planned in advance because none of its participants were quite aware that it was happening.

That was mentorship. Not a programme. A texture.

## The first portrait — Oom Hennie

I am going to give you portraits of three of the men, because abstractions about mentorship are not worth much without faces.

The first I want to tell you about, I will call Oom Hennie. That was not quite his name. His name was longer and the version everyone used in the shop was shorter, but Hennie is close enough and respectful enough for what I want to do here. He had been a boilermaker for, I think, somewhere north of thirty-five years by the time I met him. His hands looked like they had been carved out of something harder than the rest of him. He was not tall. He was not loud. He had a way of looking at a piece of work that made younger men go quiet without him having said a word.

I had been at the firm for about three months when he first spoke to me directly. I had been struggling with a layout on a small pressure vessel head — the kind of geometry that looks straightforward on the drawing and turns into a small private nightmare on the steel — and I had been redoing my marks for what was probably the fourth time when his shadow fell across my workpiece.

He did not say hello. He did not introduce himself. He had been three bays away and I had not known he was watching.

He said, *Show me what you did.*

I showed him. I expected to be torn into. I had seen him tear into other apprentices, in a flat unsmiling way that did not raise his voice and did not need to.

He looked at the steel. He looked at the drawing. He looked at me. He said, *Your method is wrong. Your maths is right. Come stand here.* And he showed me, in about three minutes, a way of laying out that piece that I have used, with very small variations, for the rest of my career. He used a piece of chalk. He drew three lines. He made one small remark about why the way I had been taught it in the trade test was theoretically correct but practically slow, and he did not say a word against the instructors who had taught me. He simply offered a better way and let me decide what to do with it.

Then he walked back to his bay and did not speak to me again that day, or the next.

It was about a fortnight later that I worked out what he had done. He had not just shown me a technique. He had given me permission to think about the work, rather than simply execute the work. He had quietly let me in on something that the trade does not tell young men loudly enough: that the methods you are taught are starting points, not commandments, and that part of becoming a real tradesman is learning when to vary from them and when not to. He had trusted me with that distinction before I had any right to it.

I started watching him after that. Not in a stalking way. In the way young men watch older men they have begun, despite themselves, to want to learn from. I watched how he held himself in meetings with engineers. I watched how he spoke to younger apprentices, including ones less promising than I was. I watched how he behaved when a job went wrong, which was almost the most instructive of all. He did not look for someone to blame. He looked for what had failed in the process, and then he fixed the process, and then, separately and privately, if a person needed to be spoken to, he spoke to them out of earshot. The thing he never did was create a public spectacle around a mistake. I have seen many managers since, with degrees and titles he did not have, fail that basic test of decency, and I have never once seen Oom Hennie fail it.

Years later, when I was running my own crews, I had occasion to make a hard call on a job that was over budget and over time, and I caught myself, in the meeting, about to do exactly the wrong thing — about to assign blame in the room. I stopped. I do not know why I stopped. I think a small, dry voice somewhere in the back of my head said something like *not in front of them, my boy*. I closed the conversation, moved the meeting on, and spoke to the person privately at the end of the day, the way he would have.

That is what mentorship does to you. It plants a voice in you that you will hear for the rest of your working life, often when you least expect it, almost always at exactly the moment you were about to do something you would have regretted.

## The second portrait — the foreman who put me on the lift

The second man I want to tell you about was a foreman. I am going to leave him nameless, because the story is not flattering to the people he worked for at the time, and he had children who still live in the town.

He was younger than Oom Hennie. Maybe fifteen years older than me. He had come up through the trade in a different shop and had been brought in to run the day shift after some kind of dispute with the previous foreman that nobody on the floor was ever told the details of. He was an outsider, in other words, in a workshop that did not warm to outsiders quickly.

He warmed to me before he had any reason to.

I had been there for about a year and a half. I was no longer the rawest of the apprentices, but I was not yet trusted with anything that mattered. The work I was being given was repetitive, safe, low-risk. I was beginning to feel the dullness that creeps into apprentices at that stage of the journey, when the novelty of the trade has worn off and the satisfaction of mastery has not yet arrived. It is a dangerous middle. A lot of good young men leave the trade in that middle, because nobody has thought to challenge them and they do not yet have the language to ask for a challenge themselves.

He noticed.

I do not know how he noticed. He cannot have been watching me closely. He had a whole shift to run, with its own crises and its own characters, and I was one apprentice among several. But one morning, a job came in that needed a small crew sent up onto a high lift to do some patch work on a structure that was not, strictly, my level. It was a senior tradesman's job, with a competent assistant. He picked me as the assistant.

The senior tradesman he had picked to work with was, I should tell you, not an easy man. He had a temper and a tongue and a very low tolerance for apprentices who slowed him down. Pairing me with him on the lift was, in one reading, a deliberate cruelty. In another, it was the foreman's quiet way of testing whether I could hold my own in a hard situation with a hard man, in a place where the consequences of a mistake would be physical rather than theoretical.

I was terrified. I was also, almost immediately, alert in a way I had not been for months. The dullness of the middle vanished within five minutes of stepping onto that platform. The senior man barked at me twice in the first hour, both times deservedly. He stopped barking by the end of the day. He even said, as we came down, *not bad, china. Tomorrow we do the south side.*

We did not become friends. He went back to being his ordinary difficult self on the next job. But the foreman who had put us together had moved something in my standing in the shop that did not move back. I had been seen, by a man whose opinion the rest of the floor respected, working at a level above the one I had been kept at. The next job I was assigned to was harder than the previous one. The one after that, harder still. None of this was announced. None of it was framed as a promotion or a development pathway. It was simply that the doors began to open, one after another, in a sequence the foreman had quietly designed, and I walked through them.

About six months later, in a corridor, I tried to thank him for it. I was clumsy about it. I was a young man and I did not have the vocabulary for what I was trying to say, and the words came out as something close to *thanks for, you know, the lift job and the others*.

He looked at me as if I was being slightly ridiculous, which I was. He said, *I did not do you a favour. I put you somewhere I thought you could be useful. If you had not been useful, I would have moved you. Do not make it more than it was.*

And then, because he could see I was still standing there, he said, more quietly, *but it is the right kind of question to ask. Just ask it less often. Get on with the next thing.*

I have thought about those two sentences a lot. *Do not make it more than it was.* He was protecting both of us, I think now, from a kind of sentimentality that would have made the thing harder to repeat. If the next apprentice he gave a chance to had to come and thank him every time, he would have stopped doing it. The pact, unspoken, was that you took the chance, you used it, and you went and gave somebody else a chance later when you were in a position to do so. That was the price. That was the only price. You did not have to feel grateful in any way you would have to perform. You just had to keep the chain going.

I have tried, in the years since, to keep it going. I will tell you, before this chapter ends, about a young man I think I may have done it for. I cannot be sure. The point of doing it is that you do not get to be sure.

## The third portrait — the charge-hand who did not give up on me

The third man I want to tell you about is, in some ways, the most important, because he gave me the kind of chance that the others did not have to give — the chance to stay in the trade at all.

I had reached a point, somewhere in my second-to-last apprentice year, when I had begun to wonder whether I was going to make it through. I will be honest: I was not the most natural tradesman of my intake. I had hands that learned, but they learned a little slower than the best of the apprentices around me. I had a head for the maths, which helped, but heads do not weld plate. There was a stretch of about four months when I produced a run of work that was, frankly, mediocre, and I could feel the older men beginning to make the small, careful adjustments they made around an apprentice who they had quietly decided was not going to come right.

The charge-hand on my section at the time was a man I will call Sipho, although that was not his name either. He was a generation older than me, had come into the trade by a much harder road than mine, and had a reputation for being a slightly stern, slightly distant figure who got the work done and did not say very much that was not strictly necessary.

He did something, in those four months, that I have spent decades trying to understand.

He did not encourage me. He did not pull me aside and tell me I had it in me and that I should just keep going. He did not give me speeches. What he did, instead, was put me on every difficult job he could find, with the kind of expectation that I would manage them, and let me struggle in front of him without rescuing me.

It was the opposite of giving up on me. From the outside it might have looked harsh, and on bad days it felt harsh from the inside too. But what he was communicating, by his behaviour rather than his words, was this: *I do not believe you are bad. I believe you are stuck. I am going to keep treating you like a man who is going to come right, because that is the only treatment that gives a stuck man a chance of coming right. If I start treating you like a man who is not going to make it, you will not make it. So I am not going to do that, even when the evidence is against you.*

I have never had this confirmed by him. He died, some years ago, before I ever had the wits or the courage to sit down with him and ask. But I am almost certain that this was what he was doing, because I have since done it, deliberately, with one or two of my own people who hit the same kind of trough, and it works for the same reason. A struggling person who is treated as a struggling person becomes a failing person. A struggling person who is treated as a competent person who is having a hard month often, not always but often, finds their feet again.

In my case I found them. The fourth month passed. The work began, slowly, to come back. By the time I qualified, I was once more solidly in the middle of my intake, and Sipho was solidly in the upper end of the older men whose opinion of my work I most cared about. He never said a word to me about the bad stretch. He never alluded to it. He treated it, after the fact, as if it had not happened, and that too I have come to understand is part of the gift. He had decided that the bad months were not who I was, and he was not going to be the one to make them part of my permanent record.

I owe him, in many ways, the existence of the rest of this book.

## What I came to understand they had in common

After many years, when I had spent enough time on the other side of the relationship to begin to see the structure of it, I started to notice a pattern in the men who had taken a chance on me.

They were not, on the whole, the most famous tradesmen in the shop. The famous ones, the ones with the reputations, were usually too busy being famous to spend time on apprentices. The ones who actually did the work of bringing young men along were, almost without exception, of a slightly quieter sort. They were respected, but they were not lionised. They produced excellent work, but they did not draw attention to it. They were sometimes, in fact, slightly underused by the official structure of the shop, which had a way of not noticing the people who did the steady, useful, non-spectacular things.

They had each, I think, been mentored themselves in roughly the same way. None of them said this out loud, but the patterns of their behaviour bore the marks of someone older having done the same things for them. Oom Hennie's quick, dignified correction was not a personal invention. It was something he had received, and was passing on. The foreman's quiet placement of an apprentice on a harder job than he was strictly ready for was something he had clearly seen done. Sipho's stubborn refusal to give up on a stuck young man almost certainly came from somewhere in his own history, in a place none of us were ever told about. The chain went back further than any of them, and forward further than any of us would live to see.

They did not, ever, expect to be thanked properly. The clumsy attempt I had made in the corridor with the foreman was probably the high-water mark of how any of them was ever thanked by me when they were alive. They would have been embarrassed by anything more, and they would have suspected anything less. The transaction they were running was not really with me. It was with the trade itself, and possibly with the older men who had done the same for them. I was a beneficiary of the transaction, but I was not exactly a party to it.

And they were, almost without exception, harder on themselves than on the people they were bringing along. This is the part I would most like to underline for any reader who is sitting in the position they were in and wondering whether they are getting it right. The men I most respected as mentors did not have a high opinion of their own skill at it. They thought, mostly, that they were just getting on with the work. They would have been astonished to be told that they were the difference between an apprentice making it and not making it. They did not see themselves that way at all. They were, in their own minds, simply the next man up the line, doing what the man up the line from them had done for him. That modesty, I think now, is one of the reasons the thing worked. The moment somebody starts to think of themselves as a great mentor, they almost always become a worse one. The role does not respond well to self-consciousness. It responds to the kind of attention that is mostly directed at the work, and at the person, and almost not at all at the relationship.

## What the apprentice owes back

I have been writing as if the only direction of obligation runs from the apprentice towards the older man. That is not how I now think of it. There is a debt that runs the other way too, and it is easier to overlook because no one ever bills you for it.

The apprentice owes the man who took a chance on him three things, none of which the older man will ever ask for.

He owes him, first, the seriousness of effort. The greatest insult you can pay to somebody who has invested their time in you is to be casual about it. If a senior tradesman has spent twenty minutes showing you something he did not need to show you, and you go away and treat the lesson as one among many, equal in weight to the time you spent loitering at the coffee urn, you have wasted both your time and his. You owe him at least the dignity of taking the gift as a gift. That means trying the technique. Trying it more than once. Coming back, if it does not work, and saying *I tried this, this and this, and it is not landing — what am I missing?* That sentence is the apprentice's primary obligation in the relationship. It is not gratitude. It is engagement.

He owes him, second, the discipline of not leaning. There is a temptation, when someone takes you under their wing, to want them to do it more and more often. To make yourself dependent. To go and find them every time you have a small problem you could have worked out by yourself. This is, I have come to believe, a serious failure of the apprentice's side of the bargain. The whole point of the mentorship is to make you into a man who can stand on his own. If you use it to make yourself into a man who cannot stand without his mentor, you have not understood the gift, and you have made yourself into a heavier and heavier burden on a man who has plenty of other work to do. The apprentice's job, increasingly as the years go on, is to need the mentor less, not more. The good mentors I have known were quietly pleased when an apprentice stopped coming to ask them things, because that meant the work had taken. They were never offended by it.

And he owes him, third, the obligation to pass it forward. This is the part that is non-negotiable, and the part I have come to think of as the actual repayment of the original debt. The senior man did not invest in you for your benefit alone. He invested in you because that is how the trade survives across generations. If you receive the gift and do not, in turn, become the kind of older man who passes it on to somebody coming up behind you, you have broken a chain that has, in most cases, been running for longer than you have been alive. You do not get to opt out of this. It is the only price the older man ever charged, and he did not even charge it openly, because he assumed you would understand.

I have not always lived up to this third obligation. I want to be honest about that. There were years, particularly in the early management period I will describe in later chapters, when I was so busy and so unsure of my own footing that I let several young men pass under my nose without giving them the kind of attention the older men had given me. I look back on that with some shame. Not enormous shame. I was not a monster. I just did not yet realise that the work of bringing somebody along did not require an enormous block of time and a formal arrangement. It required, mostly, attention. A two-minute correction in the right place. A harder job offered at the right moment. The refusal to give up on somebody who was in a bad month.

When I started, deliberately, to do those small things again, in the way the older men had done them for me, I felt, for the first time, that I had begun to repay the debt properly. Not by saying thank you to Oom Hennie at his funeral, although I did that too, and meant it. By behaving, in front of a younger man, the way Oom Hennie would have behaved if he had been in the room.

## The one I think I may have done it for

I want to end with one short story, because everything I have said so far has been about the people who did it for me, and the chapter would be incomplete without at least a glance at the other side.

There was a young man, some years after I had become a charge-hand myself, who arrived at the shop as an apprentice and was, very obviously, going to struggle. He had the right intelligence. He had the wrong confidence. He had a way of pulling back from a job at exactly the moment when he should have been leaning into it, and the older men around him had begun, within about a fortnight, to make the small adjustments around him that meant they had quietly decided he was not going to come right.

I watched this happening, and I recognised it, and I thought, *I have seen this story before. From the other side.*

I did not give him a speech. I did not pull him aside and tell him I believed in him. He would have been suspicious of either, and he would have been right to be suspicious. What I did, instead, was put him on harder jobs than he was strictly ready for, with the steady expectation that he would manage them, and let him struggle in front of me without rescuing him. I corrected him quickly when he was wrong. I did not draw attention to his mistakes in front of the others. I assumed, openly, that he was a competent young man who was having a hard month.

He came right. Not immediately. Not dramatically. Over the course of about a year, the slumped shoulders straightened, the work got better, the confidence began to catch up to the intelligence. By the time he qualified, he was a solid mid-pack tradesman with a future, and within a few years after that he was beginning to be noticed for the kind of steady, useful, non-spectacular work that the older men in any shop instinctively know how to value.

I have never told him what I was doing. I do not intend to. I do not even know, for certain, that what I was doing was the difference. He may have come right anyway. He may have had his own Sipho I did not know about, somewhere in his life outside the shop, holding the line for him in a way I never saw.

But I think, on balance, that the chain did pass through me on its way to him. That is the best I can say, and the best I think any of us can say about this kind of work. You do not get to be sure. You only get to behave, in front of the next person, the way you wish someone had behaved in front of you, and to trust that, somewhere down the line, the same thing will be done by them for somebody else.

That is, I have come to believe, the only honest repayment of the debt I started this chapter with. You cannot pay it back to the men who gave it to you. Most of them are dead now, and the ones who are not would refuse the gesture. You can only pay it forward, in the same small unannounced way it was paid to you, and you will not get a receipt for it either.

## A short list, for the apprentice still on the floor

If you are reading this as a young person, somewhere in the middle of the trade or the early years of any work that demands a craft, I want to leave you with a few things that I wish someone had said to me plainly when I was where you are.

- **Notice who is watching.** Most of the men around you are not watching. A handful are. The handful matters more than the rest combined. Behave, every day, as if those people are in the room, because some days they are and you do not always know which days.
- **Do not chase the loud men.** The tradesmen who will teach you the most are almost never the loudest ones. They are usually quieter than they should be for the quality of their work. Stand near them. Hand them tools. Ask short, useful questions. Go back to your bench and try what they told you.
- **Take the harder job when it is offered.** It will not always be offered explicitly. Sometimes it will just be left lying around in front of you to see if you pick it up. Pick it up. If you cannot manage it, you will learn that quickly enough. If you can, you will have opened the next door without anybody having to open it for you.
- **Do not lean on your mentors.** Need them less, not more, as the months go by. The relationship is meant to make you into someone who can stand on his own. If you find yourself going to the same man for every small thing, you have not understood the gift, and you are wasting a piece of his life.
- **Keep the chain.** When you become the older man, do for the next apprentice what the older men did for you. Not with a programme. Not with a speech. With the small, almost invisible behaviours that move a young person forward without his quite knowing how. The trade has survived this long because that chain has held. It survives the next generation because of you.

And remember, on the days when you cannot quite see why somebody more senior bothered with you: it was not, in the end, about you. It was about the work, and the people who would come after you, and a quiet promise that older tradesmen have been making to younger ones for longer than any of us has been alive. You are part of that promise now. The men who took a chance on you did so on the assumption that you would keep it.

Keep it.

The next chapter is about the day they finally let me write *tradesman* after my name, and the small, dangerous mistake that almost all of us make in the first year of that title — the mistake of believing that the ticket in our pocket is the destination, rather than the starting line. The men in this chapter would have laughed, gently, at any of us who thought that. Most of them, by then, had been tradesmen for thirty years, and were still, in their own quiet way, apprentices to the work.
