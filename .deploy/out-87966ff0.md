# Transforming SA Oil Mills: The First Blueprint

There is a particular moment in a career when a habit you have been practising for thirty years quietly turns into a method. You do not notice it on the day it happens. You notice it later, sitting on a flight home or walking out of a board meeting that has gone unusually well, and you realise that the thing you used to feel your way through, plant by plant and problem by problem, has organised itself, without your asking, into something repeatable. SA Oil Mills was that moment for me. It was not the largest assignment of my working life, and it was not the most lucrative. It was the one in which the playbook I had been assembling, piece by piece since the cyanide plants of AECI and the validation files of Adcock Ingram, finally became a blueprint I could read aloud.

I came to it sideways. The severance from Kolosus had returned the family to Gauteng. I had spent a few weeks doing what every transformation man does between contracts — drinking tea I had not had time to drink for years, walking the dog of an afternoon, and waiting, with a discipline I had learned from Antonette more than from any of my employers, for the right phone call rather than the next phone call. The right call came from a senior man inside the Tiger Brands group. There was a joint venture, he said, that needed someone who could see a business plainly and act on what he saw. Tiger Brands and Senwes had taken equal shares in an edible-oils operation called SA Oil Mills. The asset was respectable on paper. The economics were not. Would I come and meet the board.

I went and met them. By the end of that morning I had agreed, in principle, to take responsibility for the operation, and I had agreed, more importantly, on the terms by which I would be measured. The two shareholders wanted a transformation, but they also wanted a clean answer to a question neither of them had quite formulated yet. Was this asset, in its current shape, worth keeping inside their respective portfolios at all. My job was to make it good enough that the question could be answered honestly, in either direction, on a foundation of operational reality rather than on the rumours of a tired plant.

## The Two-Week Blue Paper

I have a habit I have followed at every company I have ever joined, and I have written about it elsewhere in this book. In the first two weeks I do not write a single email asking anyone for a presentation. I do not call for the strategy decks the previous management team prepared. I do not sit in the office reading historical board minutes, although I will read them later. I walk. I stand on the line next to the operators. I watch a crushing press for an hour without speaking. I count the small stoppages, the micro-changeovers, the quiet inefficiencies that nobody on the original team has noticed because they have been looking at them for so long. I have lunch in the canteen with the maintenance fitters. I read the breakdown reports. I walk the warehouse. I drive the perimeter of the property.

I gave myself two weeks to do that at SA Oil Mills, and at the end of the two weeks I wrote what I have called, ever since, a blue paper. The colour is not symbolic. The original document was simply printed on the blue stock of a printer that had been left in the corner of the office I had inherited, and the name stuck. A blue paper, in my private vocabulary, is the document a transformation man writes to himself first and to his shareholders second. It tells the truth about what is in front of him, in language a board can understand, without any of the dressing consultants are paid to add. It is short. It is honest. It is testable.

The blue paper for SA Oil Mills, when I had written it, ran to no more than a dozen pages. It said, in summary, what the previous teams had not been willing to say in print. The operation was carrying three sets of nearly everything. Three crushing routes for the seed coming in. Two refining streams that did very similar work to one another. Multiple packing lines arranged for a product mix that had not, in truth, existed for several years. The fixed cost of running all that duplicated capacity was draining the contribution of the variable margin. The plant was not failing because the people were poor — the people were, as it happened, the best part of the operation. The plant was failing because nobody had ever been given the authority, or the courage, to rationalise its own footprint down to one of each.

That was the central recommendation of the blue paper. One crushing line. One refining stream. One packing flow. The rest mothballed, sold, or repurposed, in that order of preference. Around the rationalisation, a focused capital programme of about ten million rand to automate the lines that would remain, so that the labour-intensive workarounds the operation had been living on for years could be removed at the same time as the duplicate capacity was taken out. And around the capital programme, an ethical retrenchment process, written out in detail before the first letter was signed, that would honour the people whose jobs the rationalisation was going to remove.

I took the blue paper to the joint shareholders' meeting two weeks and one day after I had walked through the gate. I read it to them aloud. I answered their questions for an hour. They gave me the mandate.

## Rationalising Down to One of Each

The work that followed was not, in its mechanics, exotic. It was the same work I had done in smaller doses at Adcock Ingram and in different forms at Kolosus, applied now to an operation that needed it across every step of its value chain at once.

We chose the best of the crushing lines and committed to it. The decision was not a beauty contest. We measured each line on a small set of variables that mattered — yield off the seed, reliability over the previous twenty-four months read from the breakdown reports, energy consumption per ton, and the cost and feasibility of bringing it up to the standard we wanted to operate at once the duplicates were gone. The line that scored best on that combined picture became the line we would run. The other two were stopped, in sequence, on dates we wrote into the project plan and held to.

The refining decision followed the same logic, and the packing flow followed it again. There is no point, in a thin-margin business, in keeping two pieces of capital running because the organisation is sentimental about a particular machine, or because a particular supervisor has spent his career on a particular line. The kindest thing a leader can do, in that situation, is to choose quickly, communicate clearly, and not let the decision drift. Drift is what destroys morale. Decision, properly explained, is what restores it.

While the rationalisation was being executed on the shop floor, I was running, in parallel, the capital case for the automation. The R10-million capex was modest by the standards of the projects I had run at Adcock Ingram and would later run at Tiger Brands, but every rand of it had to earn its return on a clear payback. We targeted the steps where the labour-intensive workarounds were doing the most damage — the manual handling on the front of the packing flow, the changeover routines that were eating an hour off every shift, the inspection points where a missed defect was going straight into a consumer pack. I specified the equipment myself. I procured it the way I had been taught to procure equipment at AECI, with a tender process that put the suppliers on the same page about what we needed and asked them to compete on engineering rather than on the size of the lunch they could buy us. We installed the new equipment in scheduled outages that did not lose the operation a single day of production it could not afford to lose.

The automation, when it was commissioned, did exactly what the business case had said it would. The hours-per-ton came down. The reject rate on the packing flow came down with it. The single crushing line, freed of the burden of running two of its siblings alongside it, ran at the kind of utilisation a board could be proud of. The single refining stream did the same. The operation, six months after the blue paper had been read aloud, looked like a different business.

## The Ethical Retrenchment

I want to write carefully about the retrenchment process, because it was the most important piece of the whole transformation, and it is the piece I am, even now, the most careful to do justice to.

A rationalisation of the kind I had recommended in the blue paper cannot be done without a reduction in headcount. To pretend otherwise — to claim, in the language some restructurings use, that there will be no impact on people, or that affected staff will be redeployed into roles that do not in fact exist — is a particular form of dishonesty I have refused to participate in throughout my career. If you are going to take out a crushing line, you are going to take out the jobs that ran it. The honest question is not whether the impact exists, but how the impact is handled.

We handled it the way I have insisted on handling it at every operation I have ever led. We wrote the entire process down before we acted. We engaged the unions and the shop stewards at the start of the project rather than at the end. We costed an enhanced separation package that was meaningfully better than the legal minimum, on the basis that the operation could afford it and that the people who had built that operation deserved it. We set up retraining and outplacement support for those who wanted it. We offered, where the skills allowed, internal redeployment into the roles that the remaining capacity would still need. We gave realistic notice, not the kind of compressed notice that simply leaves families with a shorter runway. And I made a point, as I have made the same point at every operation since, of being personally present in the room when the difficult conversations happened. The man who has authorised the retrenchment should not, in my view, be at his desk while another manager carries the letter.

There were people on that site who, despite all of that preparation, found the news very hard. I want to be honest about that too. An ethical retrenchment is not a comfortable retrenchment. It is one in which the discomfort is at least carried by the right people, in the right order, with the right respect. Several of the men and women who left the site that quarter wrote to me afterwards. A few asked for personal references, which I gave without hesitation, having always held the view that a person who has worked well for me does not deserve a lesser reference because the role they worked in has been engineered out from underneath them. One or two of those references opened doors at other operations in the same town. I think of those letters, on the days I find myself doubting whether the hard decisions of a transformation life have been worth the weight they have carried, more often than I think of the savings the rationalisation delivered.

The retrenchment process, when it was complete, was audited by both shareholders. It passed without a single legal challenge. It passed, more importantly, without a single piece of the local resentment a careless retrenchment leaves behind for years. The plant that emerged on the other side of the process was smaller in headcount than the one I had walked into. It was also, and I say this without sentimentality, a healthier place to work.

## The Exit to Continental Oils

By the time the rationalisation was bedded in, the automation was running, and the financials had spent two consecutive quarters in territory the board could read without flinching, the question I had been hired to answer had answered itself. The asset was worth keeping inside somebody's portfolio. It was not, however, obvious that the somebody had to be the Tiger Brands and Senwes joint venture. Edible oils, as a category, sat awkwardly between the food-brands logic Tiger Brands was built on and the grain-handling logic Senwes was built on. The strategic fit on both sides had always been more presentational than industrial.

I did not write that observation into a memo. The shareholders, both of them, were perfectly capable of reading the same picture I was reading. What I did do, when the question was put to me directly, was to give my honest view that the cleanest outcome for the operation, its people and the two shareholders was a sale to a buyer for whom the asset was a strategic core rather than a strategic edge. The board agreed.

The buyer, when the process was run, was Continental Oils. The fit was right. The price was fair. The deal closed without the kind of acrimony mergers in that part of the food industry sometimes attract. The plant retained its name through the transition. Most of the people who had stayed through the rationalisation stayed through the change of ownership as well, which, in my private scoring of a transformation life, is one of the better measures of whether the work was done honestly. The shareholders walked away with the asset converted into capital on terms they could each live with. The joint venture was dissolved cleanly. The chairman of the board took me aside after the closing and thanked me, in fewer words than the work probably deserved, which was the sort of thanks I had always preferred.

I closed my office at the site on a Friday afternoon. I drove back to Gauteng on the Saturday. By the Sunday evening I had written, in the same notebook where I had drafted the original blue paper, a single page of observations to myself about what the assignment had taught me.

## The Realisation: Seeing the Big Picture in Days, Not Months

That single page is the reason this chapter sits where it does in the book.

The observation that mattered most, written at the top of the page, was this. For most of my career up to that point, I had needed months to understand a new operation. The rhythm I had followed at Adcock Ingram, and again at Kolosus, had been the patient one — walk the plant, read the records, talk to the people, sit with the numbers, and let the structure of the business reveal itself slowly over a quarter or two. That patient rhythm had served me well. But at SA Oil Mills, for the first time, I had not needed it. The blue paper had been written in two weeks. The diagnosis had been correct on the day I read it aloud, and nothing in the months that followed had asked me to revise it materially. The structure of the business had not revealed itself to me. I had recognised it.

I sat with that thought for some time. I did not want to overstate it. Pattern recognition is a thing experienced operators talk themselves into far too easily, and the moment you decide you can see a business in two weeks is usually the moment you begin to miss what makes that particular business different from the ones that taught you what you know. I had no interest in becoming the kind of transformation man who walks into a plant convinced he already knows what is wrong with it. I had spent thirty years learning the opposite discipline.

But the truth, honestly examined, was that the years had begun to compound. The cyanide and peroxide plants of AECI had taught me how a heavy-chemicals operation breathes. The validation files of Adcock Ingram had taught me how a regulated operation thinks. The export packing flows of Kolosus had taught me how a customer-driven operation prices itself. The seed-handling, crushing, refining and packing of SA Oil Mills had been, in a sense, the first operation I had run that pulled all three of those instincts into the same building at the same time. And the blue paper I had written in two weeks had been, on closer inspection, less a feat of insight than a careful inventory of patterns I had already learned to read elsewhere.

That, written plainly at the bottom of the single page, was the realisation. I could now walk into almost any operation in almost any related industry and see the shape of its problem in days rather than in months. Not because I was any cleverer than the men who had managed those operations for years. But because I had served a long apprenticeship in enough industries that the basic structures had begun to rhyme. A duplicate refining stream in an oils plant rhymes with a duplicate packing format in a confectionery line. An overstaffed crushing route rhymes with an overinstrumented chemicals reactor whose readings have stopped being read. A poorly documented changeover routine in a seed mill rhymes with a missing validation protocol in a pharmaceutical packing hall. The vocabulary is different. The grammar, underneath, is the same.

## The Method, Written Down

I will record the method here in the form it took at the end of the SA Oil Mills assignment, because it is the form I have used at every operation since.

- Walk the operation for two full weeks before writing anything substantial. Listen more than you speak. Eat in the canteen. Read the breakdown reports. Watch one piece of equipment for an unbroken hour on each of the shifts that runs it.
- At the end of the two weeks, write a short, honest document — a blue paper — that names the structural problem in language a board can act on. Resist the consultant's instinct to add hedges and qualifications. If the operation has three crushing lines and should have one, say so.
- Around the structural recommendation, design a focused capital programme that removes the daily workarounds the operation has been living on. Modest budgets, clearly justified, beat ambitious ones every time.
- Around the capital programme, design the people process before the first machine moves. Engage the unions and the shop stewards at the start. Cost the enhanced separations honestly. Be in the room when the difficult conversations happen. Write the references the next morning.
- Hold the implementation timeline tightly. Drift is the enemy of morale. Decision, properly explained, is its friend.
- When the operation is stable and the financials are honest, ask the shareholder question plainly. Is this asset in the right portfolio. If it is not, run a clean process to put it where it belongs, and walk out at the end of it with the standards higher than when you walked in.

I have read that list back to myself, in the same form, at the start of every contract since. The names of the industries have changed. The arithmetic of the numbers has changed. The list has not.

## What SA Oil Mills Gave Me

What the SA Oil Mills assignment gave me, more than the financial outcome and more than the clean sale to Continental Oils, was the quiet confidence that the work I had been doing for thirty years had organised itself into a method. The method did not belong to a particular industry. It did not depend on the size of the capital budget. It did not require a famous brand on the gate. It required only a willingness to look honestly at an operation, to write down what was structurally wrong with it, to design the people process before the machine process, and to hold the implementation timeline against the inevitable pressures to let it drift.

That confidence travelled with me out of the SA Oil Mills gate and into the rest of the contract years. It was the confidence that allowed me to take on, without fuss, the export and confectionery brief that came next, knowing that even when the structural conditions were against me I would at least be able to read them honestly. It was the confidence that let me, some time later, accept a Tuesday-morning call about a R200-million mogul installation at Tiger Brands and know, before the second sentence of the conversation, what kind of work would be required. And it was the confidence I would carry, eventually, into the BEVCO and Future Life and Black Cat assignments that closed out the working part of my contract life.

The blue paper has been many things since SA Oil Mills. A mogul-line diagnosis. A peanut-butter Pareto chart. A high-speed beverage line specification. The colour of the paper has changed. The discipline has not.

Antonette, on the Sunday evening I had finished writing my single page of observations, found me at the kitchen table and asked, in her quiet way, whether the assignment had been worth the months in residence and the weeks of late nights. I told her, more directly than I might have done earlier in our marriage, that it had been the assignment in which the work of all the previous assignments had finally added up to something I could carry into the next one. She nodded the way she always nods when she has heard a sentence she takes seriously. She did not need me to elaborate.

I put the notebook back in the drawer where I kept the working papers of the contract life. I would open it again, in due course, for the next assignment. The blueprint, by then, was already written. The next operation would only have to fill in its particular numbers.
