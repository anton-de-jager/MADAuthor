# Crisis Without a Manual

Every manager has a drawer somewhere, real or imagined, full of the manuals he has been given over the years. Quality manuals. Safety manuals. HR manuals with policies written by people who have never had to apply them at four in the afternoon on a Friday. Standard operating procedures, audit checklists, incident report templates, decision trees laminated in plastic so the grease will not get to them. The drawer is heavy. You add to it every year. You almost never open it.

And then one day something happens that none of those manuals predicted, and you sit at your desk for a long moment looking at the drawer the way a man looks at a toolbox that he has just realised does not contain the spanner he needs.

That is the chapter I want to write now. Not the recurring problems that a working life teaches you to handle. Those are not crises. Those are just **Tuesday**. I mean the other kind. The one that arrives without warning, that does not fit any of the categories on any of the forms, and that asks of you, very quietly, a kind of judgement you did not know you had been training for.

I am going to keep the specifics of mine deliberately blurred. Not because the story is too painful to tell — I have told it often enough, in small rooms — but because the details are not the point. If I tell you it was a fire, or a death on site, or the collapse of a major client, or a fraud uncovered late on a Friday afternoon, you will hear those specifics and decide the chapter is about your industry or not about your industry, and you will miss what I am trying to hand on. The lesson is not in the *what*. It is in the *what now*.

## The shape of a real crisis

Let me try to describe what a real crisis actually feels like, because the word has been worn smooth by overuse. People in the corporate world use **crisis** for things that are, in truth, just bad mornings. A printer is down. A key person has resigned. A client is angry. Those are problems. They have shapes. They have textbooks. You handle them and go home and forget about them by Sunday lunch.

A real crisis has a different texture, and you recognise it not by the size of the event but by the silence that follows it. There is a moment, usually quite short, in which everyone who has just heard the news stops moving. The phones stop ringing for a few seconds, or it feels like they do. People who were arguing in a meeting room go quiet. Someone laughs, then catches themselves, then does not laugh again. The whole building seems to be listening for something it cannot quite hear.

> A real crisis is not measured by the noise of the event. It is measured by the quality of the silence that comes after it.

In that silence, two things happen at the same time. The first is that your training, whatever training you have, stands up and offers itself. Procedures, checklists, the rules you have memorised — they all line up neatly in your head and wait to be useful. The second is that you realise, with a kind of cold clarity, that none of them quite fit. They cover ninety per cent of what is in front of you. The remaining ten per cent is the part that matters, and there is no laminated card for it.

That gap, between what you have been taught and what is actually being asked of you, is the country this chapter is about. It is a country you cannot study for in advance. You can only learn to walk in it by being there.

## The first ten minutes

The first ten minutes of a crisis are the ones that will be talked about afterwards. Sometimes for years. People will remember where you stood, what you said, whether your voice was steady, whether you looked at the right person at the right time. They will remember it more clearly than they remember any speech you gave at any company function. You will not, at the time, know that you are being watched this closely. You will think you are just trying to get through the moment. Everyone else will be filing it away.

I learned this the hard way. The first true crisis of my management life — and I will leave it at that — caught me in my office on what had been a perfectly ordinary morning. I was reading something dull on the screen. The phone went. I picked it up. The voice on the other end told me, in short sentences, what had happened. I remember saying, "Right." Just that. **Right.** Then I stood up, walked to my door, opened it, and went out into the corridor.

I did not know, in that moment, that I had already made three decisions.

The first decision was that I had stood up. I had not stayed seated. I had not asked the caller to send me an email. I had not, in any way, treated the news as something to be processed at my own pace. I had moved my body, immediately, in the direction of the thing. That is a decision, even when it does not feel like one, and the people who saw me coming down the corridor registered it.

The second decision was the word. *Right.* Not *oh no*. Not *I cannot believe this*. Not *how could this happen*. Those are honest responses. They are also useless to anyone who needs you in the next sixty seconds. *Right* is a small, almost meaningless word, but it does a particular job. It accepts the news. It signals that the news has been received, that the receiver is still functional, and that something will now be done. You can train yourself to say *right* under your breath when bad news lands. It is one of the few rehearsals that has, in my experience, actually paid off.

The third decision was that I went out into the corridor before I had a plan. I did not sit at my desk and try to think it through. I knew that whatever I was about to do, I needed to be among people while I was doing it, not above them. The instinct to retreat and *strategise* in a crisis is one of the most expensive mistakes a manager can make. People do not want a wise message from a closed door. They want to see your face. They want to see that you are in the building.

I want to be careful here. I am not romanticising panic. There is a difference between *going towards the thing* and *running at it without thinking*. The point is not to abandon judgement. The point is that in the first ten minutes, your visibility is itself an act of leadership. You are not yet making the big decisions. You are establishing, by walking through the building, that the big decisions are going to be made by someone who is present.

## What the manuals get right, and where they stop

I do not want to be unfair to the manuals. They are there for a reason. Most of what happens in any complicated business is some variation on something that has happened before, and the manuals are the slow, patient memory of the organisation. They keep you from repeating, in a panic, the mistakes a hundred predecessors have already made. They tell you who to phone. They tell you what to log. They tell you what the regulator will need by Friday. In the first hours of any serious incident, you will lean on them, and you will be glad they exist.

What they do not do, and cannot do, is tell you what kind of crisis you are actually in.

That sounds strange when you first hear it. Surely a crisis is whatever the event was. The fire is a fire. The fraud is a fraud. The death is a death. You categorise it, you open the relevant section, and you proceed.

But every real crisis I have lived through has had at least two layers, and the one that mattered most was almost never the one written on the front of the file.

A fire is a fire. It is also, by the next morning, a question about whether your people believe you care about them more than you care about your insurer. A fraud is a fraud. It is also, by lunchtime, a question about whether the rest of the team thinks you knew. A lost client is a lost client. It is also, by the end of the week, a question about whether your remaining clients are quietly wondering if they should be next. The visible crisis sits on top of an invisible one, and the invisible one is almost always about **trust** — who has it, who is losing it, who is watching to see what you do.

The manuals handle the visible crisis. They do a competent, sometimes excellent job of it. They have almost nothing to say about the invisible one. That is where you, the manager with no script, have to earn your title.

## The decisions you make before you know enough

One of the cruellest features of a real crisis is that the most important decisions usually have to be made before you have enough information to make them properly. By the time you have enough information, the decisions have already been made for you by events. So you are forced, again and again, to act on incomplete pictures, knowing that some of what you decide today will look foolish on Friday, and that some of it will be defended, in writing, by people who were not in the room.

I do not think there is a clean technique for this. There is, however, a posture, and I have come to trust it.

The posture is this. When you cannot know enough to be right, decide on the basis of what you would still respect about yourself in five years' time. Not what is easiest. Not what is most defensible to the auditors. Not what the lawyer would prefer. Those are real considerations, and you weigh them, but they are not the spine of the decision. The spine is the question of which version of yourself you can still meet in the mirror, on a quiet Sunday morning a long time from now, when nobody is asking and nobody is grading.

> In a crisis you cannot decide your way to certainty. You can only decide your way to integrity, and hope that certainty catches up later.

That sentence sounds like a slogan when I write it. In the moment, it is not a slogan. It is a way of cutting a knot you cannot untie. You have three options in front of you, each with consequences you cannot fully see, and there is no spreadsheet that will rank them honestly. So you ask: which of these three, if I follow it through, will let me sleep? Which will let the people who trust me keep trusting me, not because they liked the outcome, but because they recognised the manner in which I chose? You pick that one. You write down, somewhere, why you picked it. And then you move.

I have made decisions in crises that turned out, commercially, to be the wrong ones. I have also made decisions that turned out to be right, in narrow financial terms, that I am not proud of. The ones I am most at peace with, twenty and thirty years on, are not always the ones that saved the most money. They are the ones in which I behaved, under pressure, the way I had told my people, in calmer weeks, that we behaved.

That is the use of culture, by the way. People talk about company culture as if it is a poster in reception. It is not. Culture is the set of things you do under pressure when nobody has time to consult the poster. If your culture does not show up at three in the morning during a crisis, you do not have a culture. You have a brochure.

## The conversation with the people on the floor

Somewhere in the first day or two of any real crisis, you will have to stand in front of the people who work for you and tell them what is happening. Not the board. Not the lawyers. Not the journalists. The people on the floor.

This is the conversation I have, over the course of my career, found hardest to get right. The board wants reassurance. The lawyers want carefulness. The people on the floor want the truth, and they want it from a face they recognise, in language they use.

I have, in my time, watched men in suits try to deliver bad news to a workshop in the language of a press release. It does not work. It does worse than not work. It convinces the room that the man speaking does not understand what has happened, or does not respect them enough to speak plainly. You can lose ten years of patiently built trust in fifteen minutes of corporate language, and you will not get it back by the end of the year, because the people in that room will tell the story, accurately, to everyone they know.

I have come to a few rules about that conversation, and I will share them here. They are not laws. They are habits.

- **Stand where they stand.** Not on a stage. Not behind a desk. If the news belongs in the workshop, deliver it in the workshop. The geometry of where you stand tells the room more than your sentences do.
- **Say what happened in the words you would use to your brother.** Not in the words your insurer would prefer. Plain nouns. Plain verbs. If the company has lost money, say *lost money*. If someone is hurt, say *hurt* and use the name if you have permission to. The dignity is in the directness.
- **Say what you do not yet know.** This is the part most managers skip, and it is the part that earns you the most credit. *I do not know yet whether* is a phrase that costs nothing to say and buys you weeks of patience from a room that would otherwise spend those weeks inventing answers for themselves.
- **Say what happens next, even if "next" is small.** *By Friday we will know X. On Monday I will be back here to tell you Y.* People can live with uncertainty if it has dates on it. They cannot live with uncertainty that has no shape.
- **Stay in the room after you have finished.** Do not announce and leave. Take the questions. Take the awkward silences. Take the one quiet man at the back who waits until the others have gone before he comes up to you with the question that actually mattered. That conversation is the one the room will remember.

If you do these things, you will not solve the crisis. You will, however, hold the building together while the crisis is being solved, and in my experience the building is at least half of what you are trying to save.

## What gets lost

I want to be honest about a thing that the management books do not often dwell on. In a real crisis, you lose things. Not always money. Not always people. But something is always taken from you, and pretending otherwise is one of the ways a manager goes hard in the wrong way over a working life.

Sometimes you lose a piece of innocence. The first time you understand, in your bones, that a person you trusted has been stealing from you, something dies that does not grow back. You can be wiser afterwards, and more useful afterwards, but you will never be as open as you were the morning before you knew. That is a real loss. Mourn it briefly, and do not pretend it did not happen.

Sometimes you lose a relationship. A peer you used to like, a director you used to respect, a client who turned out to be smaller, in a crisis, than they had pretended to be in good times. You learn things about people under pressure that you would have preferred not to know. You cannot unlearn them. You can be civil. You cannot be close in the same way again.

Sometimes you lose a version of yourself. The man who walks into the crisis is not always the man who walks out. The one who walks out has had to make decisions the one who walked in had not yet had to make, and the choices leave marks. Most of those marks are useful — they harden the parts of you that needed hardening, and soften the parts that needed softening — but they are still marks. You earned them honestly. Do not be ashamed of them, and do not pretend they are not there.

The reason I am dwelling on this is that I have watched too many managers, including a younger version of myself, try to come out of a crisis acting as if nothing has been spent. As if the cost was all paid by the balance sheet and none of it by the man at the top of the organogram. That posture impresses no one and exhausts the man performing it. There is a quiet dignity in acknowledging, at the right time and to the right people, that the year took something from you. It does not weaken your authority. It deepens it.

## What gets saved

But things are saved, too, and this is the part I most want to put on the record, because the literature of crisis is often weighted too heavily towards loss. A real crisis, well handled, also reveals the parts of a business and a man that nothing else can reveal.

You discover, in a crisis, which of your people can be trusted with the truth. You did not know before. You thought you knew. You had a list in your head of the ones you would lean on, and the list is almost always wrong in interesting ways. Some of the people you had quietly written off will turn up at six in the morning with a clear head and a useful question, and you will look at them differently for the rest of your career. Some of the ones you had built up in your mind will turn out to be smaller than you thought, and you will, gently and over time, adjust the weight you give their opinions.

You discover what your systems actually do, as opposed to what the org chart says they do. Information that should have flowed from one department to another did not. Decisions that were supposed to be made at one level had been quietly drifting up or down to another. The crisis lights up the wiring of your business like a torch in a dark cellar, and you see, suddenly, where the splices are and where the wires have been hanging loose for years. You can fix some of that afterwards. The opportunity is rare. Use it.

You discover, sometimes, what your business is *for*. That sounds grand. I do not mean it grandly. I mean that you find out, in the choices you are forced to make under pressure, which of the things you say in good times you actually mean. If you have said for years that you put safety first, the crisis will tell you whether that is true. If you have said for years that you look after your people, the crisis will tell you. If you have said for years that you deal straight with your customers, the crisis will tell you. The answers are not always comfortable. They are always useful.

And, if you are very fortunate, you discover something about yourself that you would never have learned in calmer weather. Most of us do not, on a quiet Tuesday, know what we are made of. We have not been asked. The crisis asks. The answer it gives back is not always flattering, and it is almost never the answer we expected, but it is honest, and we get to carry it for the rest of our working lives.

## The long tail

A crisis does not end when the immediate event ends. This is one of the lessons that is not, I think, properly taught in any management course I have heard of, and it cost me dearly the first time I learned it.

There is the event. There is the response. There is the cleanup. And then, when everyone is exhausted and wants the chapter to be over, there is the long tail, and the long tail is the part that requires the most discipline.

The long tail is the months — sometimes years — afterwards, in which the official story has moved on but the actual consequences are still working their way through the building. People who were affected are still affected. Trust that was bruised is still bruised. The new procedures are bedding in, or, more often, quietly being ignored because everyone is tired. The board has stopped asking. The press has stopped asking. The auditors have signed off. And it is precisely in this period that the lessons of the crisis are either embedded into the organisation forever, or quietly lost.

I have seen good companies handle the event well and then lose all the value of the lesson by neglecting the tail. The big meeting was held. The big speech was given. The big procedure was written. And then everyone went back to what they were doing in the same way they had been doing it. Eighteen months later, you can tell, by walking through the floor, that nothing was actually learned. The same gaps are still there. The same wires are still hanging.

So I learned, after my first serious crisis, to put a date in the diary for six months later, and another for a year later. Not to mark the anniversary. To sit down quietly with the few people who had been in the room and ask, in plain language: *What are we still doing because of that? What have we already let slip?* It is not a glamorous meeting. It is one of the most important meetings I have ever held. The crisis itself was forced on me. The discipline of the tail was the part I could choose.

## What only became clear in hindsight

There is one more thing I owe this chapter, because the brief I gave myself was to be honest about what only became clear in hindsight. I will give it to you in three sentences, because it does not need more.

The first thing that became clear, only afterwards, was how much of what looked like decisive leadership at the time was actually fear, well dressed. I would not have admitted that at the time. I am willing to admit it now.

The second was how much I had relied, in the moment, on a handful of habits installed in me decades earlier on a workshop floor by men I have already written about elsewhere in this book. Habits about standing where the work was. Habits about saying *I do not know* without flinching. Habits about not asking anyone to do anything I had not been seen, that morning, doing myself. I thought, in the crisis, that I was reaching for new tools. I was, in truth, reaching for the oldest ones I owned.

The third was that the day I had been most afraid of, before it happened, turned out — once it had happened and been survived — to be the day on which I most clearly became a manager in the full sense of the word. Not because I handled it perfectly. I did not. But because something in me, after that day, stopped waiting for permission to lead. The borrowed coat fitted, after that, in a way it had not fitted the morning before.

## A short list, for the man at the desk when the phone rings

If I had to compress this whole chapter into a few lines for the version of myself who was sitting at his desk on the morning that call came in, it would be these:

- A real crisis is recognised not by the loudness of the event but by the **silence that follows**. Listen for that silence. It is your cue.
- The first ten minutes are not about the plan. They are about being **seen, present, and steady**. Stand up. Say *right*. Walk towards it.
- The manuals handle the visible crisis. **You** have to handle the invisible one underneath, which is almost always about trust.
- When you cannot know enough to be right, decide your way to **integrity**, not certainty. Pick the option you can defend to yourself in five years.
- Speak to the people on the floor in the language you would use to your brother. **Plain nouns, plain verbs, named uncertainty, dated next steps.** Then stay in the room.
- Do not pretend nothing has been spent. Acknowledge, to the right people at the right time, what the year has cost. It deepens authority. It does not weaken it.
- Notice what has been **revealed** — about your people, your systems, your business — and do something with it before the room forgets.
- Diary the long tail. Six months. A year. The crisis is not over when everyone says it is over. It is over when you have either embedded the lesson or, by inattention, lost it.
- And remember, on the morning you most wish you had a manual, that you are not actually alone. You are standing on every habit any decent tradesman, foreman, or mentor ever installed in you. Reach for the **oldest tools**, not the newest ones. They are the ones that will hold.

That is the chapter. The crisis itself sits in my memory now in a particular way, the way a long illness sits in the memory of someone who has recovered: clearly, but without the heat. I would not wish it on anyone. I would not, given the choice and the impossibility of going back, trade it either. It taught me, in the space of a few weeks, what a decade of calm management could not have taught me, and it left me — bruised, more careful, slightly older around the eyes — finally able to do the job whose title I had been wearing, in those earlier years, like a borrowed coat.

The compass, I would discover later, does not point most truly on the easy days. It points most truly on the days when the map has burned and the manuals do not fit and the room is waiting, in that particular silence, for the man whose name is on the door to walk out into the corridor and begin.
