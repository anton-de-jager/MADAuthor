# Germany, Cyanide and the Habit of Delivering Early

We flew from Jan Smuts in the late winter, Antonette and our three small children and I, with more suitcases than the airline preferred and a folder of AECI papers in my hand luggage that I had been told, in so many words, not to lose. The folder contained the technical specification for a reactor that did not yet exist, written in a mixture of English engineering language and the German metric conventions that I had spent the last fortnight in Sasolburg teaching myself to read without flinching. Somewhere on the other end of the flight, in a yard outside Dusseldorf, a contractor was waiting to build that reactor for us. My job, in the politest possible language, was to make sure he built the one we had paid for and not the one he would have preferred to build.

I had never been to Europe in my life. I had not, until that point, been further from the East Rand than the Cape. The continent on the cabin map looked, from where I sat with a child on each knee and a third asleep against Antonette's shoulder, like a country I had borrowed someone else's nerve to visit.

## Dusseldorf in Winter

Dusseldorf in those years was the kind of industrial city that did not waste hospitality on visiting engineers. It received us politely, found us an apartment in a suburb the contractor's office had pre-approved, gave us a small German car that I never quite stopped over-revving on the autobahns, and got down to business on the Monday morning of the following week. I learned, in the first round of meetings, three things about doing technical business in Germany that have stayed with me through every overseas assignment since.

The first was that the specification was sacred. In South Africa we had treated a specification as the opening position in a negotiation that would unfold, in practice, over the life of the project. In Germany the specification was the project. Any deviation from it, however small, was treated as an event in its own right, with its own paperwork, its own meeting, and its own quietly disapproving silence from the contractor's senior people. I came to appreciate that discipline within a fortnight. It saved an enormous amount of argument later.

The second was that punctuality was not a courtesy. It was an instrument of work. A meeting that began at eight o'clock began at eight o'clock, and the man who arrived at five past eight had, by his arrival, made a statement about himself that the room would not easily forget. I adjusted within a week. I have not, in the years since, adjusted back.

The third was that the contractor's project manager, a man whose name I will not put on the page but whose face I can still see clearly, had decided, before I arrived, that the South African engineer assigned to his site was probably going to be a nuisance. He was a courteous man. He did not say so. But the early meetings had a particular quality of formal politeness that, in any language, means a senior person is waiting to find out whether the junior person across the table is going to be worth speaking to properly.

I was determined that he would decide, by the end of the first month, that I was.

## The Specification Meetings

The first phase of the work, before any steel was cut, was a sequence of specification meetings in a panelled room above the contractor's workshop. There must have been forty of those meetings in the end. They began at eight, they ran until lunch, they resumed at one, and they finished, on the contractor's clock, at five precisely. Coffee was strong, water was cold, and the only thing on the table apart from the documents was a small dish of hard sweets that nobody touched.

What I learned in those meetings was that I had been sent to Germany not because I was the most technically gifted engineer at AECI, which I plainly was not, but because the company needed someone who could sit, for hour after hour, across a table from men who knew the technology better than he did, and refuse to be intimidated into agreeing with things that did not suit the plant we were going to have to run when they had all gone home.

I asked, in those meetings, a great many more questions than the contractor had been expecting. Some of them were technical. Many of them were not. I wanted to know, in plain language, why a particular nozzle was sized the way it was, what the tolerance on a particular weld assumed about the operators who would maintain it, what the cleaning regime was going to look like in a Sasolburg summer when the cooling water temperature was nothing like the German benchmark on which the design assumed. The contractor's people, to their credit, answered. They answered, at first, with a slight air of indulgence, the way a senior engineer answers a graduate. By the end of the second week the indulgence had gone out of the answers, and the answers had become better.

I noticed, somewhere in the middle of all of that, a habit of mind in myself that I had not previously had a name for. I was instinctively pulling each design decision apart, asking what it would mean for the man on the spanner end of the job, and reassembling it on the page in front of me in the form of a question the contractor had to answer. That habit of disassembly and re-assembly, which I now understand to have been a kind of analytical engineering reflex, was the thing the German project manager eventually decided was worth speaking to properly. The formal politeness, in the third week, began quietly to thaw.

## Persuading a Contractor to Run Nights

The project plan, when it was finally signed off, ran to a duration that the AECI head office in Johannesburg had accepted as inevitable, but which I had come to believe was longer than it needed to be. The reactor work was bottlenecked, not by the technology, but by the contractor's working hours. The workshop ran a generous single day shift, with a long lunch in the middle of it, and went quiet from the early evening until the following morning. If the workshop ran nights, even a partial night shift on the critical-path operations, the project would finish weeks earlier than the bar chart suggested.

In South Africa I would have raised it on a Monday morning and the second shift would have started on the Wednesday. In Germany I had to do it differently.

I went, first, to the contractor's senior people, with a small folder of numbers I had prepared in the evenings in our Dusseldorf apartment. I had laid out, in their own format and in their own units, what an earlier completion would mean for the AECI plant in Sasolburg, what it would mean in landed cost for the South African economy, and, more importantly for the conversation in that room, what it would mean for the contractor's standing on the next round of tenders. I made no demand. I made an argument. I left the folder behind and I went away.

It took, in the end, the better part of three weeks of careful conversation. There were meetings in which I said nothing, because the contractor's senior people needed to talk to one another in German without a foreigner in the room, and I had learned by then to make myself useful by being somewhere else. There were meetings in which I produced a single sheet of paper, no more, and answered the questions the senior people had brought to it. There was a Friday afternoon, towards the end of those three weeks, when the project manager looked at me across the long table and said, in the careful English he had been speaking to me for two months, that the workshop would run a partial night shift on the reactor operations from the following Monday, on condition that I would personally be on site at the start of each of those shifts for the first fortnight.

I was on site. I was on site at six in the evening when the night crew came in, I was on site at two in the morning when the senior welders relieved their juniors on the critical seams, and I was on site, more often than I would like to admit, when the day shift came back in at eight. Antonette never reproached me for those weeks. The children, who had begun to make small German friends in the apartment block, accepted that their father had become a man who came home to sleep rather than to live.

The reactor was finished, in the end, almost a full month ahead of the schedule the AECI head office had been expecting.

## Munich, and a Second Yard

Part way through the Dusseldorf work the project picked up a second leg in Munich, on a related reactor for the same downstream chemistry. I shuttled, for several months, between the two yards on the German rail system, with the folder of papers thickening as the project went on, and with a pattern of work that I have used in every project of any scale since.

The pattern was simple to describe and difficult to live. I spent the first day of any week on the older site, walking the work in progress with the foreman and the senior welders and noting, in a small notebook of my own, anything I had nearly missed on the previous visit. I spent the second and third days on the newer site, applying the same discipline of fresh attention. I spent the fourth day with the contractor's planners, in a room with a large board, reconciling what I had seen with what their bar chart claimed. I spent the fifth day writing a single, clear note for the AECI head office in Johannesburg that said, in plain language, what was on track, what was not, and what I was doing about each line on the second list. I posted it on the Friday afternoon. I never missed a Friday.

What I learned, in the shuttle between Dusseldorf and Munich, was that the engineer who travels between two sites carries something the people who never leave either one cannot easily carry. He carries the comparison. He sees the small differences in housekeeping, in toolbox discipline, in the way a foreman runs his Monday morning, that any single yard, looked at on its own, will quietly normalise. I borrowed, in those months, the best small habits of one yard for the other, and the contractor's senior people noticed. Both yards finished better than either of them, on its own, would have done.

## Thirty Days Early

The number that the AECI executives in Johannesburg eventually wrote on a memorandum, when the project was closed out, was that the German work had finished approximately thirty days ahead of the contractual schedule, and that the early completion had unlocked, by the company's own internal calculation, the better part of thirty million rand of value in 1980s money. I will not pretend to have run those numbers myself. The savings were the cumulative effect of an earlier start-up of the South African plant, a forward sale of product into a market that was, at that moment, short of supply, and a series of follow-on contractual effects that the planning people in Johannesburg understood better than I did.

What I did understand, and what I have carried in my bones ever since, was the underlying lesson. A project does not finish early because somebody works harder at the end. It finishes early because somebody, at the beginning, refuses to accept that the schedule on the page is the schedule the project has to keep. The thirty days, when they came, had been quietly assembled out of the night-shift conversation in week six, the specification arguments in weeks one to four, the shuttle pattern between two sites in the middle months, and the Friday notes that had kept the head office close enough to the work to back the decisions when they had to be backed.

I flew home with my family in the early summer, in a different mood from the one in which I had flown out. The folder in my hand luggage was thinner. The continent on the cabin map looked smaller. I had become, somewhere in the German winter, a different kind of engineer.

## Cyanide for the Gold Mines

The promotion that waited for me in Sasolburg was to the management of the cyanide plant. Cyanide, in the South African context, is not an exotic chemical. It is a working tool of the gold-mining industry, used in the leaching processes that recover gold from milled ore, and the gold mines of the Witwatersrand were, in those years, the dominant customers of the AECI cyanide operation. The volumes were enormous, the chain of custody was strict, and the consequences of a supply interruption ran, by the time they had propagated through the mining industry, into the production planning of half the country.

I came into that plant with two convictions and a notebook. The convictions were that the plant was capable of more than it was producing, and that the reason it was not producing more was not a shortage of engineering but a shortage of analytical attention to its own operation. The notebook was the same private notebook I had begun keeping in the VCM years, in which, every Friday, I wrote down the three things I had nearly missed that week.

The first thing I did was walk the plant. I walked it in the way the German shuttle had taught me to walk a yard, which is to say I walked it on a Monday morning with the senior foreman, on a Tuesday afternoon with the analytical chemist on duty, on a Wednesday night with the night-shift charge hand, and on a Thursday with whichever operator had been on the plant the longest. I asked, in each of those walks, what the plant was being prevented from doing, and I listened, often for a great deal longer than was comfortable, to the answers.

The answers, when I had collected enough of them, formed a pattern. The plant was not bottlenecked in the place its instrumentation suggested. It was bottlenecked, in a quieter and more chronic way, in a series of small operational compromises that had accumulated over the years and had come to be regarded as the normal state of affairs. A reactor that was being run a little below its design temperature because an early operator, long since retired, had been nervous of running it any closer to the limit. A feed sequence that had been adjusted to suit a particular crew's preference and never adjusted back. A maintenance window that had been lengthened by half a shift, ten years previously, on the recommendation of a fitter who had since left the company, and which nobody had ever asked to revisit.

None of those compromises, taken on its own, was wrong. Taken together, they were costing the plant something on the order of half its potential throughput.

## Fifty Per Cent More, Without a New Plant

I worked the corrections through, one at a time, over the better part of a year. I did not announce a programme. I did not put a poster on the wall. I sat with the senior operators, one crew at a time, and I asked, with the analytical patience the German months had taught me, whether a particular compromise still needed to be in place. Sometimes the answer was yes, and the compromise stayed. More often the answer, when it was looked at squarely, was that the compromise had outlived its original reason, and could be retired without harm.

By the end of that year the plant was supplying, on a sustainable basis, roughly fifty per cent more cyanide to the gold-mining industry than it had been producing when I had arrived. There was no new capital. There was no new equipment of any significance. There was only a plant that had been allowed, by patient operational adjustment, to do what its original designers had always intended it to do. The mining customers, who had been managing their own supply anxieties for years, were both relieved and, for a while, mildly suspicious. Increases of that order, in an established commodity, were not what they were used to seeing from a long-standing supplier.

The peroxide plant followed, in due course, with a similar story in a different chemistry. I will not repeat the detail. The pattern was the same: a plant that had quietly settled into a fraction of its potential, a series of small operational compromises that nobody had recently questioned, and a steady, careful, crew-by-crew renegotiation of what the plant was capable of when its own people were given permission to stop accepting the compromises as permanent.

## The Analytical Habit, Named

It was somewhere in the middle of those two plant turnarounds that I gave a name, for the first time, to the habit of mind that had been forming in me since the Dusseldorf specification meetings. I had begun to think of it, in my own private language, as analytical engineering. It was not a school of management. It was not a method. It was a discipline, of looking at any operation as a structure of assumptions, and of pulling those assumptions apart one at a time to see which of them still deserved to be there.

I want to set out, as plainly as I can, what that discipline actually consisted of in the years it was being formed. The components were these.

- A refusal to accept any operational compromise as permanent without first understanding what its original reason had been. Half the compromises in any mature plant, I came to believe, are widows of reasons that have long since died.
- A bias to walking the work. The German months had taught me that an engineer who does his thinking in his office is being out-thought, on average, by the engineer who does his thinking on the floor. I never went back to the desk-bound version of the job.
- A weekly written note. The Friday memoranda I had sent from Germany to Johannesburg had taught me that the discipline of compressing a week's observations into a single clear page is, in itself, an analytical tool. I have written some version of that note every working week of my life since.
- An instinct, in any planning meeting, to ask whether the schedule on the page was the schedule the project actually had to keep, or merely the schedule the room had got used to looking at. A great many German workshops had taught me how much earlier a project can finish when somebody is willing to ask that question early.

That last instinct, more than any other, became the habit by which I think the rest of my career was shaped. I learned, in the cyanide and peroxide years, to give my management the dates I believed in rather than the dates the project plan made convenient, and then to do the work of making the dates I believed in stand up. The phrase that I came to use for it, much later, when younger engineers asked me about it, was that I had decided to be a man who under-promised and over-delivered. It was not a slogan. It was a working method. It said, in plain language, that I would rather walk into a board meeting with a finish line behind me than with a finish line still in front of me, and that I would do whatever the work required, in advance, to put myself on the right side of that line.

## What Germany Had Bought

I look back now, from the distance of forty years, on the German assignment and the two AECI plant turnarounds that followed it, and I see them as a single piece of work. They taught me, between them, the four things I had not learned at Sub Nigel or at Libanon or in the early Sasolburg years, and that I would draw on for the rest of my working life.

They taught me that a specification, properly defended, is the cheapest insurance any project can buy. They taught me that a contractor, properly persuaded, will do more for a project than any contract can compel him to do. They taught me that a plant, properly listened to, will tell its manager where its real bottlenecks live, and that those bottlenecks are almost never in the place its instruments are pointing. And they taught me that an engineer who is willing to commit, in advance, to a finish line earlier than the room expects, and then to do the small disciplined work that makes that finish line stand up, will, over a career, accumulate a particular kind of credibility that no qualification can substitute for.

I had gone to Germany as a plant manager from Sasolburg with three small children and a folder I had been told not to lose. I came back as an engineer with a working method. The folder, by then, was much thinner. The method, by then, was much harder to lose.

Antonette and the children settled back into the East Rand. The cyanide plant ran. The peroxide plant ran. The Friday notes went out. And somewhere in the head offices of a consumer-goods business in another part of the country, on a desk I had never seen, my name was beginning to be spoken in conversations I was not yet in the room for.
