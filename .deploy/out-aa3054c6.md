# Early Career and the Move to AECI

There is a particular kind of light that falls on a gold mine in the late afternoon, when the day shift has come up and the evening shift is filing in, and the headgear throws a long shadow across the yard. I remember standing in that light one Friday in the early seventies, in clean overalls and a new hard hat, looking up at the wheels of the shaft and realising that the place where I had served my apprenticeship was about to become the place where I would learn to be an engineer. The trade had been honest work. The next step would have to be something else entirely.

The transfer to Libanon Gold Mine came through with very little ceremony. A letter from the company, a handshake from my section foreman at Sub Nigel, a quiet word from one of the older engineers who had taken an interest in my studies, and that was that. I packed my tools, signed the papers and presented myself at the new mine as an Engineer-in-Training. The job title had three words in it where the apprenticeship had only had one, and I felt the weight of every additional syllable.

## A Different Pair of Boots

The difference between artisan work and engineer-in-training work is not, as I once thought, a difference of skill. It is a difference of accountability. As a Boilermaker on the floor, I had been measured by the quality of what came off the bench: a weld that held, a bracket that ran true, a repair that did not need to be redone on the next shift. As an Engineer-in-Training, I was being measured by something less tangible and considerably more uncomfortable. I was being measured by whether the things I planned actually worked when other men, in other overalls, eventually built them.

That shift in accountability was an education in itself. I learned, in those first months at Libanon, that an engineer's job is not to know more than the artisan. It is to be answerable for the consequences of decisions the artisan cannot reasonably be expected to make alone. The drawing has to be right. The specification has to be right. The sequencing has to be right. And if it is not, the man on the spanner end of the job is the one who pays for it in sweat and lost hours, while the man on the drawing-board end goes home to his supper.

I was determined, even then, never to be the man who went home easily while other men were still paying for my mistakes.

## Marriage at Twenty-Two

In the middle of all of this — the long evening classes, the early-morning shifts, the slow accumulation of mining knowledge that no diploma can teach you — I married Antonette. I was twenty-two years old. She was, by any reasonable measure, the better part of the bargain.

I will not pretend that I knew, at twenty-two, what marriage actually was. Nobody does, no matter what they tell you on their wedding day. What I knew, and what I have come to be grateful for in every decade since, is that I had married a woman whose centre was not me. Her centre was her faith, and from that centre everything else found its proper place: the household, the children when they came, the patience with my hours, the quiet, unsentimental insistence that the family would pray together on a Sunday no matter what the work week had cost us. All of that came out of a still point that had nothing to do with me and everything to do with the God she trusted.

That has made all the difference. A career like mine, with its travel and its plant emergencies and its years of being too tired at the dinner table to be useful company, would have ended a marriage built on shallower ground. Ours was not shallow.

## Three Children in Quick Succession

The children came, as children do in a young marriage, faster than the salary increases that were supposed to accommodate them. Our first three arrived inside the early Libanon years, and the small house we lived in began to feel as if it were inhaling them rather than the other way around. There were cots in corners and prams in passageways and, for a long stretch, a permanent low background hum of small voices that I have since come to miss in a way I never expected to.

Antonette carried most of it. I will not dress that up. I was on shift, or at lectures, or at the drawing-board, or in the workshop trying to coax some piece of obstinate mining gear back to life, while she was at home with babies who had no understanding of mining schedules. She did it with a steadiness that I, with all my engineering training, could not have replicated for a single weekend.

I learned, in those years, that the household is its own kind of plant. It has its own throughput, its own bottlenecks, its own quiet maintenance regime. And, like every plant, it runs on the discipline of whoever is responsible for it when nobody senior is watching. In our house that was Antonette, and the plant ran.

## A Father Who Came Up for the Last Time

The decision to leave mining did not arrive as a single moment of clarity. It arrived, as so many important decisions do, in stages, the way a fever rises. The first stage was the lung disease that had begun, almost imperceptibly, to bend my father.

He had spent the working life of a man underground at Sub Nigel. He had gone down with his lamp and come up with his cough, year after year, decade after decade, and never once complained that the bargain had been a poor one. My father was not a complaining man. He was a churchgoing, plain-spoken miner who believed that work was a duty and that duty did not have to be enjoyable to be honourable. He believed in his shaft and in his Lord, in roughly that order on a weekday and in the reverse order on a Sunday, and he had raised five of us on those two beliefs.

He died at sixty-three. The disease that took him was the disease that had taken many of his shift-mates before him. I had grown up watching old miners cough into handkerchiefs and apologise for the noise. I had grown up knowing, in the way a child knows things without admitting them, that the air in those shafts was not entirely the same air the rest of the country breathed.

When the doctors confirmed what the cough had already announced, I made a decision that, on paper, looked like a career choice and, in fact, was a son's promise. I would not spend my working life underground. I owed my father too much to make the same trade he had made. The mine had given me my trade test, my engineering diploma and my first appointment as an Engineer-in-Training, and I would never speak ill of it for that. But it had also taken my father, and I had three small children now, and I was not going to ask Antonette to live the life my mother had lived, with a husband whose lungs were slowly being audited by the rock.

I began, quietly, to look for work above ground.

It is worth saying, because I have heard the question put to me more than once over the years, that this was not a rejection of the mine. The mine had been good to me. It had paid me through my trade test, sponsored my engineering studies, watched my father witness my graduation, and given me my first engineering appointment. I owed it a debt I have never tried to deny. But debts and decisions are not the same currency. You can be grateful to an institution and still know, in the part of yourself that is responsible for the next thirty years of your family's life, that the next chapter has to be written somewhere else. I was grateful, and I was leaving.

## AECI, Sasolburg

The opportunity that came was at AECI in Sasolburg. For an engineer in that period, AECI was a serious employer in a serious country town: a big chemicals operation in a corner of the then Orange Free State that had been purpose-built around heavy industry. The plant ran twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, three hundred and sixty-five days a year, the way a chemical plant has to run if the economics of its first cubic metre of feedstock are going to make any sense at all.

I accepted the offer with a mixture of relief and apprehension. The relief was easy to understand. I would be out of the shaft for good, and the children would grow up with a father whose lungs had a fair chance of seeing them married. The apprehension was less articulate and, in retrospect, more useful. I had no chemical-plant experience. I had spent my engineering years on rock-breakers and winch motors and steel structures, not on reactors and distillation columns and the unforgiving chemistry of monomers. I was about to learn an entirely new industry while still pretending, in front of fifteen other engineers, that I belonged in the room.

Sasolburg, in those years, was a town that ran on the rhythm of the plants around it. You could tell the time by the shift change. You could tell the season by the colour of the veld between the perimeter fences. The houses were neat, the lawns were green, and the wives knew each other through the school and the church and the shared understanding that their husbands' beepers were going to go off at the worst possible moments.

We moved Antonette and the three children into one of those houses, joined the small congregation up the road, and began.

## Fifteen Engineers and a 24/7 Plant

A 24/7 chemical plant is not a workplace. It is a living organism, and an engineer's first job is to learn its temper. There were fifteen of us in the engineering complement, each responsible for a slice of the operation, each with a pager that did not respect dinner times or christenings or sleep. The plant did not know that Tuesday at half past three in the morning was an unreasonable hour to call an engineer out of bed. The plant only knew its set points and its alarms, and when one of those went the wrong way, somebody in overalls had to be standing in front of it inside the hour.

I learned, in those first eighteen months at AECI, three things that I have carried into every plant I have ever run since.

- A chemical plant rewards engineers who arrive on site before they have to. The man who is already pulling on his overalls in the change-room when the second alarm goes is the man who saves the batch. The man who is still in his car, three kilometres out, is the man who explains the lost batch to the production director at eight the next morning.
- A 24/7 operation has no patience with personal calendars. Either your domestic life is constructed in such a way that it can absorb a plant emergency at any hour, or it is not, and a great deal of difficulty in young engineers' marriages comes from pretending that it is when it is not. I had Antonette. Many of my colleagues did not have a partner of that calibre, and I watched, with quiet sadness, what the pager did to some of those households.
- An engineer in a chemical plant earns his stripes not by knowing the chemistry better than the chemists do, but by understanding, in his bones, what failure looks like before it fails. A leaking flange that is not quite a leak yet. A pump bearing that is half a decibel louder than it was yesterday. A reactor temperature climbing in a way the trend chart has not yet thought to alarm. Those are the things that separate the engineer who runs a plant from the engineer who is merely employed by one.

I was determined to be the first kind. I made it my private discipline, in those Sasolburg years, to walk the section I was responsible for at least once on every shift I was on, even when the shift was quiet. I wanted my plant to know my footsteps before it knew my voice on the radio.

## The VCM Plant

The appointment that changed the shape of my AECI years came when I was made manager of the VCM plant — the vinyl chloride monomer operation that sat at the heart of the site's downstream chemistry. VCM is not a forgiving product. It is a feedstock for PVC manufacture, and the conditions under which it is produced require respect of a kind that a younger engineer can take a while to acquire. The temperatures, the pressures, the toxicity, the explosion limits, the catalyst handling — every variable in the operation had a way of punishing inattention.

Being given that plant to manage was, in the politest possible language, a test. AECI did not put men into the VCM seat to see what happened. They put them in to see whether they would still be there in eighteen months with the plant running at or above nameplate, and with a safety record they could defend in front of a board.

I gave the plant everything I had. I built a routine of walking the unit at the start of every shift I worked, including the night shifts that nobody senior was ever going to inspect. I learned the names of the operators on each crew, and their wives' names where I could, and the names of the children they were trying to put through school on shift allowance. I sat in on the toolbox talks. I read the shift logs at the end of every week, line by line, until the patterns of small abnormalities began to form a kind of map in my head. I learned, in the VCM years, that a plant tells you what is wrong with it long before it tells you in the form of a trip — if you are prepared to listen at the level of small abnormalities rather than at the level of headline alarms.

The plant performed. The board, in due course, noticed.

One small habit from those VCM months has stayed with me through every plant I have run since. I started keeping a private notebook — nothing official, nothing the company ever saw — in which I wrote, at the end of every week, the three things I had nearly missed. Not the things I had caught and corrected, which any production report would already have captured, but the things I had almost not caught: the gauge I had walked past twice before noticing the drift, the operator complaint I had nearly dismissed, the trend I had almost rationalised away. Reading those notebooks back, six months at a time, taught me more about how plants actually fail than any safety course I have ever attended. Plants do not fail because engineers are stupid. Plants fail because engineers, including very good ones, learn to look at the wrong thing first.

## A Telephone Call from Germany

The conversation that ended this chapter of my life began with a telephone call I was not expecting. AECI had a relationship with a German technology partner, and a project of some significance was being scoped on the other side of the world. A South African engineer was wanted on site, for a period long enough to take a family for, in a role technical enough to matter. My name had been put forward.

I will write properly about Germany when it is the right moment to write about it. For the purposes of this chapter, it is enough to say that the call came, and that the offer was put on the table, and that I went home that evening to Antonette and our three children and laid the offer in front of her the way you lay an instrument in front of a craftsman: not to ask her permission, exactly, but to ask whether she could see what I could see in it. She could. She always could.

## What Sasolburg Taught Me

I had left the mining industry as an Engineer-in-Training, full of borrowed authority and untested theory. I left Sasolburg, several years later, as the manager of one of the most demanding plants in the country, with a habit of walking my unit before anyone asked me to, and a marriage that had not only survived a 24/7 environment but had quietly hardened into the partnership that would carry the rest of my career.

If I had to compress what those AECI years gave me into a single sentence, it would be this. A plant teaches you, more thoroughly than any classroom ever can, that the price of operational excellence is paid in advance, in the small disciplines of attention, and never in arrears, in the loud heroics of recovery. The engineers who tried to pay it in arrears burned out, or buried their reputations under one too many lost batches, or — in the most painful cases I watched — buried their marriages instead. The engineers who paid it in advance, in the long, unglamorous, walk-the-line, read-the-logs, learn-the-operators' currency of preparation, were the ones who eventually got the German telephone calls.

I had paid it in advance. The German telephone call had come. And on the East Rand, in a small cemetery I still visited when I could, the miner who had spent his working life underground at Sub Nigel had, in his quiet way, helped me decide which kind of engineer I was going to be.
