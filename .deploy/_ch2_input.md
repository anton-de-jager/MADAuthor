# Tools in Hand

The first tool an apprentice is given is, in nearly every case, not the one he thinks it is.

He thinks it is the hammer, or the chipping hammer, or the small flame-cut torch that the senior tradesman lets him hold for a moment and then takes back again. He is wrong. The first tool he is given is a broom. The second is a rag. The third, if he is paying any attention at all, is the small, dull realisation that the man who has handed him the broom is not insulting him. He is teaching him, in the only language the trade knows for teaching this particular lesson, that the workshop is going to have to come before he does for a while. Not as a matter of hierarchy. As a matter of physics. A floor that has not been swept is a floor that will trip you. A bench that has not been wiped is a bench that will hide your marks. A trade that has not learned to respect its own small, boring acts of maintenance is a trade that produces, eventually, work that nobody wants to put their name on.

I did not understand any of this on my first morning. I thought I had been demoted, on my way in, from the apprentice I had been told I was going to be into some sort of cleaner with overalls. I picked up the broom anyway, because the man who had handed it to me had not made a joke when he did it, and you learn very quickly on a workshop floor that the things people do not joke about are the things that matter.

That broom is where this chapter starts. Not because sweeping is the point of the trade. Because the small habits that you build in the first six months — long before anybody lets you strike an arc on a piece of plate that matters — are the habits that will, twenty-five years later, be doing the actual managing for you on a Friday afternoon when everything is going slightly sideways and there is no time to think.

## What the trade was, in the years I came up

I want to describe the world the apprenticeship took place in, because I have noticed, lately, that younger people sometimes assume the workshops of that era were either grimmer than they were, or more romantic than they were, depending on whose film they last watched.

The reality was neither, mostly.

The workshop I started in was a long, high-roofed building that smelled, in roughly equal measure, of cutting oil, hot steel, old rags, and the particular kind of cheap instant coffee that came in a tin with a yellow label. There was a row of bays down one side where the boilermakers worked, each with its own bench, its own clamps, its own carefully arranged set of templates that the senior tradesman who owned the bay had built up over years. There was a fabrication shop further in, with the bigger jigs and the heavier work. There was a lean-to outside where the gas bottles were stored, and a small office near the door where the foreman sat with a clipboard that always had more on it than he was admitting to.

The noise was constant but not, on most days, oppressive. You learned to read it the way a farmer reads weather. The shriek of a grinder told you somebody was finishing a weld. The slow tap-tap of a chipping hammer told you somebody was knocking slag off a fresh pass. The dull boom of plate being moved on overhead chains told you that a new job had landed and that, in about twenty minutes, the foreman would be looking for a body to help on it. Somewhere in the background, almost always, there was a radio nobody seemed to own, playing a station nobody had chosen, at a volume nobody adjusted.

The men were a particular mixture. Some of them had been there for thirty years and gave the impression of having been built into the building, like a beam. Some were ten or fifteen years in, in the long middle of a career, neither apprentices nor old men. Some were on their way out, slowed by bad backs and worse knees, doing the lighter work and teaching the apprentices because there was nothing else useful left for them to do at full pace. And then there were us. The youngest layer. Five or six of us at any one time, in various stages of green, all of us trying to look as if we had any idea what we were doing.

The pay was modest. The hours were honest. The work was real in a way that I have never quite found a substitute for since. You could see, at the end of a day, exactly what you had made or failed to make. The steel did not flatter you. It did not punish you out of malice either. It told you, in its own quiet and entirely unambiguous way, whether your hands had done what you thought they had done, and you could either listen to it or you could go and find another trade.

## The first six months — the part nobody warns you about

The first six months of any honest apprenticeship are, in my experience, deliberately and usefully boring.

I want to put it that way around, because there is a way of telling this story that frames the early stretch as a kind of injustice — *they had me sweeping for half a year, can you believe it* — and that framing has, I think, done some quiet damage to how young people now approach the early part of any craft. It treats the boring part as the part to be hurried through. It treats the small, repetitive jobs as a tax to be paid before the real work begins. It assumes, in other words, that the trade has not yet started.

The trade had started. I just had not noticed.

In those first six months, I was given, in rough order, the following: a broom, a rag, a wire brush, a chipping hammer, a tape measure, a square, a piece of chalk, and a notebook. Each one was handed to me, sometimes without comment, sometimes with about thirty seconds of explanation, and I was expected to learn what it was for by using it on small, low-stakes work where the only consequence of getting it wrong was that I had to do it again.

The broom taught me that the workshop was a system. You did not sweep around the bay you were working in. You swept the path the next man would have to walk down. You swept under the bench where the offcuts were going to fall during the next job. You swept the corner where the leads from three different welding machines crossed each other, because the lead that nobody noticed was always the one that somebody tripped on. None of this was explained. I worked it out by being told off, briefly and without malice, when I got it wrong, and by watching what the older apprentices did when I had the sense to copy them.

The rag taught me that finish mattered before anyone had asked it of me. The benches in the bays I cleaned were, by the end of a shift, covered in the small filthy residues of a day's work. A senior tradesman would not begin a fresh piece of layout work on a dirty bench. He would either clean it himself or he would, more usefully, look pointedly at the apprentice whose job it was supposed to be. The lesson was that the work begins before the work begins. The setup is part of the trade. If the bench is not right, the layout will not be right. If the layout is not right, the cut will not be right. If the cut is not right, the weld will be a small daily punishment to whoever has to make it. The whole chain runs from a rag.

The wire brush taught me that the surface you are about to work on is not the surface you think it is. There is always a layer of something — mill scale, rust, paint, oil, old slag, whatever — sitting between you and the steel itself. The apprentice's job, on a great many days, was to remove it. Slowly. Properly. Down to the bright, honest metal. I will be honest: I did not always do this well, in the beginning. I would brush at the surface for a few minutes, decide it was good enough, and hand the piece on. The senior man would, without raising his voice, hand it back to me. *Again, my boy. Properly this time.* I would brush it again. He would hand it back a second time. By the third time, I had begun to understand that *properly* was not a vague encouragement. It was a specific physical standard, and he could see it on the steel with about one glance, and I could not see it yet, and the cure for not being able to see it yet was to keep doing it until I could.

The chipping hammer taught me, in its small ringing way, that mistakes have a sound. When you knocked slag off a fresh weld, you could tell, even at my level, whether the underlying weld was sound. A good weld answered the hammer with a clean, almost musical note and a thin curl of black scale that came away in one piece. A bad weld answered it with a duller noise and a shower of small grey crumbs that took half an hour to clean up, and then, more often than not, with the senior man's hand on your shoulder and a quiet *do it again*. I have heard those two sounds, ever since, in many other contexts. There is a clean, ringing sound that a well-built process makes when you tap it, and a dull, crumbly sound that a badly built one makes, and the second sound is one of the most useful pieces of information any manager can teach themselves to notice.

The tape measure, the square, and the piece of chalk taught me a thing about precision that I had not understood from the trade test. I had thought precision was something you tried hard at. I learned, in those first six months, that precision is something you set up for. You did not measure once and cut. You measured twice. You measured from a single reference edge, not from whichever edge happened to be nearest your tape. You marked your lines with chalk first, walked away, came back, looked at them again, and only then did you reach for anything that was going to remove material. The cost of a five-second pause before a cut was a five-second pause. The cost of skipping it was, sometimes, the whole afternoon.

And the notebook taught me, eventually, that I was not as good at remembering things as I had been telling myself I was.

## The notebook

I want to spend a few paragraphs on the notebook, because it is the tool from those first six months that I still use today, in a slightly modernised form, and the one I most wish I had taken seriously sooner.

The notebook I was given was nothing special. A small hard-covered thing with squared paper, about the size of a postcard, that fitted into the breast pocket of my overalls. It came with no instructions. I was told, more or less, *write things down*, and that was the end of the briefing.

For the first few weeks I did not really use it. I wrote down a name or two. I wrote down a phone number once. I lost it for about a week and found it again under the radio in the canteen, which would have been embarrassing if anyone had noticed, which they had, and had decided not to mention.

The thing that changed my relationship with it was a small, ordinary humiliation that I do not think I will ever forget.

A senior tradesman had shown me, in a brisk three or four minutes, the sequence for laying out a particular kind of bracket. He had drawn it on a scrap of plate with chalk. He had walked me through the four steps. He had asked me whether I had it. I had said I had it, because at that age the only acceptable answer to that question was yes. He had nodded and gone back to his own work.

Three days later, the same bracket landed on my bench. He was not in that day. The chalk drawing had been wiped off the plate by someone cleaning up. I sat there for about ten minutes trying to reconstruct the sequence from memory, got it badly wrong, and ruined enough material that the foreman noticed and asked me, in front of two other apprentices, whether I had been listening properly the first time.

That evening I sat on the bus home and I wrote the four steps of that bracket in the notebook, from what I could now reconstruct of them after having spent an afternoon getting them wrong. The next morning, I asked the senior man, quietly, by the urn, whether I could check that I had it right. He looked at the page. He made one small correction with his pencil. He handed it back without saying anything other than, *better*, which from him was a great deal.

After that I wrote everything down. Not neatly. Not in a way that would have impressed a teacher. I wrote in short, ugly phrases that would mean something to me later. *Bracket: ref edge LH, mark at 47, square down, second line 12 above, weld both sides 4 mm fillet.* That kind of thing. I drew small bad pictures. I noted the names of the tradesmen who had taught me each technique, so that I would know, later, who to go back to if it did not work. I noted my own mistakes, in a column down the right-hand side of the page, with a small mark next to the ones that had cost me material.

Over the next two years that notebook quietly became the most valuable thing in my pocket. By the time I was a senior apprentice I had filled two of them. By the time I qualified I had filled four. They are sitting, today, in a drawer in my study, and I take them out sometimes when I am about to teach a younger person something. They remind me how slowly I learned the things I now do without thinking, and they keep me honest about how patient I need to be with somebody else's slow learning.

The notebook is not, in itself, a craft tool. It is a discipline tool. And the discipline of writing down what you have just been taught, in your own ugly handwriting, in your own short ugly phrases, is one of the few habits that I would say, without qualification, transfers from the workshop to every other job a person is ever going to do. I have used the same habit, in a slightly more grown-up notebook, in every meeting that mattered to me since. The argument for it is simple and almost unfair: the people who write things down remember them, and the people who do not, mostly do not. There is no third category.

## Learning to listen to the steel

There is a kind of attention that the trade demands which is, I think, very hard to describe to someone who has not done physical work.

It is not concentration, exactly. Concentration is what you do over a piece of mental work — a calculation, a chess problem, a paragraph you are trying to write. The attention the steel asks for is a different shape. It is partly with your eyes, partly with your hands, partly with your ears, and partly with a sense I do not have a clean word for, which is something like the body's own quiet feedback about whether the work it is doing is going well.

I learned this most clearly on the grinder.

The grinder, for those who have not used one, is a deceptively simple tool. A motor, a disc, two handles. You bring it to a piece of steel and it removes material. Children could be shown how to switch it on. Apprentices used to take it as a sign of how little anyone trusted them yet that the grinder was one of the first power tools they were allowed to use unsupervised.

The trick of it, which nobody told me in so many words, is that the grinder is not really controlled by your hands. It is controlled by your ears and the small tremor in your forearms. A grinder running over good metal at the right angle makes a particular sound — a clean, even, almost contented note. A grinder running over the same metal at the wrong angle makes a slightly thinner, slightly shriller sound, and at the same time your forearms feel a small unwelcome vibration that they did not feel a moment ago. A grinder running over a weld that is not properly tied in at the edges makes a different sound again, a kind of skipping, intermittent noise, because the disc is bouncing very slightly over the discontinuity.

You do not work this out from a manual. You work it out by spending several hundred hours with a grinder in your hands, listening, and noticing, and gradually beginning to associate certain sounds with certain results when you put the piece on the bench and looked at it afterwards. After a while, you found that your hands had begun to adjust the angle of the tool before your conscious mind had even noticed that anything was wrong. The body had learned what the ear was telling it. The ear had learned what the steel was saying. And the steel, as it always is, was telling the truth.

I have come to think of this kind of learning as one of the most underrated things a young person can be exposed to. The world we live in now has fewer and fewer jobs that train it. A great deal of modern work is mediated through screens, where the feedback is delayed, abstract, and easy to talk yourself out of. The workshop has no such kindness. The steel does not care what you intended. It only shows you what you did. Spend a few years inside that kind of feedback loop and your sense of cause and effect is permanently sharpened, in a way I am not sure can be acquired by any other route.

When, much later, I sat in management meetings and listened to the way a project was being described by the people running it, I found that I was listening for a particular tone. Not the words. The tone underneath them. A project that was going well had a certain clean, even sound when its people spoke about it. A project that was in trouble had a thinner, shriller note, even when the language being used was confident. The difference between the two was, almost exactly, the difference between a grinder on good metal and a grinder on bad. I had learned to hear it as an apprentice, on a bench, with a tool in my hands, fifteen years before I had a meeting to attend.

## Attention to detail as a moral position

There is a thing the trade insists on quietly, which the world outside the workshop has, I think, partly lost.

Attention to detail, in the world I came up in, was not a productivity virtue. It was a moral one.

I want to be careful with that word. I do not mean that a man who left a sloppy weld was a wicked man. I mean that the older tradesmen treated craft attention as if it were continuous with honesty. They behaved as if the small acts of care that nobody would ever see — the extra five minutes of cleaning up an edge, the second look at a measurement, the discreet ninety-second re-do of a pass that *would have been alright but was not quite right* — were not optional add-ons to the job. They were part of what it meant to be the person you said you were.

The man who left a weld looking acceptable but knew it was not quite sound had, in their view, done a small dishonest thing, even if the customer would never know. The piece would go out of the shop. It would carry his work in it. His name would be on the job card. The fact that the failure, if any, might not appear for two years was, to them, exactly the reason the standard mattered. The whole structure of the trade depended on the assumption that other tradesmen, in other shops, in other years, had been quietly resisting the temptation to let the marginal piece go. If they had not been, the chain of trust collapsed and nobody could build anything bigger than a bench. So you did not let it go. Not because anyone was watching. Because if you did, you became, in a small but real sense, a different sort of person, and the older men did not want that for you, and after a while you did not want it for yourself.

I have not always seen this transferred well into the corporate world. The corporate world rewards, often, the appearance of attention more than its substance. A polished slide can hide a sloppy analysis. A confident voice in a meeting can carry a half-formed idea further than it deserves to go. The temptation, in that environment, is to begin to think of attention to detail as a presentation skill rather than as a moral position about the work.

The workshop will not let you make that mistake. The plate does not care how confident you sound. It will fail at exactly the load and exactly the moment that the maths and the physics say it will fail, and no amount of presentational skill will change that. I have carried, into every meeting I have ever sat in, a small voice from the workshop that asks, when somebody is being persuasive, *yes, but what does the steel actually say*. It has saved me from a number of mistakes I could otherwise have made without much difficulty.

## Respect for the work, respect for the worker

The other thing the trade installed in me, in those early years, was a particular relationship between the work and the person doing it.

You did not, in that workshop, condescend to a piece of work. Not even a small one. The senior men did not produce a different quality of effort for an unimportant job than they did for a flagship one. They produced their quality, which was their quality, and they applied it to whatever steel was in front of them that morning. A small repair on a farmer's trailer got the same set of habits as the components of a pressure vessel. The reason was not, I think, primarily about the customer. It was about the worker. You did not let yourself be a different tradesman on a Tuesday than you had been on a Monday. You did not let the size of the job dictate the size of your effort. Once you started doing that, you became a man who could be reasoned out of his standards by circumstances, and the older men had seen, in their own long careers, what happens to that kind of man. He drifts. The drift is not dramatic. It is just that, year by year, the work gets a little looser and the man's reputation gets a little quieter, until one day he is not the tradesman he thought he would be, and he does not remember when it happened.

The same logic, in mirror image, applied to how you treated the people you worked with.

You did not condescend to the apprentice either. The senior men I most respected did not have a separate, simplified manner for talking to us. They spoke to us in the same plain, slightly weary, slightly amused voice they used with each other, and they expected us to keep up. If we did not, they would slow down, briefly, and re-explain whatever it was. They would not soften it. They would not perform patience. They would just say it again, with one small thing added, and trust us to catch up. That assumption — that you were, in principle, the same kind of person they were, just earlier in the journey — is one of the most generous things anybody can do for a young person, and the trade did it for me, by reflex, every day, without thinking of it as generosity at all.

I have tried, since, to do the same for the people who have worked for me. I have not always succeeded. There are days when the temptation to talk down to someone younger or less experienced is real, especially when you are tired or under pressure, and the trade's quiet refusal of that temptation is, I now think, one of the most undervalued lessons it teaches. The workshop trains you, very early, to treat people roughly the way you would want to be treated when you were standing where they are standing. That is not a soft skill. It is the operating system underneath every team that ever works well.

## What the apprentice does not yet know

There is a thing I want to say to the version of myself who was standing in that first workshop, holding the broom, slightly resentful about it.

The work you are doing right now is not preparation for the trade. It is the trade.

The trade is not the moment when you finally strike an arc on a job that matters. The trade is the broom. The trade is the rag. The trade is the second look at the measurement, the third pass of the wire brush, the small ugly notebook in the breast pocket of the overalls. The trade is the discipline of listening to the steel and adjusting before anybody has told you to adjust. The trade is the refusal to be a different tradesman on a small job than on a big one. The trade is the assumption that the man next to you is the same kind of man you are, and the willingness to give him the same room you would want for yourself.

If you do those things, for five or six years, while everyone around you is doing them too, something quiet happens. You stop noticing that you are doing them. The habits become so deeply set into how you move through a workshop that you would no more skip the second look at a measurement than you would walk past your own father without nodding. And then, much later, when somebody hands you a different kind of job — a crew to run, a project to deliver, a budget to manage, an office with a door — you discover that the habits travel. They do not stay in the workshop. They walk into the office with you. They sit down at the desk with you. They do, quietly, a great deal of the actual work, while you sit there in a clean shirt and pretend you are managing.

I did not know any of this at the time. I do not think anybody could have told me in a way I would have understood. The trade does not teach by telling. It teaches by use, and by the slow, almost invisible accumulation of small honest habits, and by the absence of any short cut around them.

That is the part of the apprenticeship that, in the end, I am most grateful for. Not the certificate at the end. The shape of attention that the years between the beginning and the certificate quietly built into me, on days I do not particularly remember, in the gap between one boring job and the next.

## A short list, for the apprentice on his first week

If you are a young person reading this on the eve of your first day in a trade, or any other working life that demands a craft, I want to leave you with a few small things that I wish someone had set out for me plainly when I was where you are.

- **Take the broom seriously.** It is not a punishment. It is the trade beginning. Sweep the path the next man will walk, not the floor you happened to be standing on. The difference is the whole job, in miniature.
- **Get a notebook.** A small one. Put it in your pocket on the first day and keep it there. Write down everything you are taught, in your own ugly phrases, the same evening. You will not remember it. Nobody does. The people who pretend to are lying, mostly to themselves.
- **Set up before you cut.** Measure twice. Mark from one reference edge. Walk away. Come back. Look again. The five-second pause is the cheapest insurance the trade has ever invented.
- **Listen for the sound the work makes.** Grinder, hammer, weld, file. Each of them tells you the truth about what you are doing if you slow down enough to hear it. Train your ear early. It will be one of your best instruments long after your hands have slowed.
- **Do not have a small-job voice and a big-job voice.** Do the small job the way you would do the big one. Once you start to vary the standard with the size of the work, the standard belongs to the work, not to you, and you have begun the long quiet drift that ends in mediocrity.
- **Copy the older men, but do not impersonate them.** Watch how they hold their tools, how they stand, how they look at a piece before they touch it. Borrow the habits. Leave the mannerisms. You are not them. You are the next version of what they were.
- **Ask short questions.** *What am I missing?* is the best one. *Is this right?* is the second best. The worst is silence dressed up as competence, which everyone older than you can see straight through anyway.
- **Treat the rag like part of the trade.** The bench you do not wipe is the layout you get wrong. The corner you do not sweep is the trip somebody else takes. The detail you do not notice is the failure you will be asked about, much later, in a meeting where you no longer remember the job.
- **Be the same person on Tuesday that you were on Monday.** The trade is built on people who do not vary themselves by mood or circumstance. Choose, early, to be one of them. The choice gets harder to make every year you postpone it.

And remember, on the days when you cannot quite see why any of this matters: the work is also working on you. While you are shaping the steel, the steel is shaping the kind of person you are going to be in twenty years' time. There is nothing romantic in that sentence. It is simple physics, applied to character. You become, slowly, the sum of the small honest acts you perform when nobody is checking.

The next chapter is about the first real failure of mine that the workshop did not let me hide from. Up to that point I had been getting things slightly wrong, in the ordinary apprentice way, and the older men had been correcting me in the ordinary apprentice way. What happened next was different. It was the first time the trade looked at me properly and asked me, in language I could not misunderstand, whether I was going to be the kind of tradesman who learned from a hard lesson or the kind who hid from it. The answer to that question is, in some sense, the whole point of an apprenticeship, and it is where this story goes next.
