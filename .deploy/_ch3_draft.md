# Study Commencement: The Apprentice Who Refused to Stay One

The first morning I rode the cage down at Sub Nigel as a signed-on apprentice was not, in any honest sense, the beginning of my engineering career. The cage was the wrong instrument to mark the beginning. The instrument that actually marked the beginning was a kitchen chair, pulled up to the corner of a kitchen table on the row of married quarters on the East Rand, in the dark hour before the morning hooter, with a single light on above it and a kettle warming on the stove. My mother had set that chair against the corner of that table for my brother before me, and she set it against the same corner for me on the morning after I came home with the apprenticeship papers in my hand. She did not make a speech about it. She did not need to. The chair was pulled up. The light was on. The kettle was warm. The message, by then, was unmistakable.

I want to write this chapter with that chair on the page. Everything else in the years that followed — the trade test at nineteen, the diploma at twenty, the day my father was brought up from underground to watch me graduate — happened on the back of that chair. I have stood at podiums since, in suits that would have horrified my mother on the grounds of expense, and I have been given certificates printed on heavier paper than the ones the mine ever handed me. None of them have meant what the chair meant. The chair was the engine. The certificates were only the output.

## Signing on at Sub Nigel

I have written, in the previous chapter, about the decision to leave school at sixteen and to sign on as an apprentice Boilermaker at Sub Nigel Gold Mine. I do not want to repeat that decision here. I want to describe what happened on the inside of it, in the first months after the signature went on the page and the mine became the place I went to every working morning instead of the place I had only ever heard about over a kitchen table.

The signing on itself was a quiet, almost administrative event. A clerk in an office with a fan running in the corner took my details and entered them into a ledger that already had my father's name in it from twenty-odd years before. He asked me which trade I had selected. I said Boilermaker. He wrote it down in a neat, slanting hand and turned the page towards me for my signature. The contract was on a single sheet of paper. I signed it without reading every line. The mine would teach me a trade. The mine would pay me a wage during the teaching. I would, in return, give the mine the better part of my next four years and the right to require me to sit a trade test at the end of them. It was a fair bargain by the standards of the time and the place. I have not, in the fifty-seven working years since, signed many contracts I have considered as fair.

The first day in overalls is a thing every apprentice on the row remembered for life, and I am no exception. The overalls were stiff with newness. The boots were heavier than my own feet. The belt of tools at my waist was the belt of a man who did not yet know which tool to reach for first. I walked from the change-room to the workshop with the awkwardness of someone who has been issued a body he has not yet been taught to operate. The older artisans, who had been through the same first morning two or three decades earlier, did me the kindness of not laughing where I could see them. They laughed somewhere else, no doubt, and forgot about me within an hour, because the day was a day like any other and the work was the work.

What I noticed, on that first morning, was that there was a rhythm in the place, and the rhythm did not depend on me. It did not depend on any single artisan either. It depended on something larger — a shift discipline that had been built up over the working lives of three generations of men, and that an apprentice was not invited into so much as absorbed by. I have seen, in the factories of my later career, what happens to plants that have lost their rhythm. They are unmistakable. They look the same on paper but they sound wrong on the floor. Sub Nigel did not sound wrong. It sounded like a place that intended, by all available evidence, to go on doing its work.

## A trade you learn with your hands

A Boilermaker's trade is not a trade you can learn out of a textbook. You can learn the geometry of it out of a textbook. You can learn the metallurgy of it, up to a point, out of a textbook. But the trade itself sits in the hands, and the hands learn it the way they learn any honest skill — by doing it badly for long enough that the badness becomes its own form of instruction.

In those first months I cut steel badly. I welded badly. I bent plate badly. I marked out, with chalk and centre-punch, lines that were not square and arcs that were not true, and an older artisan would come past, look at the work, say nothing for a while, and then point at the place where the line had wandered. He did not lecture. Boilermakers, in my experience, do not lecture. They point. They wait. They allow the apprentice to see, for himself, why the line should not have wandered in the way it had. There is a particular humility that comes from being corrected by an older man who has chosen not to use words. I have tried, in every plant I have ever managed, to extend the same courtesy to a younger engineer. The point is enough. The lecture is for a man who has not yet learned that the work itself does the talking.

By the end of the first year, my hands had begun to behave themselves. The welds had begun to hold. The brackets had begun to run true. The lines marked out on a sheet of plate had begun to land where they were supposed to land. None of this was remarkable. Every apprentice on the row went through the same progression. What was remarkable, to me at least, was the inner change that accompanied the outer one. I had begun, without noticing it, to think of myself as a working man rather than as a school-leaver. Somewhere in the lining of my own self-description the word had quietly changed from "boy" to "tradesman", and the change was not reversible.

I have come to believe that this is the real work of an apprenticeship. The trade is the visible product. The reorientation of the apprentice's idea of himself is the invisible one, and it is the one that lasts. It is the only route in which the work, rather than the certificate, is what changes the man.

## The chair at four in the morning

I want to come back, now, to the chair at four in the morning, because nothing I have written so far in this chapter would have led where it did without it.

My mother had decided, long before I had decided anything for myself, that the trade was not going to be enough. She did not say this in so many words. She had not been raised to debate the futures of her sons across a kitchen table. She made her case the way she made every other case in her life, which was to set up the conditions under which her case became, in practice, irresistible. The chair was pulled up. The light was on. The kettle was warm. The books were stacked on the corner of the table where the sugar bowl should have been. And four in the morning, in our house on the row, became the hour at which the working apprentice was no longer a working apprentice and was, instead, a correspondence student of an engineering course the mine had agreed to sponsor.

The course itself was the mine-sponsored engineering programme that the mining houses ran in those years for promising tradesmen. The arrangement was a sober one. The mine paid the fees. The student paid the time. The textbooks arrived by post, in brown-paper packages tied with string, and the assignments went back the same way, in envelopes my mother kept ready on the dresser. The lectures, where there were lectures, were in the evenings on the nights I could get there from the workshop in time to sit at the back with my overalls still smelling of the day.

I do not want to pretend, at this distance, that the studies were easy. They were not. There is a particular tiredness that belongs to a young man who has spent a full shift on the bench, has sat at a lecture hall for two hours on mathematics he is not entirely on top of, has slept for four or five hours, and has then been woken in the dark by his mother for another hour at the kitchen table before the morning hooter. That tiredness sits in the bones. I carried it for years. I suspect that a great deal of the early-morning discipline I have been credited with, over a long working life, is simply the residue of those years. The body remembers the hour. The body does not forget the chair.

What I want to say, more than anything, is that the chair was not a punishment. It did not feel like a punishment at the time, and it does not feel like one in retrospect. The chair was a small, daily act of confidence on the part of a shy woman who believed that her youngest son was capable of more than the row had so far asked of him. She did not lecture me on that confidence either. She demonstrated it, in the only currency she had — the hour before the day, the light on, the kettle warm, the books waiting. I have not, in the fifty-seven working years since, been the recipient of a more eloquent vote of confidence from any chairman of any board.

## The trade test at nineteen

The trade test, when it came, came in the fourth year of the apprenticeship, which made me nineteen years old. I have been asked, on more than one occasion in later life, whether nineteen is too young for that kind of qualification. I do not think it is. I think the conditions under which nineteen is too young have to do with the apprentice and not with the age, and the conditions under which the apprentice has been raised either get him to that test in working condition or they do not. Mine had.

The test itself was conducted, as it was for every apprentice of my generation, by a panel of older artisans and supervising engineers in the mine workshop. There was a written component, which I do not remember in any detail. There was a practical component, which I remember almost too well. You were given a set of drawings, a sheet of plate, a length of section, a welding set, a marking-out table, and a deadline. You were expected to produce, by the end of the day, a piece of work that conformed to the drawings within the tolerances the panel had agreed upon in advance. The panel walked the floor while you worked. They did not interfere. They did not comment. They watched the cut, they watched the weld, they watched the way you handled your tools, and they made their notes on a clipboard.

I do not remember every detail of the piece of work I produced that day. I remember that the geometry was honest. I remember that the welds held. I remember that the panel, at the end of the afternoon, looked at the finished article, looked at each other, and made the small economical nods that older artisans use when a younger one has done what they had hoped he would do. There was no speech. The trade test, like the trade itself, did not require one.

The certificate followed in due course. I was a qualified Boilermaker Artisan, at nineteen years old, in a workshop on the East Rand, on the strength of a panel of men whose judgement I respected more than I have respected any panel since. The wage scale changed. The overalls did not. What changed was the small badge of competence I carried around inside my chest from that afternoon onwards. I had been tested. The test had been honest. The result had been entered in the books. I was, by any reasonable definition, a tradesman.

I did not, on the evening of the trade test, allow myself to feel as if I had arrived. There were two reasons for this. The first was that my mother, who had pulled the chair up to the corner of the table for four years, would have considered any visible celebration to be premature and faintly indecorous. The second was that the trade test, by my own private reckoning, had only ever been the first of the two qualifications I had set myself. There was a diploma still to come, and the diploma was the one that interested me more.

## The diploma at twenty

The Mechanical Engineering Diploma, in the mine-sponsored programme of those years, was the qualification that opened the door out of the workshop and into the drawing-office, and from the drawing-office, in due course, into the planning room, and from the planning room, in further due course, into the offices in which decisions about whole sections of the mine were made. I did not know, at the age of twenty, that the diploma would do all of that work. I knew only that it was the next step, and that my mother had set the chair up against the corner of the table for the next step in the same way she had set it up for the first one.

I sat the final examinations of the diploma in the year I turned twenty. The course had been built around the constraints of a working apprentice — evening lectures, correspondence material, occasional block-release weeks at a technical college. There was, by then, a small and not very visible cohort of us in the workshop who were sitting the diploma in parallel with one another. Most of them were Fitters and Turners. None of them, as far as I am aware, were Boilermakers. The diploma was thought of by the older artisans as a Fitter's qualification, on the loose grounds that a Fitter spent his day on tolerances and threads and clearances and was therefore considered to be the natural candidate for engineering theory. I will not litigate that view at this distance. I will say only that a Boilermaker who has spent four years marking out plate, cutting it, bending it, and joining it within a tolerance that has to hold under load is no stranger to the geometry, materials science and stress calculation that the engineering examinations were going to ask him about. The diploma did not, in any sense I can identify, sit awkwardly on a Boilermaker.

When the results came through, I had passed the diploma. I had become, in the records of the mine, the first Boilermaker ever to have done so at Sub Nigel. I do not say this to gloat. The mine had qualified Fitters with that diploma for a long time. The mine had qualified Turners with that diploma. It had not, until then, qualified a Boilermaker. I was twenty years old. I was, by the same records, the youngest qualified artisan on the mine. The combination of those two facts produced, in the front office of the mine, a small administrative ripple that I had not anticipated and that nobody in the workshop had prepared me for. The mine, it turned out, intended to make something of it.

## The day my father was called up

The graduation ceremony was held at the mine, in the way the mine held those ceremonies in those years, with the senior management on a small stage at one end of a hall, the qualifying tradesmen and engineers in rows of chairs in front of them, and the families of the qualifiers in further rows of chairs behind. There were no gowns of the kind a university hands out. There was a certificate, a handshake, a photograph for the company newsletter, and a short address by one of the senior engineers about the importance of the trade and the importance of the technical education that had been built up around it. It was a sober, plain, characteristically mining-house occasion. I had not invited a great many people. There would have been no point. The two people I had invited were the two people who had built the chair-and-kettle apparatus that had carried me to the ceremony in the first place, and one of them — my father — was on shift underground when the ceremony began.

I had not expected the mine to do what it did next. The senior engineer who read the citation paused, when he reached my name, and made a small announcement to the hall. He said that the mine had taken the unusual step of calling Mr Anton's father up from underground for the duration of the ceremony, so that he could be in the hall when his son was handed his certificate. There was a small, almost startled silence in the rows in front of me. The men in the rows knew, in the way miners know these things without being told, what it meant for a working miner to be brought up off shift in the middle of the day. It did not happen. It was not what the mine did. The cage went down at the start of the shift, and the cage came up at the end of the shift, and the only event that interrupted that sequence was an event that no one in the hall wanted to think about. The mine had set that sequence aside for one afternoon, for the benefit of a man on a hook in a change-room whose youngest son was about to be handed a piece of paper in his name.

My father walked into the hall in his shift clothes. He had not had time to change. The dust of the shaft was still on his sleeves. He found his row, and he sat down in it, and he did not say anything to anyone. The address continued. The citations continued. My name was called. I walked up to the stage. The handshake was given. The certificate was handed across. The photograph was taken. The senior engineer said something I no longer remember about a Boilermaker and a diploma and a first for the mine. I walked back to my seat. My father did not stand up. He did not applaud above the level of the rest of the hall. He sat in his row, in his shift clothes, and he looked at me from the rows behind in the way a working man looks at his son when the son has done something the working man himself was never going to be in a position to do.

I have not, in the years since that afternoon, been more steadily and durably moved by anything that has happened in any hall I have ever been in. The certificate, eventually, went into a drawer. The photograph, eventually, went into an album. What has not been forgotten was the fact that the mine had called my father up from underground so that he could be in the hall. The mine had done it without being asked. I suspect, looking back, that it had been one of the older engineers in the company — one of the men who had taken a quiet interest in my studies — who had arranged it. I never asked. It would have spoiled the moment to know the name of the arranger. The arrangement was the point.

My mother sat beside my father. She had her hands in her lap. She did not say anything during the ceremony either. She did not need to. The chair, by then, had said everything she was ever going to say on the subject. The hour before the day had said it. The kettle had said it. The books on the corner of the table had said it. The light on at four in the morning had said it. The afternoon in the hall, with my father called up from underground in his shift clothes, was only the small visible event that the long invisible labour of a kitchen at four in the morning had been quietly producing all along.

## What the two qualifications gave me

I want to end this chapter with what the two qualifications actually gave me, because the qualifications, in themselves, are not the thing I would want a younger reader to take away.

The first qualification — the trade test — gave me a working definition of competence. Competence, on the East Rand workshop floor, was not a feeling. It was not a confidence. It was not a self-description. It was a measurable, panel-tested, drawing-and-tolerance event that either happened or did not happen on a particular afternoon in front of a particular set of older artisans. I have carried that definition into every plant I have managed since. Competence, in any factory I have ever walked into, is the question of whether the work conforms to the drawings within the tolerances that have been agreed upon in advance. Everything else is decoration.

The second qualification — the diploma — gave me a working definition of ambition. Ambition, in the household I had been raised in, was not a sin and it was not a virtue. It was a tool. You used ambition to lift a household budget out of the territory it had been stuck in. You used ambition to put a younger generation on a slightly different track from the one the older generation had walked on. You did not, in our house, brandish ambition. You did not announce it. You set up the chair at four in the morning, you woke the boy in the dark, and you let the ambition do its quiet work over a long stretch of years. I have, in every business I have ever helped to turn around, tried to bring some shadow of that posture into the planning room. The strategy is the strategy. The chair at four in the morning is the thing that decides whether the strategy actually arrives at a result.

I had gone in through the door of the mine as an apprentice at sixteen, on the back of a brother who had walked the path one year ahead of me. I had come out, at twenty, with a trade test, a Mechanical Engineering Diploma, the small additional weight of being the youngest qualified artisan on the mine and the first Boilermaker ever to have earned a diploma there, and the memory of a father in shift clothes in a hall on a particular afternoon. The mine, by then, had given me a great deal more than it had bargained for when the clerk with the fan in the corner of his office had written "Boilermaker" in his slanting hand four years earlier. The mine had paid the fees. My mother had set up the chair. My father had gone down on his shift every working day of the four years it had taken to get to the hall. And I, having taken what the mine had been prepared to give and having added to it what the chair and the shift had been prepared to give, was, by the end of that afternoon, no longer an apprentice and, in a quiet way I did not yet announce to anyone, no longer a man who intended to stay on the mine.

The intention to leave was older than the diploma, as I have already written. The diploma did not invent it. The diploma only confirmed that the intention had earned the right to act on itself. What the apprentice years had given me was not a place to stay. They had given me a place to leave from honourably, with a trade in my hands, a diploma on my certificate, and the small, durable memory of an afternoon in a hall on the East Rand in which the mine, of its own accord, had brought my father up from underground to watch.
