# BEVCO, Future Life and Still Going at Seventy-Three

There is a particular silence that arrives the day you cross sixty-five. The retirement notices have been signed, the leaving cards read out, and the people who promised to stay in touch have already begun the slow, polite drift back into their own diaries. The garden is suddenly visible from the kitchen window in a way it never quite was before. The phone, for a week or two, sits very still. And the question that arrives in that silence, whether you welcome it or not, is the only question that really matters at that age: are you finished, or are you only between contracts?

I was not finished. I have never, in fact, been particularly good at being finished. Even as a boy on the East Rand, watching my father come up out of the shaft with the dust of Sub Nigel in the creases of his hands, I had a sense that work was not something done to a person but something a person did with the time they were given. My father did it with a pick and a lamp. I have done it with a clipboard and an OEE screen. The instrument changes; the obligation does not. So when the retirement letters were signed and the cake was eaten and the corporate balloons came down, what I felt was not relief, and certainly not nostalgia. What I felt was the quiet, almost shy curiosity of a man who suspects that the most interesting third of his career might still be ahead of him.

It turned out that it was.

## A contract called BEVCO

The first phone call of that third act came from a face I knew very well. The Tiger Brands executive who had originally recruited me into the confectionary group, the man who had handed me the failed two-hundred-million-rand mogul line and watched me put it back into production in six months, had moved on himself. He had been appointed Supply Chain Director of BEVCO, a beverage company in Johannesburg, and he had not forgotten the way I worked. Toward the end of my Tiger Brands contract he picked up the phone and asked, in the easy way of a man who already knows the answer, whether I would come and help him with a beverages business.

I have learned, over the years, to take such calls seriously. The single most reliable currency in a long career is not your CV, no matter how carefully you have decorated it. It is the people who have worked next to you in difficult years and would, without hesitation, work next to you again. I had finished on a high note at Tiger Brands. I had finished on a high note at every company before it. That habit, which I had begun almost by accident in my AECI years, was now compounding. Every closing handshake had quietly become the opening line of the next contract.

BEVCO was a beverage business in the truest sense of the phrase: high volumes, thin margins, ferocious competition, and a customer base that does not forgive a stockout. In the world of pharmaceuticals, where I had spent the long Adcock Ingram years, your moat is regulatory; the product itself is difficult to make and impossible to fake. In confectionary, your moat is brand and distribution. But in beverages, where every cent counts and every shelf is contested, your moat is operating cost. Lose a few percentage points of productivity in a beverage plant and you have not lost an inconvenience. You have lost the business.

I walked the lines as I always did. I have a routine I have followed at every company since SA Oil Mills, and it has never failed me: in the first two weeks I do not write a single email of substance, I do not make a single promise, and I do not propose a single capital project. I walk. I stand on the line next to the operators. I watch a packing machine for an hour without speaking. I count the small stoppages, the micro-changeovers, the inputs that are out of specification, the bottle of cleaning fluid that has been put down in the wrong place because somebody, three months ago, decided it was easier than walking the extra ten metres. I read the maintenance logs and the deviation reports. I have lunch with the shift supervisors. By the end of those two weeks I know what is broken, and almost always I know who knows how to fix it, even if they have stopped telling anyone.

At BEVCO the answer was productivity. The plant could move, but it could not move the way a modern beverage plant has to move. We attacked the small stoppages first, because in a high-speed line the small stoppages are everything; they look insignificant on any single shift, but compounded over a year they cost you a fortune in lost output. We tightened changeovers, sharpened the maintenance routines, and brought the operators into the conversation rather than leaving them outside it. We focused on cost savings through improved productivity, because in a competitive beverage business there is no other option. The man who had recruited me wanted improvements, and over the year I spent there we delivered them.

It was a short contract by the standards of my earlier life. A year, no more. But the BEVCO experience taught me something specific about the new shape of my career, something I had only half understood at the end of the Tiger Brands stint. The old model, the one I had lived for thirty years, was to join a company, climb inside it, and grow with it. The new model was different. Companies in trouble needed a transformation engine for twelve months, eighteen months, perhaps two years, and then they needed their own people to run what had been transformed. My role, increasingly, was to leave a stronger company behind me than I had found, and then to leave. That had always been my private definition of leadership. Now it was becoming, more openly, my business.

## Back to Tiger Brands, this time across the country

The phone rang again, and again I knew the voice on the other end. Tiger Brands wanted me back. They had decided that the confectionary group, all of it, needed the same hand that had repaired the mogul line and improved the candy and chocolate operations. This time the role was bigger and the geography was different. I was offered a contract as Supply Chain Director for the confectionary business, with the plants now running across the country and the heart of the operation in Durban.

I accepted. I had finished on a high note last time. I had a relationship with the team and with the executives who had hired me. The deciding factor, however, was something quieter than any of those things. It was the conviction, which had only deepened with each contract, that I could now walk into any business, of any size, in any sector, and within two weeks tell the board where the leverage was. I had been training for that capability my whole working life without knowing it: the years on the shaft, the years on the chemical plant, the years in pharmaceuticals, the leather years at Kolosus, the oil mills, the exports, the candies. Each of them had been a different angle on the same underlying machine. The machine is always a machine. The product changes; the operating principles do not.

The new Tiger Brands contract gave me responsibility for the supply chain across all the confectionary plants. Gums and jellies, the line I had already restored. Candy and chocolate. The operations I had transformed before were no longer on probation; they were running, they had earned their certifications, and the people who ran them were the same people I had stood next to in the early months. There is a particular pleasure in returning to a plant you have transformed and finding that the discipline you put in has held. The 5S markings are still on the floor. The morning huddle is still happening at the start of every shift. The real-time monitoring screen is still on the wall, still telling the truth, still being looked at. The transformation has become the culture, which is the only kind of transformation that actually lasts.

What I had not expected, when I returned to the Durban site, was the welcome. The staff celebrated my return. They jumped up and down and embraced me like a member of the family who had been away too long. Over a long career I have received many gifts from companies. A handshake from a chairman. A bonus letter. A leaving plaque, even one or two awkward speeches. But I have never received a gift quite like that one. It was, very simply, proof that the Trust-and-Inspire philosophy I had built my management style around was not theoretical, was not academic, was not an item from a textbook. It was something the people on the floor had felt, had remembered, and had been willing to demonstrate when I came back.

For the next year I led that supply chain. We protected what had been built. We pushed productivity further. We kept food-safety standards where they needed to be, which in confectionary means above the level of the certifications, not at it. I have always believed that compliance is the minimum bar of a serious business, not the target. The target is to be embarrassed when an auditor finds something you should already have fixed.

And then, before that year was out, Tiger Brands made another request. The Black Cat peanut butter plant in Randfontein had problems, and they wanted me to go and look at it.

## Black Cat and the lesson of the raw material

I have, in the various chapters of this life, transformed many factories. Some of them were misbehaving for management reasons, some for capital reasons, some because a previous executive had bought a plant that did not quite fit the product. Black Cat was different. Black Cat was misbehaving because of the peanut.

Peanut butter is a deceptively complicated product. To a consumer it is a jar of one ingredient, ground and salted, perhaps sweetened. To a manufacturer it is a chemistry experiment that depends on the raw material more than most processors are willing to admit. The peanut comes in graded by size, by oil content, by moisture, by the proportion of broken kernels, by residual aflatoxin testing, by season, by supplier, by field. Get the wrong peanut and there is no roasting profile in the world that will rescue the product downstream.

When I arrived at the Black Cat plant the line was producing, but it was not producing well. There were processing problems linked, I quickly suspected, to the quality of the raw materials coming in. The people who ran the plant had been treating it as a manufacturing problem. They had been adjusting screws on machines and reviewing the maintenance logs of grinders. I have nothing against adjusting screws on machines, but if the peanut going into the front of the line is variable, no amount of fine-tuning at the back of the line will save you. The first hour I spent in the goods-receiving area told me where most of the answer lay.

I implemented the same toolkit I had brought to every previous Tiger Brands plant. The real-time production monitoring system went in, so that the OEE numbers were on the wall and could not be hidden in a monthly report. We put in the same standards, the same housekeeping discipline, the same audit cadence that the candy and chocolate plants now took for granted. Then I worked with the procurement team and the technical team to attack the raw material problem at its source. We tightened the specifications. We changed the way we tested incoming peanuts. We worked with suppliers, in some cases ruthlessly, to bring consistency to what was arriving at our gates.

Output from the plant increased tremendously. The food-safety position was reinforced. The quality certifications, which had been under quiet pressure, were stabilised and improved. Another successful contract period. Another full bonus. Another company left in a better state than I had found it.

It bears noting, because I think it matters, that all of this work, the BEVCO year, the Tiger Brands Supply Chain Director year, the Black Cat year, was done under contract. I had passed the formal retirement age of sixty-five some time before. By the time I closed off the Black Cat assignment I had been working eight years past my retirement age. I was, on the day I packed up my office, seventy-three.

If you are a younger person reading this, especially someone in your fifties wondering whether your best work is behind you, I want you to hear this part very clearly. The single most powerful asset I have at seventy-three is not my CV. It is not even my track record, although that has helped me. It is the dense, quiet network of relationships I built across forty years of work, every one of them watered by the discipline of finishing on a high note. The phone calls that have come to me in this third act have all come from people who knew me in the second. Word of mouth, in a career, is the slowest compounding force there is, and also the most reliable. You are never too old to be successful if you produce quality work. The market will find you, because the people who have worked alongside you will find you for the market.

## The Future Life chapter

The headhunter who called me at the end of the Black Cat contract was talking about a company called Future Life. I knew the brand. Most South African families do. It is a powder cereal business, a porridge brand, that had grown by ten to twenty percent per annum for ten years on the back of a clear nutritional proposition and a loyal consumer base. That kind of growth, sustained over a decade, will outgrow any factory eventually. Future Life had reached the moment in its history where the building it had grown up in could no longer carry the business it had become. The company had moved from its older premises to a new site in Durban, and the new business needed a new operating discipline. The old site, by their own honest assessment, did not comply with food-safety standards. Many standards were lacking. They needed someone who had spent a career raising standards, and who could do it without breaking the growth engine in the process.

I relished it.

The seventeen months that followed were, by my own measure, the most successful contract period of the entire third act. Not because the work was harder than what had come before, although it was hard, but because the new Future Life site allowed me to exercise the full range of what I had spent fifty years learning. There is a particular gift in being asked to apply everything at once. The pharmaceutical instincts from Adcock Ingram. The 5S discipline from the Japanese fool-proof philosophy that had become a permanent operating instinct for me. The OEE measurement work from the Tiger Brands confectionary turnarounds. The food-safety standards that had, by then, become as natural to me as breathing. The high-speed packing-line procurement experience from the AECI years. The leadership style I had been refining for four decades.

The first thing I did, as I always do, was walk the plant. I did not promise anything in the first fortnight. I observed. I sat with the operators on the lines. I watched a powder packing machine for an unbroken hour and counted every minor stop. I read the food-safety audit reports and the customer complaint log. I had quiet lunches with the engineering team and the QC team. I let the people who knew the place tell me what they would change if they were given permission to change it. By the end of week two I had a draft of what was needed. By the end of week four I had a plan the executive team had bought into. By the end of month two we were executing.

The results, when they came, were significant in a way that satisfies an old operating mind. We delivered massive bottom-line savings. Profitability improved tremendously. I installed a real-time production monitoring system, the same approach that had worked at every Tiger Brands plant, and it simplified the operation enormously. Once the OEE numbers are visible on a screen at the end of the line, you do not need to argue with anybody about whether a machine is performing. The machine tells you. The operators know it. The supervisors know it. Management knows it. The conversation stops being about blame and starts being about improvement.

The most consequential intervention, however, was the new high-speed packing line. Future Life had been running six days a week to meet demand. Six days, week in and week out, with all the overtime, all the maintenance pressure, and all the labour cost that a six-day operation entails. I specified, procured and installed a new high-speed packing line that lifted output dramatically. The result was that we could now meet demand by working only four days a week. The labour savings were substantial. The maintenance windows opened up. The shift rosters became saner. Productivity improved across the board. Food-safety standards were upgraded everywhere. The plant was a different operation from the one I had walked into seventeen months earlier.

I closed that contract at the end of March, on the high note I have always insisted on for myself. The team celebrated. The executives wrote letters that I have kept. The handover was clean. The systems were running. The standards had been embedded. The OEE screens were on the walls and being watched. The four-day-week pattern was holding. The new packing line was producing. There was nothing left for me to do in that plant that the existing team could not do better and more sustainably than I could.

## Still going at seventy-three

So here I am.

Seventy-three years old. A wife of fifty-one years, who has carried this whole journey with me through every overseas trip and every contract relocation, and who continues to be the steadiest centre of gravity in my life. Four children, all grown. Nine grandchildren. Three great-grandchildren. A long career that, at every reasonable point in the last decade, the world has expected me to wind down. And a phone that, against every demographic prediction, has continued to ring.

The plan from here is not to retire. The plan from here is to formalise the third act.

I am establishing my own Business Transformation Company, fronted on LinkedIn, designed to reach the influential leaders and boards who are quietly wondering whether their operations are as productive, as safe and as profitable as they could be, and who would benefit from a hand that has done this many times before. I have superb testimonials from every assignment of the contract years. I have the BEVCO experience, the second Tiger Brands stint, the Black Cat turnaround, the Future Life transformation, and the long pre-contract history before any of those. I have the relationships from forty years of finishing on a high note. I have the body of work to prove that what I do works, and the willingness to put my name in front of the next group of leaders who need a transformation.

It should be mentioned, because it has shaped the work in ways that are not obvious from the outside, that across all of these contracts I have personally carried the responsibility for industrial relations. The annual wage negotiations. The hard conversations about retrenchments where the business required them. The collaborations with shop stewards. The disciplinary hearings that no manager ever enjoys but every senior manager must conduct fairly. That work has built up, year by year, into something I now regard as a separate qualification, an unwritten degree in human relations under pressure. It is not a degree I would wish on a young manager early; the strain is real. But by the third act of a career it is the kind of experience that allows you to walk into any negotiation in the country, on either side of the table, and have a sense of where the room actually is.

I want to underline one more lesson, because it is the one I would most want a younger version of myself to hear. Relationship building is not a soft skill. It is not a sideline to the technical work. It is, in the long arc of a career, the single most important asset you will ever build, and you will only realise this in the years when you have stopped trying to impress anyone. The people you treat well in your thirties are the people who will recruit you in your sixties. The people you take time over in your forties are the people who will write your testimonials in your seventies. Every closing handshake of every contract becomes, in the fullness of time, an opening line. Lean on those relationships when you need them; build them when you do not.

And finish on a high note. Always finish on a high note. Walk out of every company with the standards higher than when you arrived, with the people better trained than when you found them, with the books in better shape than they were on the day you signed the contract, and with a clean office and a courteous goodbye. You may never set foot in that building again. Or, more likely than you think, you may walk back in on a contract ten years later to a team that jumps up and down at your return. Either way, the standard of the leaving is the standard of the legacy.

Who knows what is next.

I do not say that with any anxiety. I say it with the same curiosity I felt as a sixteen-year-old apprentice at Sub Nigel, walking into the first morning of the trade and wondering, quietly and privately, just how far this might go. The instrument has changed many times since then. The clipboard has been an OEE screen, a packing-line specification, a leather negotiation in Paris, a chemical plant in Sasolburg, a peanut butter procurement spec, a porridge factory in Durban. The next instrument may very well be a LinkedIn message and a first remote conversation with a board I have not yet met. That is fine with me. The work is the same.

You are never too old to be useful. You are never too old to be needed. You are never too old to take a struggling business by the shoulders and walk it, step by patient step, into the shape it was always meant to be in. The Lord has given me seventy-three years, a faithful wife, a family I am proud of, and a body of experience that, by His grace, still has something to offer.

The cake has long since been eaten, the balloons have long since come down, and the phone, for what it is worth, is still ringing.

I am going to keep answering it.
