# Leading Without a Title

There is a particular moment, in the working life of a man who is going to end up running things, when he discovers, with a small private shock, that the men around him have begun to ask his opinion about work that is not, strictly, his to have an opinion about.

Nobody hands you anything on that morning. There is no jacket. There is no badge. There is no announcement on the noticeboard. The foreman has not pulled you aside in any corridor and told you that something has changed. By every visible measure, nothing has. You are still standing in the same bay, in the same overalls, with the same lunch in the same tin in the same locker you have used since the apprenticeship. The wage slip at the end of the week will not be any different. The org chart, if anybody had ever bothered to draw one for the shop, would not have moved you a single line up its column.

And yet a small thing has changed, and you can feel it, and once you have felt it you do not unfeel it.

A man who has been in the trade ten years longer than you walks over to your bench in the middle of the morning and says, *Tell me what you would do with this one, my boy.* He does not say it as a test. He has not come to check on you. He has come because he genuinely wants to know what you would do, and he has decided, without saying so, that your answer is going to be worth hearing.

Or the apprentice on the next bay over, who has been there about six months, comes and stands at your elbow with a piece of work in his hand and a question he is too embarrassed to take to the foreman. He has chosen you. He has not been told to choose you. He has looked around the shop and decided, with whatever instinct apprentices use, that you are the man whose answer he wants. There has not been any conversation about it. He has simply arrived. And when you have answered him, he has gone back to his bench, and a week later he is back, and a week after that he is back, and after a few months you realise, without quite knowing how it happened, that you have a small unofficial flow of questions coming at you that nobody has formally routed your way.

Or the customer rings, and somebody in the office, instead of putting the call through to the foreman the way they always have, puts it through to your bay, because somebody somewhere — you will never quite work out who — has decided that the question the customer is asking is the kind of question you are now expected to handle.

This chapter is about that period, in my own working life, between qualifying and being given any formal title, when other people had quietly started looking to me for direction and I had not yet caught up with the fact that they were doing it. I want to write about it carefully, because I think it is one of the least understood passages in a working career, and because almost everything I have learned since about leadership was first taught to me, without any of us realising it was happening, in the months and years I am about to describe.

## What I had not been promoted to

I want to begin by being precise about what I was, on the floor, in the period this chapter covers, because the language we now use about leadership tends to skip over the very stage that matters most.

I had qualified. I had the ticket. I had been a tradesman in good standing for somewhere between, I think, eighteen months and two years by the time I noticed any of this happening. I was not a charge-hand. I was not a foreman. I was not an acting anything. I had not been called into any office and offered any extra rand on the wage. I was, on paper, exactly what every other newly qualified tradesman around me was: a man with a bench, a set of tools, a clock card, and the same work pouring onto him every morning that was pouring onto all the others.

The only thing that distinguished me from the men beside me, in those months, was something none of us would have been able to name at the time.

It was that I had begun, slowly, to be **trusted**. Not loved. Not admired. Trusted, in the specific narrow sense that men on a working floor mean the word — trusted to give a straight answer, trusted not to take advantage of a confidence, trusted not to humiliate a younger man who came over with a question, trusted to deliver what I said I would deliver by the time I said I would deliver it, trusted to admit when I had got something wrong before anybody else had to point it out. That was the whole of it, in those months. None of it was visible. None of it would have appeared on any review. But it had begun to gather around me, in a way I had not designed and could not, at the time, have described, and the gathering of it was the thing that made everything that came later possible.

I think it is one of the most useful distinctions a young man at this stage of his working life can learn to draw. There is a difference between **authority** and **influence**, and the order in which a man acquires them tells you almost everything about what kind of leader he is going to turn into.

Authority is what the company gives you. It comes with a title, a stripe on the jacket, a desk somewhere, a line on the org chart. The day you are given it, you have it. You did not have it the day before. You do, now. The men below you on the chart are required, at least in principle, to do what you tell them, because the company has agreed to back you up if they do not.

Influence is what the men give you, quietly, over time, before anybody in the company has noticed what has happened. It is not granted. It is not allocated. It does not appear in any document. It accumulates, the way oil accumulates in a sump — a drop at a time, over months, from a hundred small interactions in which you either did or did not behave like a man whose opinion was worth weighing. By the time you have a usable amount of it, you will not be able to remember when it started. You will only notice, one morning, that you have it, and that it is being acted on by men whom no one has ever ordered to act on it.

The men I have most admired, across a long working life, all acquired influence before they were ever given authority. The men I have least admired, almost without exception, acquired authority before they ever bothered to earn any influence. The first set of men ran things well. The second set ran things badly, often without ever working out why. It is a distinction the trade does not teach explicitly. But it teaches it, in the texture of every shop I ever worked in, to anyone who is paying attention.

## The first time I noticed it

I want to give you a specific morning, because abstractions about influence are not worth much without a moment you can stand inside.

I had been on the floor, as a qualified tradesman, for somewhere a little over a year. I cannot tell you the exact date. I can tell you the job. It was a pressure vessel modification on a tank that had been in service somewhere on the Reef for, I think, about a decade, and had been brought back into the shop for a set of nozzle replacements and a partial inspection. [unverified] It was not, in itself, an unusually difficult job. It was the kind of job the shop saw two or three of in a month. The drawings were straightforward. The materials were normal. The deadline was the deadline.

What was not normal was that the foreman, who had been managing the job himself for the first three days, had been called away to deal with something else — a delivery problem, I think, or a customer in the office, or one of those small fires that consume a foreman's morning and pull him three bays in three directions at once. He had been standing at the bay where the work was being done, walked off, and not come back.

There were four men on the job, including me. Two of them were a fair bit senior to me in years on the floor, although they had qualified, I think, around the same time I had. One was newer — possibly an apprentice, possibly a very junior tradesman, I cannot now reconstruct it accurately.

The job had hit a small snag. The fit on one of the nozzles was not landing the way the drawing had suggested it would, and it was the kind of snag that, left for another twenty minutes, was going to start eating the rest of the day. None of the men on the bay was the formal lead. The foreman would normally have been the one to call the next move. But the foreman was, as I have said, somewhere else.

There was a small awkward silence.

And then, without anybody saying anything about it, the three other men turned and looked at me.

I am writing that as if it was dramatic. It was not. It was, in fact, almost invisible. There was no conversation about whose call it was. There was no debate. The two senior men did not nominate me. The junior man did not ask. The four of us had simply been standing around the workpiece, and the question of what to do next had risen into the air between us, and three pairs of eyes had landed on me at almost the same moment, with the same quiet expectation behind them, and the choreography of the morning had decided, without consulting any of us, that I was the man who was going to answer.

I answered. I had no special authority to answer. I am not even sure my answer was the best one available. But I gave it. I said how I thought we should adjust. I gave my reasons in about three sentences. The senior men listened, nodded, and went back to work. The junior man went to fetch the tool I had asked him to fetch. The job moved on. The snag was sorted, more or less, within the next half hour. The foreman came back somewhere around lunch. Nobody told him what had happened. He did not ask.

I went home that evening and sat in my chair for a long time before I went to bed, because I had understood, somewhere between the workshop and my front door, that something had happened that day that was larger than the small adjustment to the nozzle, and I did not yet have any language for what it was.

What had happened, I came to see later, was that I had been *appointed* — not by the foreman, not by the company, not by any document — but by the other three men on the bay, in a wordless vote I had not asked them to take, to the position of *the man who answers when there is no foreman in the room*. They had handed me that, in the space of about four seconds, without any of them noticing what they were doing. And once they had handed it to me, they were not going to take it back. The same thing would happen again, on the next job, with a different snag, with a different mix of men, with the foreman absent for a different reason. And each time it happened, the thing they had quietly handed me would get a little heavier and a little more real.

I do not think I slept well that night. I was not anxious about it. I was, if anything, the opposite of anxious. But the floor of my working life had moved a fraction under my feet, and I was lying in the dark trying to feel out which direction it had moved in.

## What I had been doing in the months before

I want to spend a section on this, because the most useful thing a young man can know about influence is that it does not appear out of nowhere. It is paid for, in advance, by small habits practised long before anybody is watching you for them. By the time those habits start producing results, you cannot point at the moment you bought them. You can only point at the years in which you, without quite knowing why, kept the receipts.

I want to be honest about what I had been doing in the eighteen months between the ticket arriving and the morning at the bay, because none of it was a strategy. I was not playing a long game. I was not, in any deliberate sense, building a base for the next thing. I was doing a set of small things because they felt right to me, and because the men I most respected on the floor seemed to do them too. The fact that they turned out, in retrospect, to be exactly the things that produced the morning at the bay, was a coincidence I would not have been able to predict and that I want now to lay out for any younger man who finds himself at the same stage.

**I had been keeping my word.** Not in the grand sense. In the small sense. If I said I would finish a job by Thursday, I finished it by Thursday or I told somebody on Wednesday that I was not going to. If I borrowed a tool, I returned the tool, and I returned it cleaner than I had borrowed it. If I said I would cover for a man's bay for half an hour while he stepped out, I covered it for half an hour, not twenty-five minutes. None of these were heroic acts. None of them were the kind of thing you would put on a CV. But every single one of them was being silently logged, in the heads of the men around me, into a running tally of *can this one be trusted, or can he not*. After eighteen months of small entries on the right side of that ledger, the ledger had a balance on it that I did not know existed and that the men around me were starting to draw against.

**I had been admitting it when I was wrong.** Early. Loudly enough that the relevant person heard me, quietly enough that I did not make a performance of it. If I had welded something that was not up to standard, I said so before the inspector did. If I had laid a job out and the layout was wrong, I said so before somebody else had to. The old men in the shop respected that more than they respected almost anything else, because they had spent their working lives watching young tradesmen try to hide mistakes that everybody could see, and they knew exactly what kind of work that habit produced in the long run. A man who admits early is a man who learns fast. A man who does not is a man whose mistakes accumulate, in private, into a quiet rot inside the work, and the shop floor can smell that kind of rot at a hundred paces.

**I had been doing the awkward jobs.** I wrote about this in the previous chapter, and I do not want to repeat myself, but I will say that the habit of taking the awkward job continued to compound through that period, and it compounded in a way that I had not predicted. The men who had been watching me take the awkward jobs as an apprentice had carried that observation forward into how they thought about me as a qualified tradesman. They had decided, without telling me, that I was a man who did not flinch, and the deciding of it changed the kind of work that landed on my bay and the kind of question that landed in my ear.

**I had been keeping my mouth shut about other men's work.** That is one I want to be careful about, because it is easy to romanticise and easy to misunderstand. I am not saying I had been pretending other men's mistakes did not exist. I am saying that I had not made a habit, in the canteen or at the smoke break, of commenting on them in the way a certain kind of young tradesman quickly learns to do. There is a temptation, when you are newly qualified, to demonstrate your own competence by being publicly amused at the incompetence of your peers. It feels, at the time, like the cheapest possible way to look good. It is, in the long run, the most expensive habit a man on a working floor can pick up. The men I most respected had never done it. I had copied that, perhaps the most deliberately of any of the habits I am describing. And the cumulative effect of eighteen months of not doing it was that men felt safe around me, in a way that they did not always feel around the men who did. The safety that other men feel around you, I came to understand much later, is one of the materials out of which influence is made.

**I had been answering carefully.** When men did ask me things, even small things, I had been giving them answers that were the best I could give them at the time, and I had been giving them in plain language, and I had been saying *I am not sure* whenever I was not sure. I had not been bluffing. I had not been embellishing. I had not been trying to make myself look cleverer than I was. The shop has an exquisite, almost cruel ability to detect bluffing in a young qualified man, and it punishes it for years afterwards in a hundred small ways that the bluffer himself never quite traces back to the original sin. I had, by some combination of nerves and good luck, avoided that trap. The men around me had noticed, in their wordless way, and they had filed me, in their heads, under *that one will give you a straight answer*.

None of these were leadership techniques. None of them were in any book I had read, because I had not at the time read any books on the subject. They were just the small day-by-day behaviours of a young tradesman who was trying, without any particular plan, to be a man the older men did not mind having around. The fact that those behaviours added up, by the morning at the bay, to a small invisible promotion that no one had signed off on, was the first lesson I ever properly absorbed about how leadership actually gets made on a working floor.

## The asymmetry nobody tells you about

There is something I want to say about the period between earning influence and being given any title, because it is the part of the journey that I think the modern conversation about leadership most poorly understands.

The men on the floor will start to act as if you are leading them long before the company will start to pay you for it.

That gap is not a small gap. In my own life, it lasted, I would say, the better part of two years. I am not unusual in that. I have spoken, over the decades, to a great many men who ended up running things, and almost every one of them describes a stretch of months or years in which their colleagues had begun to treat them as the man-in-the-bay-with-the-answers while the firm had still been treating them as just another pair of hands on the wage roll. The longer that gap is, the better, in some ways, the leader who eventually emerges from it tends to be — although the gap itself is uncomfortable to live through, and a fair number of good men quit during it, because they cannot tell whether they are being taken advantage of or being prepared for something.

I want to be honest. I felt, at moments during that period, a small private resentment. It was not large. It was not loud. It was not the kind of resentment that I would have admitted to a colleague over a beer. But it was there, the way a small leak in a tank is there — not enough to stop the work, but enough that you keep noticing the wet patch on the floor. I was doing, in effect, a piece of the foreman's job. I was answering questions he would otherwise have had to answer. I was unsticking jobs he would otherwise have had to unstick. I was, on the days he was absent, providing a small invisible scaffolding under the shop's working day, without which a fair amount of the work would have moved more slowly and a fair number of the snags would have taken longer to resolve. And I was being paid, exactly, the wage of a qualified tradesman doing his bench work. Nothing more.

I want to write carefully about what I did with that resentment, because what a young man does with that small leak, in those months, is one of the most important decisions of his working life, and almost nobody discusses it with him while it is happening.

I did not act on it. That, at least, I got right. I did not march into the office and demand a promotion. I did not start visibly slacking on the unofficial work in order to make a point. I did not start grumbling in the canteen about how I was being used. I did not, as far as I can reconstruct, behave any differently on the floor from the way I had behaved before I had noticed the gap was there.

But I also, I now think, made a smaller mistake which I want to name, because it cost me less than it could have but it did cost me something. I let the leak harden, slightly, into a small private *grievance* — not loud enough to act on, not articulate enough to confront, but present, in the back of my head, as a small unfinished sentence about the firm that I never quite let myself finish. *They are getting a piece of work out of me they are not paying for.* I would not have spoken that sentence aloud. But it was, on the worst mornings, somewhere in the room with me when I walked into the shop, and I think the older men, who could read a young tradesman's posture the way some men read horses, picked up on it a few times in ways they did not call me out on but did, I now believe, take note of.

What I should have done, and what I would tell a younger man at the same stage of his career to do, is what I eventually figured out about halfway through that period and wish I had figured out at the start.

The unpaid work was not a debt the firm was running up against me. It was a *deposit* I was making, on my own behalf, into an account whose existence the firm did not yet know about and which it was going to be paying out of, in interest, for the next thirty years of my working life. Every snag I unstuck, every question I answered, every piece of invisible scaffolding I provided, was buying me — slowly, silently, without any of the men around me realising what was being bought — the only thing that, in the long run, actually matters in a working career: the *standing*, in the heads of other working men, of someone who can be relied on when the room gets difficult.

That standing is not something the firm could have paid me for in cash. It is not the kind of thing that goes on a payslip. It is more durable than any rand the firm could have put into my account during that period, because the firm could have gone out of business the following year and the standing would have come with me to whatever shop I walked into next. The wage was the small part of what I was earning. The standing was the large part. And I was the only person, at the time, who was in any position to record the deposits.

I would not have understood any of that on the morning of the bay. I came to understand it slowly, over the next several years, and I do not think I had it fully clear in my head until I was running my own crews and watching a young qualified man go through exactly the same period that I had once gone through. Watching him, I recognised the small wet patch on the floor where the leak was, and I recognised the precise shape of the grievance he was not quite letting himself finish saying, and I made a point, over a smoke break that I did not invite him to, of walking past his bay and saying, very casually, *you are doing well, my boy. The right people are watching.* I do not know if it helped him. I hope it did. It was the sentence I most wanted to have heard, on the worst mornings, in the year and a half before anything formally changed.

## What influence actually felt like, day to day

I want to take a paragraph or two to describe what the *texture* of unofficial leadership felt like on the floor, because I think the abstract version is not very useful and the specific version is.

It felt, mostly, like being interrupted slightly more often than the men around me. That was the most concrete sign. A man would walk over from another bay and ask me a quick question about a job I had nothing to do with. A foreman from the day shift would catch me in the corridor and ask me, almost in passing, what I had thought about the way a particular piece of work had been laid out the day before. An apprentice would come over with a piece of practice work in his hand and ask me whether the weld in the corner was acceptable. None of these interruptions were large. None of them, individually, took more than two or three minutes. But they accumulated, across a day, into a small constant pressure on my time that the men beside me, who were not being interrupted in the same way, did not have.

It felt, secondly, like being *included*. In small conversations I had no formal reason to be included in. A senior tradesman would beckon me over to a discussion he was having with the foreman about a job neither of them had assigned to me, and would say, *what do you think?*, and would actually listen to the answer. The office would, occasionally, ask me to walk over and look at something on a drawing before the quote went out. The customer, on the phone, would sometimes ask for me by name on a job I had once worked on, and the office would put the call through without comment.

It felt, thirdly, like being *watched* — although not in the unpleasant sense. I noticed, early in this period, that the older men on the floor had begun to look at me, occasionally, in a particular evaluating way that they did not use on the men beside me. It was not a critical look. It was, if anything, the opposite. It was the look a man gives to a younger man whom he has begun to suspect might turn out to be one of *us* — by which they meant, although none of them would have said it, the men who run things one day. I could not have proved that interpretation at the time. But I felt the look, and I knew it meant something, and I tried, as best I could, not to disappoint it.

It felt, finally, and this is the part that surprised me most, like *responsibility* — not in the formal sense, but in a moral sense. The minute the men in my bay had started to look at me when there was no foreman in the room, I had become responsible, somehow, for what came out of the next four hours. Nobody had told me I was responsible. I could not have been formally held to account if it had gone wrong. But I had taken on, by accepting the role they had wordlessly handed me, an answerability for the outcome that did not lift the next time somebody came to me with a similar problem. The weight of it was small, in any individual instance. But it was cumulative, and over months it added up into a particular kind of seriousness about the work that I had not carried before. I worked, in that period, with a small extra layer of *care*. Not because anybody had asked me to. Because I had become, in some unofficial sense, accountable.

That, I came to see, was what leadership actually felt like, in its earliest form. It did not feel like power. It did not feel like authority. It felt like a small unfinished line of accountability that ran from the work, through me, to the men who had decided to trust me with it, and that I was no longer at liberty to disregard.

## The mistakes I made in that period

I do not want to write this chapter as if I had been wise about any of it. I was not. I was, in fact, fairly clumsy about several pieces of it, and I want to record three of the mistakes I made, in case any of them are useful warnings to a younger man at the same stage.

The first mistake was that I sometimes *overreached*. There were a handful of occasions, in the first months after I had noticed the wordless appointment, where I gave answers I did not have any business giving — where I weighed in, with more confidence than the situation warranted, on a question that genuinely needed an older man's judgement and not mine. Nobody called me out for it at the time. The work went out. The shop carried on. But I noticed, in one or two cases later, that the foreman quietly walked the work back, in the next day or two, without making any reference to my involvement. He was, in his way, protecting me from a lesson he thought I would learn better if it was not delivered as a public rebuke. The lesson I should have taken from those overreaches, and did eventually take, was that the trust the men around me had handed me was not unlimited. It was specific. It was for the questions I had earned the right to answer. The minute I began answering questions outside that circle, I was spending the trust faster than I was earning it, and there is only so much of that any man can afford to do before the account runs dry.

The second mistake was that I sometimes let the *unofficial* responsibility I had picked up bleed into my own bench work. I would be in the middle of a job of my own and would let myself be pulled away into somebody else's question, and then somebody else's question, and the work on my own bench would slip past its quiet deadline by an hour or two. Nobody complained. The work still got done. But I was, in those moments, treating myself as if I were already a foreman, when in fact I was still a tradesman with bench work to deliver, and the bench work was the thing the company was actually paying me to do. I had to learn, more slowly than I should have, that influence is not a substitute for output. It is supposed to be a supplement to it. A man who picks up the role of *the one who answers* but then misses his own deadlines because of it is a man who has misunderstood the deal. The men whom I most admired had always done their own work first, and had given the answers around the edges of it, and had never, in my observation, used their influence as an excuse for slipping on the work itself.

The third mistake was that I sometimes let my private grievance — the small leak I described earlier — escape into my body language without my noticing it. There were a handful of mornings when I came in carrying it, and the older men, who could read posture the way some men read tracks, saw it on me and quietly raised an eyebrow. Nobody confronted me. Nobody had to. But I noticed, in retrospect, that on the days I was carrying the grievance most visibly, the small flow of questions in my direction temporarily slowed. The men around me had picked up, in some way I could not have articulated then, that I was not as available as I usually was. They had adjusted. They had taken their questions elsewhere for the day. The shop is much more sensitive to the mood of the men inside it than any of us realised at the time. A young man who is carrying an unfinished resentment is, for the duration of the morning he is carrying it, slightly less useful to the people around him than he would otherwise be. The men felt it. They did not say anything. They simply routed around it. And in routing around it, they were, in some small way, beginning to take back a piece of the unofficial appointment they had earlier given me. Nothing dramatic. A small recalibration. But I felt it on the floor, on the worst days, and I came to understand, slowly, that influence is not only earned; it is held — and the holding of it requires a kind of equanimity that the qualifications office had not certified me as having.

## What the men were actually responding to

I want to close with what I think, in retrospect, the men on the floor were actually responding to in those months, because if there is one thing I would want a younger man to take away from this chapter, it is this.

They were not, in the main, responding to *cleverness*. I was not the cleverest qualified tradesman in any shop I worked in. There were men beside me who could read a drawing faster than I could, men who could weld in positions I struggled to weld in, men whose technical knowledge of certain materials was deeper than mine ever was. I am not being modest about this. It is simply the record. If cleverness had been what the men were sorting for, they would have sorted for one of the other men. They did not.

They were not, in the main, responding to *charisma*. I did not have any of the charisma that is sometimes mistaken for leadership material. I was not a particularly funny man. I was not a particularly loud one. I did not tell stories well in those years, although I have, perhaps, become better at it since. I did not make people laugh in the canteen. I did not stand at the centre of any group at any social occasion the shop had. If charisma had been what the men were sorting for, they would have sorted, again, for somebody else.

What they were responding to, as best I can now reconstruct it, was something much quieter and much more durable than either of those things. They were responding to the *cumulative* impression, built over eighteen months of small interactions, that I was a man who was *predictable* in the ways that mattered and *honest* in the ways that mattered, and who could, therefore, be trusted with the parts of the working day where unpredictability and dishonesty would have done damage.

Predictable, in the sense that they could guess, in advance, roughly how I would respond to a difficulty — that I would not panic, that I would not lash out, that I would not start blaming somebody before I had understood what had happened. Honest, in the sense that they could guess, in advance, that I would tell them the actual answer to their question rather than the answer that made me look best.

Those two qualities, in combination, are the foundation under almost every working-class leader I have ever respected. Cleverness is a useful extra. Charisma is a useful extra. Neither of them, on their own or together, can substitute for the foundation. I have watched, over decades, plenty of clever and charismatic men fail to become leaders on a working floor, because they had not bothered to lay the foundation underneath. And I have watched plenty of unspectacular, quiet, predictable, honest men become the men everyone in the shop went to with their questions, with no formal authority at all, because they had laid the foundation almost without noticing they were doing it.

If I could put a single sentence into the head of every newly qualified man on the floor of any shop I have ever worked in, it would be this: the men around you are not, in those first years, evaluating how brilliant you are; they are evaluating whether they would want you near them on a hard morning. Get the second one right, and the first one will eventually take care of itself. Get the first one right at the expense of the second one, and you will spend the rest of your working life being mildly puzzled about why nobody is asking your opinion.

## A short list, for the man who is being asked

If I had to compress this whole chapter into a few lines for the younger version of myself, standing at the bay on the morning of the nozzle, with three pairs of eyes on him and no foreman in the room, it would be these:

- **The vote has already been taken.** The men have appointed you. They will not announce it. You will have to act on it without being told that it has happened.
- **Authority comes from the company. Influence comes from the men.** The order in which you acquire them will shape the rest of your working life. Earn influence first, even when it is not being paid for. Especially when it is not being paid for.
- **The unpaid work is a deposit, not a debt.** You are buying standing. The firm cannot give you that in cash. Nobody else can record the deposits but you.
- **Predictability and honesty are the foundation.** Cleverness and charisma are useful extras. Do not mistake the extras for the foundation.
- **Do not overreach.** The trust you have been handed is specific. Spend it on the questions you have earned the right to answer. The wider questions can wait until the wider trust has been earned.
- **Do not let influence eat your own bench work.** The men who run things well have always done their own job first, and answered the questions around the edges of it.
- **Watch your own grievance.** A young man's small private resentment, carried into the shop on his shoulders, is visible to everyone before it is visible to him. The men will route around it. Do not let it become a permanent posture.
- **The men are watching for who they would want next to them on a hard morning.** Be that man. The rest will follow.

That is the chapter. The morning at the bay has stayed with me, although the job itself I could not now reconstruct in any detail and the men whose eyes turned on me are most of them no longer alive. I have thought about that morning a great many times in the decades since. I have thought about it, in particular, on the mornings when I have been the older man in the room, standing slightly back from a young qualified tradesman who has just had three pairs of eyes turn on him, and I have done my best, in those moments, to do for him what the older men did for me on the day I had not yet learned to recognise — which was, mostly, to step back, to keep quiet, to let the small wordless appointment happen, and to be there afterwards, casually, somewhere near his bay, in case he wanted to ask a question he was not yet able to put into words.

That is, I think, what leading without a title finally looks like, when you have lived inside it long enough to understand what it actually is. It is not a stage you pass through on the way to something larger. It is the *substrate* underneath everything larger. The men who never quite stop doing it, even after they have been given every title the firm has to give, are the men I have most wanted to work for, and the men I have, in my better moments, most wanted to be.
