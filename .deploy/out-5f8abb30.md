# Adcock Ingram: Fourteen Years That Built a Director

The advertisement, when I first read it, did not look like the start of anything. It was in a Sunday newspaper, the kind that lay on the lounge floor of a great many East Rand houses on a Sunday afternoon, and I had picked it up out of the loose habit a working engineer has of turning past the news pages and looking, without really meaning to, at the small print of the appointments section at the back. I had no intention of leaving AECI. I had a cyanide plant that was running half as much again as it had been, a peroxide plant that had quietly followed the same path, a Sasolburg house that suited the children, and a head office in Johannesburg that had begun, in the careful language of large chemical companies, to indicate that I should not be in too much of a hurry about what came next.

I read the advertisement anyway. It had been placed by a consumer-pharmaceutical company in Johannesburg, the kind of company whose products were on the shelves of every chemist on the East Rand, and it was looking for a Company Engineer. I noticed, as I read down the paragraph, that the role was a singular one. There was no plural in it. There was no department of engineers stacked underneath a chief. There was one engineer, with one telephone number on his desk, responsible for the engineering of the whole company. I had spent the previous several years inside an AECI engineering organisation in which I was one of fifteen. I closed the newspaper, folded it onto the side table, and went out into the small back garden to think about the difference between fifteen and one.

I did not, that Sunday, apply. I applied the following weekend, on what I told Antonette was a kind of curiosity rather than a kind of intention. I posted a single sheet of typed paper that gave my dates, my qualifications, my AECI assignments and nothing else, and I addressed it to the box number in the corner of the advertisement. I expected, in the way one expects when one applies casually to a small advertisement in a Sunday paper, to hear nothing further.

## The Interview That Went Too Well

The reply came on a Tuesday. It came by telephone, through the AECI switchboard, and the secretary who put it through did so in the slightly puzzled tone she reserved for callers from outside the chemicals industry. The man on the other end of the line introduced himself in the unhurried language of a senior person who is accustomed to being heard out, named the company, named the role, and asked whether I would be willing to come into Johannesburg the following week for a conversation. I said yes, in the same way I would later, on another Tuesday and at a different desk, say yes to a chairman from Port Elizabeth before I had finished saying hello. I had not yet learned, in those years, that the working answer to that kind of invitation is yes long before the conscious answer has been formed.

I drove in on a clear winter morning, in a suit that I had last worn to a wedding, and I sat in a reception area that smelled, faintly and unmistakably, of the kind of inks and adhesives that belong to a packaging operation rather than a chemical works. The interview, when it began, was not really an interview. It was a long, slow, carefully steered conversation, conducted across a desk by two men who had clearly read my single sheet of paper more carefully than I had expected, and who had decided in advance what they were going to ask. They asked me about Sasolburg. They asked me about the cyanide plant. They asked me about the Dusseldorf work, and they asked me, more particularly, about the way I had persuaded the German contractor to run nights. They were not testing the technical content of my answers. They were testing the shape of the engineer behind them.

I noticed, somewhere in the middle of the conversation, that the meeting had gone too well. The two men across the desk had stopped asking the kind of question that elicits information and had begun asking the kind of question that builds a picture of what a working week would look like if I were already inside the building. They asked me how I would handle a particular packing-hall situation. They asked me how I would talk to a particular kind of foreman. They asked me what I would do, in my first week, if I walked into a room of senior managers who had been running their own engineering by committee for a decade. I answered them, in the unguarded way one answers when one has not yet decided one is being interviewed for the job, and by the end of the morning the offer was effectively on the desk between us.

I drove back to Sasolburg in a different mood from the one in which I had driven in. I had not, on the Sunday I first read the advertisement, intended to leave AECI. I now had an offer, in everything but writing, that would do exactly that.

## Declined, and Then Accepted

I declined the offer the following week. I did so in writing, in the careful language one uses when one is declining something one half wants, and I did so for reasons I had set out for myself the previous evening at the kitchen table with Antonette. The reasons were not strange. AECI had been good to me. The Sasolburg house was settled. The third child was in a school that suited her, and the fourth was not yet a thought we were allowing ourselves to entertain. The cyanide and peroxide plants were running on a method I had spent four years building, and the head office in Johannesburg had begun to talk, in the careful AECI way, about a future I had every reason to trust. I posted the letter on the Wednesday. I expected, on the Thursday, to feel relieved.

I did not. I felt, instead, the particular kind of unease that arrives when one has made a sensible decision against an instinct that has not yet found its words. I sat with it for a fortnight. I walked the cyanide plant on a Monday morning, and the peroxide plant on a Tuesday afternoon, and I wrote the Friday note to head office in the usual way, and I went home each evening to a house in which Antonette, who had read me longer than anyone else in my life, said nothing about the letter and waited for me to come round to the conversation in my own time.

The conversation, when we had it, was short. I had decided, somewhere in the second week of walking my plants and thinking about them differently, that I was being offered a role I had not asked for and had not previously imagined, and that the right answer to a role of that kind is not the sensible answer but the honest one. I had been a member of a fifteen-strong engineering organisation for long enough to know exactly what it would mean to become the only engineer in a building. It would mean being out of my depth in the morning and not out of my depth by the evening, every day, for at least the first two years. It would mean a kind of accelerated stretching of the analytical method I had been quietly forming in the cyanide years. It would mean answering, alone, the question I had previously been able to share with fourteen other men.

I wrote, the following Monday, to the company in Johannesburg, and I asked, in the language one uses when one is asking a question one has no formal right to ask, whether the offer was still open. It was. I gave AECI my notice that week.

## The Resignation That Shocked AECI

The AECI senior people had not seen the resignation coming. I had taken some care, in the previous fortnight, to make sure they would not. I did not believe in announcing a decision before it had been made, and I did not believe in negotiating with my own employer about an offer that I had, formally, already declined. When I walked into my manager's office on the Wednesday morning with a single sheet of paper in my hand, he assumed, until he read the second paragraph, that I was bringing him a Friday note he had not been expecting on a Wednesday. He read the page twice. He looked up at me, and he asked, in the slightly unguarded voice of a senior man who has been blindsided in his own office, whether I had any idea what the company had been planning to do with me. I said that I did, and that I was grateful for it, and that I was leaving anyway.

There were the conversations one would expect. There was a meeting that afternoon, and a meeting the following morning, and a quiet drive across the works to a more senior office in which a more senior man asked the same question in more measured language. I gave the same answer. I did not name the new company. I did not name the salary. I said only that I had been offered a role that I needed to take, that I was not negotiating, and that I would work my notice in full and leave the cyanide and peroxide plants in the state I had been keeping them in. They accepted it, in the end, with the kind of formal grace large chemical companies are good at, and I walked out of my last AECI Friday with the folder of Friday notes still on the desk for whoever came next.

I drove home that evening past the boundary fence of the Sasolburg works for what I knew was the last time as an AECI engineer. I have driven past it many times since. I have never looked at it without a quiet thankfulness for the four years it had given me, and for the analytical method it had let me form in conditions that, in retrospect, were almost laboratory-clean for the purpose.

## The Only Engineer in the Building

I started at Adcock Ingram on a Monday morning in the second half of that year. I was, as the advertisement had promised, the only engineer in the building. There was a maintenance organisation underneath me, run by foremen and fitters of long experience, and there were technical specialists scattered through the production and quality areas who knew their corners of the operation better than I did. But there was no senior engineer above me, no peer engineer beside me, and no chief of department to whom I could refer the questions I did not yet know how to answer. The questions came to my desk. They stayed there until I had answered them.

I spent the first month walking. I walked the way the German months and the cyanide year had taught me to walk, which is to say I walked with the senior foreman on a Monday morning, with the quality manager on a Tuesday afternoon, with a packing-line operator on a Wednesday night, and with whichever maintenance fitter had been in the building the longest on a Thursday. I asked, in each of those walks, the same two questions I had learned to ask in Sasolburg. What was the plant being prevented from doing. What was being treated, in the room, as a permanent compromise that was in fact only the widow of a reason long since dead. The answers were different from the cyanide answers. They came in a different language, and they pointed at different bottlenecks, but the shape of the problem was familiar. A mature operation, settled into a fraction of its potential, with a quiet structure of inherited assumptions that nobody had recently asked to revisit.

I had walked into a different industry. I had not walked out of the method.

## Loss, and a Fourth Child

The personal weather of those first Adcock years was harder than the working weather. My father had died at sixty-three of the lung disease that had been working its slow way through him for most of the Libanon years. I had carried his death into AECI and through the German winter and through the cyanide turnaround, and I had thought, by the time I arrived at Adcock, that I had taken the measure of what it would mean to live without him. I had not. My mother, who had held the East Rand house together on a miner's wage for the better part of forty years, did not long survive him. She died a year after he did, at fifty-four, in a small bedroom in the same semi-detached mine house in which she had raised the five of us.

I do not propose to write the detail of that year. It belongs to the family rather than to a memoir, and the parts of it I would want to write are the parts I am least able to. I will say only that the discipline I had been forming — the analytical method, the Friday note, the walk through the plant on a Monday morning — became, in the months on either side of my mother's funeral, the scaffolding that held the working week together when the personal week was not yet able to. Antonette held the rest. She has always held the rest. There is no chapter of my working life that has not, in some quiet structural way, been a chapter of hers.

It was in the middle of that year, or perhaps the early part of the following one, that we found out that a fourth child was on the way. She arrived in due course, healthy and welcome, and she settled into the house in the way that a fourth child settles, which is to say with three older siblings already running the daily curriculum and a father who had begun, with her birth, to think more carefully than he ever had before about the time he was giving to a working week. I made some adjustments in those years. I did not make as many as I should have. I have written, more than once in this book, that the time owed to a family is not the same as the time that fits comfortably around an ambitious career, and that the gap between the two is a debt that has to be either paid in advance or repented of later. I paid some of it in advance. The rest I am still, in my own way, addressing.

## The Packing-Hall Redesign

The first substantial piece of engineering work I did at Adcock was the redesign of one of the company's packing halls. It is not a piece of work I will dwell on at length, because the pharmaceutical-manufacturing discipline that came with it deserves a chapter of its own, and will get one. But the bones of it were these. The hall, as I inherited it, had been laid out a long time previously, in conditions that no longer obtained. The product mix had drifted. The line speeds had crept up. The hygiene-zone boundaries had been redrawn, more than once, on top of the original walls. The result was a hall that worked, in the sense that product came out of one end of it on most shifts, but that worked at a fraction of the throughput it could have carried and at a level of operator strain that, on the floor, I could feel within a fortnight.

I walked it. I sat with the line supervisors. I sat with the quality people. I drew, in the evenings at home on the kitchen table, sketch after sketch of a flow that would unwrap the loops the hall had been quietly tying around itself for years. I costed it as cheaply as I could, because I did not yet have the standing inside the building to ask for a great deal of capital. I presented it, in the end, to a board that did not, at first, quite know what to do with the only-engineer-in-the-building bringing them a redesign of a hall their longest-serving people had been running for fifteen years. They approved a phased version of it. We built the first phase over a long weekend, with the line stopped on the Friday afternoon and running again on the Tuesday morning, and the throughput lifted measurably on the same product mix and the same crew. The second and third phases followed within the year.

It was a small piece of work in the scale of the things that came afterwards. It was the piece of work that bought me, inside the building, the standing I would need for everything that came after it.

## Boston, and a Reverse-Osmosis Plant

The second substantial assignment of the early Adcock years took me overseas again. The company needed a reverse-osmosis water plant of pharmaceutical quality for one of its main manufacturing sites, and the technology, at that scale and to that specification, was best procured from a small specialist supplier in the Boston area. I was sent to negotiate it, inspect it on the supplier's floor, and accept it on behalf of the company. I flew, this time, alone, with a folder rather thicker than the German folder had been and with a different kind of nerve from the one I had borrowed in 1980.

Boston in those years was not Dusseldorf. The American supplier did business in a faster, more verbal way than the German contractor had done, and the specification meetings were shorter and louder and more inclined to settle, in the room, what the Germans had insisted on settling in writing. I adjusted my method. I kept the analytical core of it. I let the surface of it pick up the pace of the room. I asked the same questions I would have asked in Dusseldorf, but I asked them across a smaller table and in a different rhythm, and I wrote the same kind of single-page note to the Johannesburg head office on the same kind of Friday afternoon, because some habits, by then, were no longer adjustable.

I came home with a reverse-osmosis plant that performed, on commissioning, on the day the contract said it would. I came home, also, with a quiet confidence that the working method I had built in Sasolburg was not, as I had half feared, a German artefact. It was portable. It was the same method in Boston as it had been in Dusseldorf, and it would prove, over the years, to be the same method on every continent on which I would eventually have to use it.

## Germany Again, and a Sterile Machine

Some years into the Adcock work I was sent back to Germany. It was a different assignment in a different yard, on a different kind of equipment, but the country was the same and a great many of the working habits were the same, and I found myself, in the first week, falling back into the rhythms of the Dusseldorf winter with an ease that surprised me. The assignment, this time, was training on a piece of sterile-filling machinery that the company had bought for one of its higher-specification production halls. The training was technical in the narrow sense, and it was technical in the wider sense as well. The Germans were not only showing me how the machine worked. They were showing me how the machine had to be cared for, by whom, in what discipline, on what cadence, if it was to deliver the level of integrity the pharmaceutical world expected of it.

I made notes in the same small private notebook I had used at the cyanide plant. I came back to Adcock with a discipline of sterile manufacturing that we put into practice on the floor in Johannesburg, line by line, over the months that followed. The Germans had taught me, the first time, how to defend a specification. The Germans had taught me, this second time, how to defend a sterile boundary. The two lessons would, in due course, run together into the manufacturing instinct that the next chapter of this book belongs to.

## Bringing the Jeyes Line Home

Towards the middle of my Adcock years the company took on a transfer of production that did more to stretch my engineering than any single project before it. The Jeyes blue-line range, which the British parent had been producing at East Kilbride in Scotland for many years, was to be relocated to South Africa. The plant, in pieces, was to come down the Atlantic in shipping containers. The line was to be re-erected in a hall I had been preparing for it on the Johannesburg site. The product, on the other side of the transfer, was to come off the South African line in the same specification, in the same packaging, with the same shelf life, and at the same hygiene standard as it had been coming off the Scottish line for years.

I flew to East Kilbride more than once in the months leading up to the transfer. I walked the line in Scotland with the engineer who had been running it. I made the same kind of notes I had made in Dusseldorf and in Boston and in the second German yard. I asked the line's foremen, in the unhurried way one asks foremen who have run a line for a long time, what the line knew that the drawings did not. The answers, written down patiently over several visits, became the unwritten half of the transfer document. The written half was straightforward. The unwritten half was where the transfer would either succeed or fail.

We brought the line down in containers in the order I had specified. We re-erected it in the prepared hall in Johannesburg over a period of weeks that I had compressed, against the recommendation of the British engineers, by running parallel installation crews on the sub-assemblies I knew could be brought in independently. We commissioned the line in stages, with the Scottish engineer present for the first run and a South African crew running the second, and we made our first commercial batch of blue-line product on the South African floor within the window I had given the board. The product came off the line in the same specification it had come off the Scottish line in. The shelf-life trials, over the months that followed, confirmed that the transfer had not cost the product its integrity. The line, by the end of its first South African year, was running at a rate that compared favourably with its East Kilbride benchmark.

It was, by some distance, the most complex piece of engineering I had personally led to that point. It was also the piece of work that, inside the building, settled my standing for the years that followed.

## Technical Director

The promotion, when it came, did not come with a great deal of brass. It came in the unhurried language of a chairman in a quiet office, in a conversation that started with the Jeyes line and ended, somewhere in the second half of the morning, with the offer of the Technical Directorship of the Consumer Division. The role would put me on the operating board. It would give me responsibility for the whole supply chain of a division that ran several factories, dozens of lines, a procurement function whose reach extended into Europe and the United States, and a quality organisation that, in the pharmaceutical and consumer-pharmaceutical sense, carried consequences I had only begun, in the previous several years, to appreciate properly.

I accepted it. I went home, that evening, and I told Antonette over a kitchen table that had heard, by then, a great many of the working decisions of my life, that I had become a Director. She did what she always did when a working week brought home a piece of news of that scale. She thanked God for it, in the quiet language of her own faith, and she asked me what it would mean for the children. I told her, as honestly as I could, that it would mean more travel and longer days, and that I would do what I could to make sure the cost did not fall on the wrong side of the family. She nodded. She has always nodded when an honest answer has been offered, and she has always, in her own quiet way, held me to it.

I walked into the building the following Monday with the same notebook in my pocket, the same Friday discipline in my head, and a card on the office door that had, for the first time in my life, the word Director on it. The card surprised me, on the first morning, more than I expected it to. The work, mercifully, did not. The supply chain of a Consumer Division turned out to be, in its bones, a larger version of the kind of operation I had been analysing patiently since the cyanide years. The bottlenecks were in different places. The compromises were in different colours. The structure was the same. I walked the factories the way I had walked the packing hall. I wrote the Friday note the way I had written it from Sasolburg and from Dusseldorf and from Boston. I gave my people the dates I believed in rather than the dates the planning meetings made convenient.

## Fourteen Years, in the End

I look back now, from the distance of more than two decades, on the fourteen years I gave to Adcock Ingram, and I am still slightly surprised by how much of the working method I would carry for the rest of my career was finished in that building. I had arrived in the early thirties as the only engineer in the building. I left in the early forties as a Technical Director with the supply chain of a Consumer Division behind me, a fourth child at school, a wife who had held the home together through the German winters and the Boston weeks and the Scottish months, and a working method that had been tested in pharmaceuticals and not found wanting.

I will not, in this chapter, rehearse the pharmaceutical-manufacturing discipline that runs through everything I have just described. It deserves its own treatment, and it will have one. I will say only, by way of closing, that the fourteen years at Adcock did three things for me that I did not yet have when I walked in. They taught me to be the only engineer in a building, which is to say to be a man whose questions cannot be referred sideways and must be answered. They taught me to work in an industry whose consequences are quieter and longer and more unforgiving than the consequences of a heavy-chemicals plant. And they made me, by the end of the fourteenth year, ready in a way I did not yet have words for, for a telephone call I was not yet expecting, from a chairman I had not yet met, about a Managing Directorship in a city I had never lived in.

That call, when it came, would come on an ordinary afternoon. Antonette and I would sit at the kitchen table that evening with a decision of a different scale in front of us. The fourteen Adcock years would, in retrospect, look like a long and careful preparation for the question that telephone call put on the table. At the time, they had looked only like a working life.
